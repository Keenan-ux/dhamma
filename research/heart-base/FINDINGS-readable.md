# The heart-base in the Pāli canon and commentaries: where the seat of mind is posited, where it is named, and with what force the claim is made

## Abstract

A meditator trained in the living Theravāda is often told that the mind has a physical seat, the heart-base, a subtle matter lodged in the cavity of the heart. The claim is concrete and confident, and it is repeated in modern manuals as settled doctrine. Going back to the texts to look for it, one finds that the discourses never say so. This survey sorts where a term and a concept sit across the three composition layers of this literature, the discourses, the seven analytic books, and the commentaries. The name of the heart-base turns out to be absent from the canon while the bare concept it names is present in one late canonical book, unnamed. Where the canon stakes a claim under its own register of direct knowledge, the seat of mind never falls; it is laid down as a support and built upon, never put to that test. The commentators, asked how the heart-base is known, admit it is not in the canonical text and supply it from scripture and reason. This describes a shape across textual layers; it is not a proof about what the historical Buddha taught.

---

# I. Where the texts say the mind sits

A meditator trained in the living Theravāda is often told, early and plainly, that the mind has a physical seat. Consciousness, the teaching runs, does not float free of the body; the mind-element and the mind-consciousness-element rest on a particular base, the *hadaya-vatthu*, the "heart-base," a subtle kind of matter lodged in the cavity of the physical heart. The claim is concrete and confident, and it is repeated in modern manuals as settled doctrine.[^1] What is striking, when one goes back to the discourses to look for it, is that the discourses never say so. The four main collections of the Buddha's talks are silent about any material organ of mind. The gap between that confident later teaching and the canon's silence is the subject of this study.

The question I want to put is narrow, and I hope answerable. It is not whether the mind in fact has a base in the heart, a question of doctrine and of physiology that the texts and I both leave alone. It is where, across the layers of this literature, the seat of mind is first posited, where it is first *named* the heart, and with what force the claim is made when it is finally made. Two readings stand against each other. The one the data led me toward (call it the working reading) is that the heart-identification is a late development: the canon posits an unnamed material base for the mind, and only the commentaries name that base the heart and seat the stream of subconscious life upon it. On this reading the named heart-base is something the texts *posit and build upon*, never something they say they have *verified* under the canon's own test of direct knowledge. The plainer expectation, which I take as the null and which the structural shelving of the texts seems at first to support, is that the heart-base is canonical doctrine, spread across the canon's layers as its filing implies, and staked under the verification register the canon reserves for the four truths and the destruction of the taints. The pages that follow sort and describe a marker-bounded reading of where a term and a concept sit across textual layers. They are not a proof about what the historical Buddha taught.[^2]

This study proceeds as follows:

- **II. Three layers, and what was searched.** The three composition layers of this literature (the discourses, the seven Abhidhamma books, the commentaries), and a short note on how thoroughly each was searched.
- **III. What the canon actually says.** Why the *name* of the heart-base is absent from the canon while the *concept* it names is present in the canonical Abhidhamma, unnamed.
- **IV. Posited, never verified.** The canon's register for claims it says it has tested, and how the heart-base never falls under it, with the closest near-miss reading as the strongest evidence against verification.
- **V. The tradition names its own gap.** How the commentators see the canonical silence, say so, and reconcile it, and the chronology that admission reveals.
- **VI. What this means, and what it does not.** Why a late and unverified seat of mind matters for a reader, and the several things this reading cannot show.

Before tracing where the heart-base sits, I set out what the three layers of this literature are, and how thoroughly each was searched. To that frame I turn first.

# II. Three layers, and what was searched

It is easy to speak of "the canon" and "the commentary" as if the literature came in two flat slabs, an early one and a late one. For the question at hand that division is too coarse, because the move from a bare concept to a named, seated organ runs partly *inside* the canon. Three composition layers need to be kept apart:

- **The discourses (the old core).** The four main *nikāyas*, the collections of the Buddha's talks, together with the early verse. This is the material most readers picture as "the suttas," and it is the oldest stratum the corpus holds.
- **The seven Abhidhamma books (canonical but analytic, later).** A second canonical shelf, scholastic rather than narrative, that takes the categories of the discourses and arranges them into systems. It is canonical by status but later by composition, and it is here, as we will see, that an unnamed material base for the mind first appears.
- **The commentary, and the Visuddhimagga with it (later still).** The *aṭṭhakathā* and the sub-commentarial *ṭīkā* that gloss the canon, and Buddhaghosa's great manual the Visuddhimagga. One wrinkle matters throughout: the corpus files the Visuddhimagga under the same structural role as the canonical root texts, although by composition it is classical commentary. A reader who took that structural filing for a date would wrongly credit its contents to the canon.

The object of study is therefore not "canon versus commentary" but the placement of one term and one concept across these three layers. The term is *hadaya-vatthu*, the heart-base. The concept is the bare idea of a material support for the two mind-elements, which the canon can hold without ever using the name.

### How these were found

The reading ran over the live canonical and commentarial corpus of 194,710 passages, with the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana recension as ingested as the base edition and the SuttaCentral Mahāsaṅgīti reading text as the parallel canonical reading.[^3] Every count reported here is a floor, not a ceiling. The search for the heart-base was widened in steps, from the bare named term as a non-specialist would type it, out to inflected and compound forms, and then to the *concept* phrased without the name at all; what each widening recovers is shown in Fig. 1 and set out rung by rung in Appendix B.[^4] The load-bearing negatives (that the named term is absent from the discourses and from the Abhidhamma books) were rechecked by grouping on the work rather than the raw passage count.[^5] Pāli renderings of Abhidhamma and commentary that the corpus carries only in Pāli are my own gloss, checked against Ñāṇamoli's usage.

A word on the load-bearing judgments, since they are unusual for this kind of study. The key results in §IV are not a category several readers coded and agreed on, but close readings of *where* a knowing-word sits relative to the heart-base in the handful of passages where the two fall near each other. Each of those near-misses was adjudicated against the surrounding passage, and the reading for each is logged in Appendix C. No multi-coder agreement coefficient was computed for them, and I do not report one; the honest apparatus here is the adjudication log.[^6]

With the layers named and the search measured, the first question is the plainest: when the canon speaks of a seat of mind at all, what does it actually say, and what does it leave unnamed?

# III. What the canon actually says: a named term absent, a concept present

### III.1 Three readings of where mind sits

How the heart-base relates to the canon is read in at least three ways, and it is worth setting them side by side before attaching any count:

- **The heart-base is canonical Buddha-word.** On this reading, the seat of mind in the heart is something the Buddha taught and the canon records, and the commentaries merely elaborate it. This is the assumption built into much of modern practice teaching.
- **The heart-base is a pure commentarial invention.** On this reading, the heart-identification has no canonical root at all; it is a scholastic addition with nothing behind it in the Buddha-word, and could in principle be set aside.
- **The concept is canonical, the name and the heart-seat are later.** On this reading, the canon does posit a material base for the mind, but in the analytic Abhidhamma alone, and there it leaves the base unnamed; the *name* "heart-base," and the identification of that base with the physical heart, belong to the later layer.

The reading the counts support is the third, and it is more interesting than either of the other two, because it splits a question the bare name conflates. The name *hadaya-vatthu* and the concept of a material seat of mind are not the same object, and they sit in different layers.

### III.2 The name is absent; the concept is present

Searched as a name across the discourses and the Abhidhamma books, the heart-base is simply not there. The term *hadaya-vatthu* occurs zero times in the four main collections of discourses and zero times in the seven canonical Abhidhamma books.[^7] Every structurally-rooted occurrence of the name, eighteen rows once inflected and compound forms are folded in, sits in the Visuddhimagga, with one further hit in the Niddesa and one in the Milindapañha.[^8] None of these is an early discourse, and none is a canonical Abhidhamma book. By the name alone, the heart-base looks like a thing the canon does not contain.

The concept tells a different story. The Paṭṭhāna, the last and most analytic of the seven Abhidhamma books, speaks of a material support for consciousness without ever calling it the heart. It names "the matter dependent on which the mind-element and the mind-consciousness-element occur," *yaṁ rūpaṁ nissāya manodhātu ca manoviññāṇadhātu ca*.[^9] That clause, the unnamed posit, is canonical Abhidhamma in seven rows. So the concept of a material seat of mind *is* in the canon, in the analytic layer, but stripped of the name and of any identification with the physical heart. This is the eleven-row gain that the concept rung of Fig. 1 recovered, and it is the canon's whole contribution to the question: a base, posited, and left unnamed.

The neighbouring doctrines that the commentary would later weave around the heart-base fall into the same shape. *Bhavaṅga*, the "life-continuum," the stream of subconscious mind that the later tradition would seat on the heart-base and run through its whole model of the cognitive process, is absent from the four collections of discourses and appears as a bare relatum, a term in a list, thirty-eight times in the canonical Abhidhamma.[^10] The elaborate process-model built on it, the *citta-vīthi* or "course of consciousness," is the commentary's surplus, not the canonical term. The named ladder of insight-knowledges, the numbered stations a meditator is said to pass through, shows the same pattern again: as a set of *named* stations it is absent from the discourses and the Abhidhamma books alike, while the lived practice it maps, dwelling in contemplation of the rise and fall of the aggregates (*udayabbayānupassī viharati*, "he dwells contemplating rise-and-fall"), is early-canonical in twenty-five rows of the discourses, as a present-tense practice and never as a numbered station.[^11] In each case the early canon has the activity and the later layers supply the name.

So the canon holds the concept and withholds the name. A sharper question is not where the term sits but with what *force* it is claimed. The canon stakes some of what it says under a register of direct knowledge and states the rest flat as background; is the seat of mind ever something the texts say they have *verified*? Hence I turn to how the heart-base is asserted.

# IV. Posited, never verified: how the texts assert the seat of mind

*What the data seem to say here, before any detail: the canon stakes some of its claims under a test of direct knowing and states others flat; the heart-base is always stated flat, and the one place a knowing-word sits closest to it turns out to read the other way.*

The texts do not assert everything in the same key. Some claims are staked under a verification register, a small family of stock phrases that mark a thing as something the texts say they have tested and found to be so. Other claims are simply stated as background, the furniture a teaching stands on. The distinction is not mine; it is the canon's own, and it is sharp once one looks for it. The seat of mind is always on the second side of it.

Three phrases carry most of the verification weight, and each can be glossed plainly:

- *sacchikatvā*, "having directly realized it for oneself," the verb of first-hand attainment;
- *sayaṁ abhiññā sacchikatvā*, "by his own direct knowledge, having realized," the fuller form that names the knowing as the speaker's own;
- *yathābhūtaṁ pajānāti*, "he knows it as it really is," the formula of seeing a thing as it stands.

These are not rare. They saturate the corpus: the first appears in 654 rows, the fuller second form in 190, the third in 264.[^12] When the texts want to mark something as known rather than merely held, this is the grammar they reach for. The four noble truths are staked this way; so is rebirth governed by deeds, and so is the destruction of the corrupting influences, the taints. These are the claims the tradition says it has put to the test.[^13]

Against that frame the heart-base is silent. Across the whole corpus the named *hadaya-vatthu* never falls in the same window as any of the three verification phrases.[^14] It is introduced, everywhere it is introduced, by a different grammar altogether: an analysis of its existence and its supporting function, *atthibhāvo*, "its being-there," and the support-characteristic, *nissaya-lakkhaṇa*, the way a thing serves as the ground something else rests on.[^15] That is the grammar of a posit, of something laid down so the rest of the analysis can stand, not the grammar of a thing realized.

A flat zero is easy to state and easy to mistrust, so the cautious move is not to rest on the count but to walk to the places where the count is nearest to failing, the rows where a knowing-word and the heart-base actually sit together, and to read each one. Three near-misses bear reading, in proportion to how close they are.

The first is barely a co-occurrence at all. One row carries both *sacchikatvā* and the heart-base, but the two stand 87,102 characters apart, in wholly separate pericopes that happen to share a long passage.[^16] Nothing connects them; the proximity is an artifact of length.

The second is genuinely close, about 120 characters, and at first glance looks like the thing the absence-claim would forbid: the divine eye, *dibbena cakkhunā*, one of the higher knowings, sitting beside the heart-base. Reading the window dissolves the appearance. The divine eye there is the faculty being glossed, named as itself (*dibbacakkhuvasen'eva*, "by way of the divine eye"); it is not a knowing predicated of the heart-base. The two words are near because the passage is about the faculty, not because the faculty confirms the seat of mind.[^17]

The third case is the closest of all, and it carries the section.

The nearest any verification-word comes to the heart-base anywhere in the corpus is about seventy characters, in a sub-commentarial gloss on the higher knowings (one of ten rows where *abhiññā* and the heart-base co-occur, the tightest read here).[^18] One might expect the closest hit to be the strongest evidence for verification. It is the strongest evidence against. The line reads that the fifth jhāna, working by way of direct knowledge, runs dependent on the heart-base alone: *abhiññāvasena pavattaṁ pañcamajjhānaṁ … hadayavatthuññeva nissāya*, "the fifth jhāna, occurring by way of direct knowledge, dependent on the heart-base alone." The direct knowledge is real and the heart-base is right beside it, but the relation between them is the reverse of confirmation. The heart-base is what the knowing-mind stands on; it is the floor under the act of knowing, not the object the act of knowing verifies. Even where a knowing and the seat of mind are pressed as close together as the corpus ever presses them, the grammar of a posit holds at the very point one would expect it to break.

> **0**: rows where the named heart-base falls under a verification formula, against a register attested 654, 190, and 264 times elsewhere in the same corpus.

A patterned silence of this kind is admissible as evidence only if the obvious dull explanations can be cleared away, and they can be:

- It is not a recall miss. The search for the name was widened to its inflected and compound forms and then past the name to the concept, and the count held; the term was found where it lives and absent where it does not.
- It is not corpus size. The expected frame is large, not thin: 654, 190, and 264 attestations of the register are room enough for the heart-base to have entered it, had the texts wanted it there.
- It is not a synonym hiding the hit. The concept search, the one that reaches past the name, found only the unnamed Abhidhamma posit, and that posit is itself never verified either.

What the cleared-away silence licenses is bounded, and the bound is worth stating exactly. The heart-base is assumed as given, a posited material support that the analysis takes for granted and builds upon, not asserted as something the tradition tested and found true. The silence does not license the stronger and quite different claim that the tradition doubted the heart-base; there is no sign of doubt, and no sign of testing either. It is stated flat, leaned on heavily, and never once put under the canon's own test of direct knowledge, the test it reserves for the four truths and the ending of the taints. That is a narrower thing than doubt, and a more interesting one. So the seat of mind is laid down rather than verified, and the closest the corpus comes to staking it under a knowing-word is a line in which the heart-base is the ground the knowing runs on.

If the tradition posited the heart-base and never verified it, one might expect it to be unaware of the gap. It is not. The commentators see the canonical silence, say so in as many words, and reconcile it, and the way they reconcile it is itself part of the evidence, and it opens onto the chronology. To both I now turn.

# V. The tradition names its own gap, and the chronology it reveals

*What the data say here, in one line: the commentators admit the heart-base is not in the canonical text and supply it "from scripture and from reason," and the same admission marks the chronology, an early lived practice with its route-map drawn late.*

### V.1 The tradition objects to itself

A tradition does not trouble to reconcile a silence it does not feel. The clearest evidence that the canonical silence around the heart-base was felt as a gap is that the sub-commentators raise it themselves, as an objection, and then answer it. The scene is worth telling, because the move is so deliberate.

A sub-commentator stops the exposition and poses the question a sceptic would pose: how is the heart-base to be known at all, given that it does not come down in the canonical text? He answers in two words, "from scripture and from reason," *āgamato yuttito ca*, and then produces, as his scripture, the very anonymous Paṭṭhāna clause that never names the heart. The first witness, in the Abhidhamma sub-commentary, says it flatly: although the heart-base does not come down in the text, its existence is to be known from scripture and from reason; as for the scripture, and there follows the unnamed posit, cited by its canonical reference.[^19] The second witness, the great sub-commentary to the Visuddhimagga, poses the identical question and gives the identical answer, quoting the same clause in line: how is it to be known that the heart-base is the support-characteristic of the mind-element and the mind-consciousness-element? From scripture and from reason.[^20]

Two things make this more than a pious formula. The first is that the flag is explicit: the text says the heart-identification is *Pāḷiyaṁ anāgata*, "not handed down in the [canonical] text." The tradition is not under the impression that the heart-base is the Buddha's plain word; it knows it is not, and says so. The second is what it adduces to fill the gap. The scripture it produces is the unnamed Abhidhamma base, the *yaṁ rūpaṁ nissāya* clause, "the matter dependent on which the mind-element and the mind-consciousness-element occur." The commentary closes its own admitted gap with the very placeholder it is in the act of naming. The reconciliation runs in a circle, and the circle is the evidence: the heart is warranted by the posit, and the posit is what the heart was supposed to name.

That two independent sub-commentaries make the same move, in the same words, suggests the gap was not a private worry of one author but a recognized point of strain in the system.

### V.2 The walking and the map

The same admission that marks the heart-base as late marks the chronology of the whole apparatus, and here the texts can mislead if read by their shelving alone. The Visuddhimagga is filed under the structural role of root-text, *mūla*, yet by composition it is classical commentary, later by centuries than the canon it expounds. Every appearance of the named heart-base at that root-text layer sits in the Visuddhimagga or later still.[^21] A reading that took the structural shelf for a timeline would credit the heart-base to the canon through the Visuddhimagga's root-text role, and would be wrong by the width of the commentarial era. The stratigraphy shows a commentary-period development standing on a canonical-Abhidhamma placeholder, not a canonical doctrine.

The numbered ladder of insight-knowledges shows the same shape one shelf up. Its stations are first set out by name, with their own defining sections, in the Paṭisambhidāmagga, a Khuddaka analytical work that is structurally root-text yet not early, sitting just outside the old core.[^22] The named ladder is, on this evidence, a para-canonical systematization, not a canonical sequence.

The cleanest way to see the chronology is to set the early canon and the late systematization side by side on the one subject they share. Consider the contemplation of rise and fall. In the discourses, the elder Khemaka describes the practice as a plain present-tense activity: a person dwells contemplating the rise and fall of the five aggregates, seeing that such is form, such its arising, such its passing.[^23] It is a verb, a thing one does, with no list and no numbered place. This is not an isolated turn of phrase; the same practice, in the same present-participle grammar, *udayabbayānupassī viharati*, "he dwells contemplating rise and fall," recurs through the four Nikāyas in twenty-five rows.[^24]

In the Paṭisambhidāmagga the same contemplation has become a named station in a graded route: the knowledge of contemplating rise and fall, and further along the wisdom of contemplating dissolution, an insight-knowledge with its own name and place in the sequence.[^25] The territory is identical; the difference is that the later text has drawn a map over it, numbered the stages, and given each a title. The early canon has the walking. The route-map is drawn afterward.

> **25 vs 0**: Nikāya rows where the rise-and-fall contemplation is practised, against Nikāya rows where it is named as a numbered insight-knowledge.

In sum, the commentators tell us themselves that the heart-base is not in the canon and reach to scripture and reason to supply it, and the chronology bears the same signature: the experience is early and lived, while the name and the numbered ladder are drawn over it late. What a late and unverified seat of mind means for a reader, and the several things this reading cannot show, is the last question.

# VI. What this means, and what it does not

To ask where the Pāli canon says the mind sits is to find that the canon mostly does not say. It is silent in the discourses on any material seat; in the seven canonical books of the Abhidhamma it posits a base, a matter on which the mind-elements depend, but leaves that base unnamed; and only the commentary names it the heart and seats the life-continuum upon it. The familiar line between an early canon that states the doctrine and a later commentary that elaborates it is real here, but it is partial. The movement from a concept to its name does not wait for the commentary to begin. It is already half-made inside the canon, in the Abhidhamma's unnamed posit, and only completed afterward, when the commentary gives the posit a body and calls it the heart. For a meditator told today that the mind has a physical base in the heart, the honest report is that the suttas never say so, the canonical Abhidhamma posits a seat without naming it, and the heart-identification is a later, scholastic naming, stated flat and built upon, and never once put to the canon's own test of direct knowing.

Several limits bound how far that reading can be pressed, and they are best said plainly.

- The dating here is relative order, not calendar date. The decisive point, that the Visuddhimagga reads later than its root-text shelving implies, rests on standard philological grounds for placing that work and the Paṭisambhidāmagga, not on absolute years.
- Every count is a floor. A claim phrased outside the markers searched would not be caught, so the figures are the conservative end of what the corpus holds.
- The absence is admissible only in its narrow sense. That the heart-base is never staked under a verification formula licenses "posited, not verified," and no more. It does not license the stronger claim that the tradition doubted the heart-base.
- The cross-recensional reach is untested, not shown to be Pāli-unique. The Paṭṭhāna, where the posit lives, carries no parallel rows of any kind, but that is a known blind-spot of a parallels apparatus built sutta-to-sutta and thin on the Abhidhamma, not a finding that the doctrine is absent elsewhere. That the heart-base is a Theravāda differentia, that the northern Abhidharma does not seat mind in the heart, I take from comparative scholarship, not from this corpus.[^26]
- The external status of the numbered ladder is feature-unconfirmed. The Paṭisambhidāmagga's content has some Chinese Āgama parallels, but a shared container is not a shared feature; the graded ladder, as a sequence, has no clean Āgama parallel, so it is best read as a Theravāda systematization pending feature-level confirmation. The thing that does seem to be shared, and plausibly pre-sectarian, is not the route-map but the territory, the lived contemplation of rise and fall, which sits in the four Nikāyas and recurs across recensions.

What the earlier three-tier reading expected, this closer look bore out, with the cross-recensional leg held as untested rather than confirmed: the sutta silence, the Abhidhamma placeholder, and the commentarial naming hold in every cell, and gain two qualifications. The placeholder is never epistemically verified; it is posited and leaned upon. And the commentary, asked how the heart-base is known, reconciles its own naming against an admitted canonical silence, from scripture and from reason, adducing as its scripture the very placeholder it is naming.

The data can show only this much: that the seat of mind was named late and built upon, and never staked under the test the canon reserves for what it says it knows. Whether the heart is in fact where the mind sits is a question for another kind of inquiry; the texts, read in their own layers, give back the sub-commentator's own quiet admission, that the heart-base *Pāḷiyaṁ anāgata*, "does not come down in the [canonical] text."

### B.2 The triage note (which readings were load-bearing, and which were set aside)

The study's question is an authority-and-literalness question about a posited entity: where is the seat of mind first named, and with what force is the claim made? That shape makes two readings load-bearing, the epistemic marking (is the heart-base ever staked under the verification register? §IV) and the harmonization (does the tradition reconcile its own silence? §V.1), with the structured-absence reasoning as the epistemic reading's partner and the cross-recension reading for the originality question. ## Appendix C: The adjudication of the proximity reads

This study departs from the inter-annotator template in one place, and the departure is principled. The load-bearing judgments in §IV are not a category several readers coded and agreed on, so no multi-coder coefficient was computed and none is reported. What is load-bearing instead is whether a verification verb that sits *near* the heart-base is actually predicated *of* it. That is an analyst close-reading of substring proximity, and the honest apparatus for it is an adjudication log of every near-miss, not an agreement matrix.

The headline is a structured absence: across the whole corpus the named heart-base never falls in the same window as any of the three verification phrases, against a register attested 654, 190, and 264 times elsewhere. The three closest cross-window co-occurrences were each read against the surrounding passage and adjudicated:
