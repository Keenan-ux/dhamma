FACET M1-classes
[SUMMARY]
The canon does both, but in distinct strata. (a) NAMING: the four kinds of persons-by-understanding are named in a canonical Sutta, AN 4.133 (Ugghaṭitaññūsutta), which lists "ugghaṭitaññū, vipañcitaññū, neyyo, padaparamo" and does nothing else — no definitions, no criteria, just the bare enumeration. (b) DEFINING: the operative definitions are NOT in the Sutta Piṭaka but in the Abhidhamma — Puggalapaññatti, Catukkapuggalapaññatti §§148–151 — which gives each type a behavioral definition keyed to HOW dhammābhisamaya (breakthrough to the Dhamma) is reached: ugghaṭitaññū the moment it is stated (saha udāhaṭavelāya); vipañcitaññū when a brief saying is analyzed in detail; neyyo "to be led" — only gradually (anupubbena) through recitation/uddesa, questioning/paripucchā, wise attention, and attending on good friends (kalyāṇamitte sevato); padaparama for whom, however much is heard/recited/memorized/taught, there is no breakthrough "in that life" (na tāya jātiyā). (c) IDENTIFICATION CRITERIA: the definitions are retrospective markers (who in fact penetrates, and under what conditions), not a diagnostic test an ordinary person can apply in advance. The only "method" the texts give for telling the types apart is the Buddha's own buddhacakkhu: in SN 6.1 / MN 26 the Buddha SURVEYS the world (volokento) and sees beings "with little dust / much dust, keen / weak faculties, easy / hard to teach" (apparajakkhe mahārajakkhe... suviññāpaye duviññāpaye), and the commentary (Spk-a / Ps-a on the Brahmā's-request passage) explicitly maps the four lotus-stages to the four types and says he "saw all the modes" (sabbākāratova addasa). So identification is the Buddha's perceptual prerogative, not a stated rubric. The canon also carries a parallel three-fold teachability typology, AN 3.22 (Gilānasutta), classing hearers by analogy to three patients — recovers regardless, never recovers, recovers only with care — where Dhamma-teaching is "prescribed" (anuññātā) chiefly for the third, the contingently-curable. The Puggalapaññatti is itself an entire Abhidhamma book classifying persons, and its Fours-section frames the understanding-types alongside its set similes (four clouds, pots, water-pools, etc.).

FACET M2-matching
[SUMMARY]
Yes — the canon shows the Buddha matching a specific meditation OBJECT to a specific defilement (and to a specific person's situation), but it does so as object-to-defilement antidote pairing, NOT as the systematic six-carita (temperament-type) selection scheme of the Visuddhimagga. The clearest single mapping is AN 9.3 (= Ud 4.1, Meghiya), where, after Meghiya is beset by sensual/malicious/cruel thoughts, the Buddha prescribes four things "to be developed further": "asubhā bhāvetabbā rāgassa pahānāya, mettā bhāvetabbā byāpādassa pahānāya, ānāpānassati bhāvetabbā vitakkupacchedāya, aniccasaññā bhāvetabbā asmimānasamugghātāya" — ugliness to give up lust, love to give up ill-will, breath-mindfulness to cut off thinking, impermanence-perception to uproot the conceit "I am." MN 62 (Mahārāhulovāda) gives Rāhula a graded set with the same antidote logic spelled out object by object ("Meditate on ugliness... for any lust will be given up"; "on love... any ill-will"; on karuṇā against cruelty, muditā against discontent, upekkhā against aversion, aniccasaññā against the conceit "I am"), plus the four/space elements and a full ānāpānasati. AN 10.60 (Girimānanda) shows person-fitted selection of a different kind: ten perceptions taught to Ānanda to recite to a gravely sick monk, "after hearing them his illness will die down on the spot" — and Girimānanda recovers. So the PRINCIPLE of fitting an object to what needs countering is plainly canonical. The temperament-typology term carita does NOT appear in the suttas in this sense: every mūla hit for "carita" is the verb form (caritā, "having practiced/wandered," from carati), e.g. MN 12, AN 4.150, Vin Pvr 7. The systematic rāga-/dosa-/moha-/saddhā-/buddhi-/vitakka-carita-by-object scheme is therefore Visuddhimagga commentarial elaboration (Vism Ch. III), built ON the canonical antidote pairings but going beyond them.

FACET M3-count40
[SUMMARY]
The closed list of exactly 40 kammaṭṭhāna (10 kasiṇa, 10 asubha, 10 anussati, 4 brahmavihāra, 4 āruppa, 1 āhāre paṭikūlasaññā, 1 catudhātuvavatthāna) is BUDDHAGHOSA'S synthesis in the Visuddhimagga, not the Buddha's. The decisive evidence is lexical: an exact-FTS search for "kammaṭṭhāna" in the original Pāli returns hits ONLY from commentary (Aṭṭhakathā: Spk-a, Ps-a, KN-a, Sp; Ṭīkā: Sv-pṭ, Vism-mhṭ; Abhidhamma-aṭṭhakathā: Vibh-a) and from the Visuddhimagga itself (cst-e0101n.mul-84, Ānāpānassatikathā) — restricting to layer=mula yields only 2 hits, both Visuddhimagga, and ZERO Tipiṭaka (sutta/vinaya/abhidhamma) rows. The technical term and the number 40 are absent from the canon. What the Buddha actually gave is a large set of discrete objects, several already grouped into closed sub-lists in the suttas: the ten kasiṇāyatanāni (AN 10.25, DN 33/34), six anussatiṭṭhānāni (AN 6.10), four appamaññā/brahmavihāra and four āruppā (DN 33), the satipaṭṭhāna body-objects — breath, postures, 32 parts, four elements, nine charnel-ground stages (DN 22) — maraṇassati (AN 6.19), and the ten saññā of AN 10.60. Crucially the sutta lists are not identical to the Vism 40: the canonical ten kasinas end with ākāsa + viññāṇa (space + consciousness), whereas Buddhaghosa's ten substitute āloka (light) and paricchinnākāsa (delimited space) and drop viññāṇa. So the answer to (a) is NO — the Buddha enumerated no closed "40" and did not use the unifying term kammaṭṭhāna; he gave many objects, some in closed sub-lists, which Buddhaghosa later harmonized, renumbered, and capped at 40.

WEB: Provenance of the Theravāda "40 meditation subjects (kammaṭṭhāna)" and the "6 temperaments (carita)-to-object" matching scheme
The systematic enumeration of exactly forty kammaṭṭhāna in seven categories, and the matrix matching them to six character-types (carita), is a 5th-century CE COMMENTARIAL synthesis codified in Buddhaghosa's Visuddhimagga (Chs. III–XI; the carita scheme in Ch. III), with Upatissa's earlier Vimuttimagga (lost in Pāli, surviving in 6th-c. Chinese as Jietuo dao lun) as its near-identical precursor — the Vimuttimagga lists thirty-eight subjects. Neither the closed numbered list of forty nor the carita matrix appears as such in any single Nikāya sutta. The technical term "kammaṭṭhāna" is itself largely post-canonical: in the Pāli Canon it occurs only in a handful of discourses meaning secular "work/occupation," and acquires its "meditation subject" sense in the commentaries. However, nearly every individual element of the forty — the kasiṇas, recollections, brahmavihāras, asubha/foulness contemplations, and ānāpānasati — IS independently and securely attested across the Nikāyas; Buddhaghosa's achievement was systematization and the diagnostic pairing to temperament, not invention of the practices.
  - [high] The list of exactly forty kammaṭṭhāna, organized into seven categories, is codified in Buddhaghosa's Visuddhimagga (5th c. CE), Chapter III. The seven categories are: 10 kasiṇas, 10 asubha (foulness/corpse contemplations), 10 anussati (recollections), 4 brahmavihāras (divine abidings), 4 arūpa (immaterial/formless states), 1 perception (loathsomeness of food), and 1 defining/analysis (the four elements). Total = 40. Buddhaghosa uses the word kammaṭṭhāna for each of these forty objects. (Kammaṭṭhāna — Wikipedia; Kammaṭṭhāna — Encyclopedia of Buddhism)
  - [high] The carita-to-object matching matrix is set out in Visuddhimagga Chapter III. The standard scheme: GREEDY/lustful (rāga) -> the 10 foulness (asubha) + mindfulness of the body (kāyagatāsati); HATING (dosa) -> the 4 brahmavihāras + the 4 colour kasiṇas; DELUDED (moha) -> mindfulness of breathing (ānāpānasati); FAITHFUL (saddhā) -> the first 6 recollections (Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, devas); INTELLIGENT (buddhi) -> recollection of death, recollection of peace/Nibbāna, perception of loathsomeness of food, analysis of the 4 elements; SPECULATIVE/discursive (vitakka) -> mindfulness of breathing. The remaining 6 (non-colour) kasiṇas and the 4 formless states are declared suitable for ALL six temperaments. (Kammaṭṭhāna — Wikipedia; Kammaṭṭhāna | 40 Objects | Buddhism & Healing (redzambala))
  - [high] The forty-fold list and carita matrix are a commentarial synthesis, NOT a canonical sutta list. The unified numbered enumeration appears in no single Nikāya discourse; it is Buddhaghosa's systematizing arrangement of practices that the canon presents only in scattered, separate lists. The Wikipedia note observes, e.g., that Buddhaghosa's asubha section is 'textually distinct' from the asubhasaññā of the Aṅguttara Nikāya and from the canonical nine-stage corpse-decay contemplation — i.e. a reorganized commentarial compilation rather than a direct canonical quotation. (Kammaṭṭhāna — Wikipedia; Patikulamanasikara — Wikipedia (notes canon includes the contemplation in various lists, while the 5th-c. Visuddhimagga gives the systematized form))
  - [high] Upatissa's Vimuttimagga (Path of Freedom) is the earlier precursor. Traditionally attributed to the arahant Upatissa (c. 1st–2nd c. CE per tradition); its Pāli/Buddhist-Hybrid-Sanskrit original is lost and it survives via the 6th-c. Chinese translation Jietuo dao lun (解脫道論) by Saṅghapāla. It bears a striking structural similarity to the Visuddhimagga; scholarly consensus (P. V. Bapat, Vimuttimagga and Visuddhimagga: A Comparative Study, 1937) is that it predates and very probably influenced Buddhaghosa, who appears to have followed its general plan. CONTESTED/NUANCE: scholars caution the two are not in a simple copy relationship — they diverge on points such as dhutaṅga (ascetic practices) being wholesome/unwholesome and on the progress-of-insight scheme, so 'precursor and independent witness' is more accurate than 'source Buddhaghosa copied.' (Vimuttimagga — Wikipedia; The Path to Freedom (Vimuttimagga), Ehara/Soma/Kheminda trans. (PDF))
  - [high] The Vimuttimagga lists THIRTY-EIGHT meditation subjects vs the Visuddhimagga's forty. The principal divergence is in the last two kasiṇas: the Visuddhimagga's set substitutes the light kasiṇa (āloka-kasiṇa) and limited-space kasiṇa (paricchinnākāsa-kasiṇa) where the Vimuttimagga's older series has the consciousness kasiṇa (viññāṇa-kasiṇa) and space kasiṇa (ākāsa-kasiṇa). The net count differs by two. This is the single most-cited concrete difference between the two manuals' kammaṭṭhāna lists. (Vimuttimagga — Wikipedia; A Comparison of the lists and Categorisation of Meditation Practices (Kammaṭṭhāna) in the Visuddhimagga and *Vimuttimagga — Contemporary Buddhism 20(1-2))
  - [high] The technical term 'kammaṭṭhāna' (lit. 'working-ground' / 'place of work') is largely post-canonical. In Pāli literature prior to the post-canonical commentaries the word appears in only a handful of discourses and there means secular 'work' or 'trade'/occupation (e.g. MN 99, AN 8.54, 8.55, 8.76, 10.46), extended to the ascetic's 'work.' Its sense as 'meditation subject for samatha' (later extended to vipassanā) is a commentarial development, used systematically by Buddhaghosa. (Kammaṭṭhāna — Wikipedia; MEDITATION AND ITS SUBJECTS: TRACING KAMMAṬṬHĀNA FROM THE EARLY CANON TO THE BORAN KAMMATHAN TRADITIONS — Contemporary Buddhism 20(1-2) (Crosby/Skilton/Kyaw eds.))
  - [high] The KASIṆAS are attested individually in the Nikāyas, even though the systematized ten-kasiṇa series belongs to the Visuddhimagga. The ten kasiṇas (earth, water, fire, air; blue/yellow/red/white; plus light & space) are referenced at canonical loci including A.v.46-60 (Aṅguttara), M.ii.14 (Majjhima — the Mahāsakuludāyi Sutta context), D.iii.268, 290 (Dīgha), the Paṭisambhidāmagga (Ps.i.6, 95), Dhammasaṅgaṇī (Dhs.202), and Nettippakaraṇa (Nett.89, 112). (Kasiṇa — Encyclopedia of Buddhism; Kasiṇa — Wikipedia)
  - [high] ASUBHA / paṭikūlamanasikāra (foulness / repulsiveness-of-the-body contemplation) is canonically attested. The Pāli Canon includes this contemplation in its mindfulness lists at Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta (DN 22), Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta (MN 10), Kāyagatāsati Sutta (MN 119), Girimānanda Sutta (AN 10.60), Mahāhatthipadopama Sutta (MN 28), Mahārāhulovāda Sutta (MN 62), Dhātuvibhaṅga Sutta (MN 140), and SN 51.20. The 5th-c. Visuddhimagga supplies the systematized treatment, but the practice has authentic pre-commentarial canonical roots. (Patikulamanasikara — Wikipedia)
  - [high] ĀNĀPĀNASATI (mindfulness of breathing) is securely canonical and pre-commentarial. Its locus classicus is the Ānāpānasati Sutta, Majjhima Nikāya 118 (sixteen-step scheme), with parallels across the Saṃyutta Nikāya's Ānāpāna-saṃyutta (SN 54), the Satipaṭṭhāna Suttas (MN 10 / DN 22), and the Paṭisambhidāmagga; the sixteen-step instruction also recurs in other suttas and in the Āgama/Vinaya parallels of other schools. (Ānāpānasati Sutta — Wikipedia)
  - [high] The BRAHMAVIHĀRAS and the RECOLLECTIONS (anussati) are individually canonical. Mettā (the first brahmavihāra) has its locus classicus in the Karaṇīyamettā Sutta (Sn 1.8 / Khp 9), and the four divine abidings (mettā, karuṇā, muditā, upekkhā) recur across the Nikāyas (e.g. the Tevijja Sutta, DN 13, and many Aṅguttara/Majjhima passages). The recollections (e.g. Buddhānussati and the recollection of generosity/virtue/devas) likewise appear individually in the Nikāyas. Buddhaghosa's contribution is gathering these dispersed canonical practices into the single closed forty-fold scheme and pairing them diagnostically to the six caritas. (Karaniya Metta Sutta — Encyclopedia of Buddhism; Metta Sutta — Wikipedia)
[CAVEATS]
  - The traditional 1st–2nd c. CE date and named authorship (Upatissa) of the Vimuttimagga are TRADITIONAL attributions, not firmly established by modern scholarship; the work survives only in 6th-c. Chinese (Jietuo dao lun) plus Tibetan fragments, and its school affiliation (Abhayagiri vs. other) and original language are debated.
  - The Vimuttimagga–Visuddhimagga relationship is best stated as 'shared earlier tradition / probable influence,' not 'Buddhaghosa copied Upatissa.' Bapat (1937) argues for influence on Buddhaghosa; the two diverge on dhutaṅga, the wholesomeness of concentration, and the insight-progression scheme, so they are partly independent witnesses to a common Sinhalese commentarial heritage.
  - The exact carita-matching wording varies slightly across secondary summaries; the canonical anchor is Visuddhimagga Ch. III (Ñāṇamoli's translation 'The Path of Purification' is the standard English reference). The 'suitable for all temperaments' clause covers the 6 non-colour kasiṇas plus the 4 formless states; some summaries also list ānāpānasati as broadly suitable, reflecting that the carita scheme gives primary, not exclusive, recommendations.
  - Whether the forty is a strictly closed canonical-style 'doctrine' or a pedagogical taxonomy is itself a scholarly emphasis: the Crosby/Skilton/Kyaw 2018–19 Contemporary Buddhism special issue ('Variety in Theravāda Meditation') stresses that kammaṭṭhāna is a developing tradition (canon -> commentary -> boran kammaṭṭhāna), cautioning against treating the Visuddhimagga forty as the timeless or only Theravāda meditation map.
  - The kasiṇa canonical citations (A.v.46-60, M.ii.14, D.iii.268/290, Ps., Dhs., Nett.) are reported via the Encyclopedia of Buddhism / Wikipedia secondary apparatus; a scholar verifying the primary text should confirm each PTS reference directly, as some loci attest the practice of kasiṇa-jhāna rather than the full enumerated ten-fold list.
