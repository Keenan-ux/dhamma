# Matching a Person to a Practice in the Pāli Tradition: how the temperament scheme sits later than the canon, and how one word changed its work

## Abstract

A familiar piece of Buddhist meditation lore sorts people into six types by their dominant tendency and hands each type its own practice: foulness for the greedy, loving-kindness for the hating, and so through the six. It is usually read as the canon's own counsel. This survey asks a narrower thing: where, across the layers of the Pāli literature, this matching apparatus actually sits, and how firmly the tradition vouches for it. The simple antidote it is built on, keying a practice to a present defilement, is early and shared across the early schools. The six-temperament scheme laid over that seed is not in the early discourses or the Abhidhamma at all; it pools in the late and para-canonical books, with one manual carrying most of it. Its central word shifts its sense across the layers, from conduct to temperament to a reading taken off the body. And the manual that fixes the scheme declines, in its own words, to certify the most detailed part of it. The survey describes that shape and marks the limits of saying so.

---

# I. Matching a person to a practice

A teacher in the Pāli tradition is often pictured facing a single student with a single question: what should *this* person practise? Not what the path is in general, but what to hand to the one in front of them, given the cast of their mind. The answer the later tradition offers is famous and tidy. People sort into six types by their dominant tendency, and each type has its object. The greedy temperament is given the contemplation of foulness, the hating temperament loving-kindness, the deluded and the discursive their own remedies, and the faithful and the intelligent theirs.[^1] It is one of the most quoted schemes in Buddhist meditation literature, and it is usually read as the canon's own counsel, the Buddha's clinical eye reduced to a chart.

The question I want to put to that scheme is narrow. It is not whether the matching works, a practical question the texts make a teacher's business and not a reader's. It is where, across the layers of the literature, this apparatus actually sits, and what kind of authority the tradition itself claims for it. Two readings stand against each other. The one I came to favour, the working hypothesis, is that the matching apparatus is later in time than the canon it is shelved beside, that it is built on a genuinely early canonical seed, and that the tradition never certifies the elaborate part of it under its own standard for verified knowledge. The plainer expectation, which I take as the null, is that the apparatus is uniformly commentarial against a uniformly canonical core with no chronology running inside the canon, that its central term carries one stable sense throughout, and that the matching is staked as knowledge the tradition vouches for. What follows sorts and describes a marker-bounded count; it does not prove a case. One load-bearing claim, the absence of a key word from the early books, is a hard count; the chronology built around it is relative order, not calendar date, and I keep that distinction visible throughout.[^2]

This study proceeds as follows:

- **II. The canonical seed: keying an object to a defilement.** The early move the whole apparatus is built on, and a short note on how the material was found.
- **III. Where the temperament scheme actually sits.** The contested readings of the central term, and the finding that the temperament scheme is absent from the early books and pooled in the late ones.
- **IV. A word that changed its job.** How the term *carita* drifts from conduct, to temperament, to a diagnosis read off the body, to a modern personality type.
- **V. Systematized, never certified.** Why the canon stakes its knowing-faculties on capacity rather than type, and how the tradition's own manual declines to vouch for its temperament diagnostics.
- **VI. What this means, and what it does not.** Why a non-specialist should care that the matching machinery is later and softer than its shelving implies, and the limits of saying so.

Before any of that machinery, the canon already does something simpler: it keys an object to a present defilement. That seed is where I start.

# II. The canonical seed: keying an object to a defilement

Long before the six temperaments, the early discourses make a plainer move. They do not sort the person; they read the defilement that is active and prescribe against it. The clearest form is a short antidote formula: foulness is to be developed for the abandoning of lust, loving-kindness for the abandoning of ill will, mindfulness of breathing for the cutting off of discursive thought, and the perception of impermanence for uprooting the conceit "I am."[^3] The pairing is functional, not characterological. It does not say the person is a lustful type; it says that where lust is present, this is the practice that answers it.

The four pairings are worth setting out plainly, because everything later is built on them:

- **Foulness** (*asubha*, the contemplation of the body's unlovely constituents) answers **lust** (*rāga*).
- **Loving-kindness** (*mettā*) answers **ill will** (*byāpāda*).
- **Mindfulness of breathing** (*ānāpānassati*) answers **discursive thought** (*vitakka*).
- **The perception of impermanence** (*anicca-saññā*) answers the **conceit "I am"** (*asmimāna*).

A companion discourse keeps the same logic with a shorter list, setting foulness, loving-kindness, and wisdom against greed, hate, and delusion.[^4] Alongside this antidote keying the canon carries a second move the later apparatus will lean on: the yoke of calm and insight, *yuganaddha*, in which tranquillity (*samatha*) and insight (*vipassanā*) are developed together rather than ranked or split into separate vehicles.[^5] Both moves are squarely in the discourses, and both, as the parallels will show, reach back past the Pāli into the lines other schools preserved. They are the ground. What the tradition builds on that ground, the sorting of persons into types and the welding of each type to an object, comes later, and the rest of this paper is about the difference between the seed and the structure.

# III. Where the temperament scheme actually sits

*What the data say about this scheme, in one line: the temperament typology is absent from the early canon and concentrated in the late and para-canonical books, with the Visuddhimagga carrying most of it.*

### III.1 Three readings of one word

The whole apparatus turns on a single Pāli word, *carita*, and the word does not mean one thing. Before attaching any finding to it, the contested readings belong side by side, because the argument of this paper is partly that the readings belong to different layers of the literature.

- **The conduct sense.** The oldest layer. *Carita* is built on the verb *carati*, "to course, to move, to conduct oneself," and survives in the ethical compounds *sucarita* and *duccarita*, "good conduct" and "bad conduct." Here the word is about how one behaves, not what type one is.
- **The temperament sense.** The famous scholastic reading. *Carita* names a fixed disposition, the dominant tendency that sorts a person into one of the six types and selects their meditation object.
- **The modern personality-type reading.** The reception frame, in which the six caritas are presented as something close to a typology of stable character types, read through a vocabulary of psychology the Pāli does not itself supply.

These three are not three nuances of one stable sense. They sit at different depths in the literature, and the question of where the temperament reading sits is the question this section answers.

### III.2 Absent from the early books, pooled in the late ones

The finding is a plain absence. The temperament-sense *carita* compound (*rāgacarita*, *dosacarita*, and the rest) is attested zero times in the four Nikāyas and zero times in the seven books of the Abhidhamma.[^6] It is not faint there; it is not there. Where it does occur, in the fifty-three mūla rows that carry the disambiguated compound, every single one is either the Visuddhimagga or a work of the para-canonical Khuddaka layer; not one is from the four Nikāyas, and not one is from the Abhidhamma. The Visuddhimagga alone holds thirty of the fifty-three.[^7]

> **0**: attestations of the temperament-sense *carita* compound in the four Nikāyas and the seven Abhidhamma books. The scheme's central word is simply not in the early canon.

The same shape repeats for the two other pieces of the apparatus that usually travel with the temperament scheme: the technical sense of *kammaṭṭhāna* ("meditation subject") and the closed list of forty meditation subjects.

The middle row needs a word of care, because it corrects an earlier overstatement of mine. The word *kammaṭṭhāna* is itself canonical; what is not canonical is its technical meditation sense. Of the 148 mūla rows that carry the word, 134 are the Visuddhimagga, and the handful in the Aṅguttara, Majjhima, and Dīgha carry the ordinary sense, a livelihood or place of work, as in *gharāvāsa-kammaṭṭhāna*, "the household occupation."[^8] So the word is old and the technical sense is late, a distinction that confirms and quantifies the reading of Crosby, Skilton, and Kyaw (2019). The bottom row is starker still: the enumerated closed set of forty subjects occurs in exactly two mūla rows, both in the Visuddhimagga's own chapter on the forty, and nowhere in the four Nikāyas or the Abhidhamma.[^9] That the Vimuttimagga preserved in Chinese counts thirty-eight rather than forty (Bapat 1937) is a useful reminder that the closed forty is a settling, not a given; a sibling manual in another line drew the boundary differently.

The decisive thing about these absences is that no register label is doing the work. The point is not that the temperament scheme is written in a commentarial style; it is that the word is not in the early books in any style. A genre judgement could be argued about; a count of zero is harder to argue with, and it is the count that carries the finding.

If the scheme is not in the early canon, where did its central word come from, and did it always mean what it means in the Visuddhimagga? It did not. The word changed its job, and the change is legible across the layers. To that drift I now turn.

# IV. A word that changed its job

*What the tracking shows, before the detail: the central term of the whole apparatus, carita, did not mean "temperament" in the old discourses; it acquired that sense late, and one can watch it move.*

If the six-temperament scheme is not in the early discourses, the natural next question is where its central word came from, and whether it always carried the meaning the Visuddhimagga gives it. It did not. The Pāli word *carita* changed its job over the life of the literature, and because a single term can be tracked stratum by stratum, the change is visible in a way a flat split between canon and commentary would hide. The principle is simple: when one word is followed across the layers and its sense shifts under a verbatim anchor drawn from each layer in turn, a re-coinage shows itself as a measured fact rather than an impression. No early anchor here is filled from a later one; each cell is read in its own stratum.[^10]

The drift runs through five resting places, and it is easier to see as a strip than as a list.

Read along the strip, four moves separate the ends.[^11] In the early prose the noun of temperament is simply not there; what is there is the verb *carati*, "to course, to conduct oneself," and the ethical compounds *sucarita* and *duccarita*, "good conduct" and "bad conduct."[^12] The function the later apparatus performs, keying a practice to a defilement, is already in the canon, but it travels under no such name: it is the plain antidote formula of AN 9.1 and Ud 4.1.[^13] At the next stratum a new sense is minted on the old word. In the para-canonical Khuddaka the temperament reading first appears, and it appears alongside, not in place of, the older conduct reading. From there the commentary keeps only the temperament sense and hardens it into a fixed six-cell matrix of person against object, with the machinery for assigning one to the other.[^14] The sub-commentary then adds a layer the commentary did not have, reading temperament off the body itself. And the modern translator, last in the line, renders the whole thing through a category the Pāli never carried, the stable, diagnosable "personality type."

> **Five strata, one word, four re-coinages**: from a verb of conduct in the early prose to a "personality type" in the modern gloss, with the temperament sense minted, fixed, somatized, and finally psychologized along the way. Not one of the four re-coinages happens inside the four Nikāyas.

### IV.1 A scene at the fork: the Culaniddesa

The earliest place the full six-cell matrix appears is not a discourse but a gloss, and that fact is itself the finding. The Culaniddesa, an analytical work of the late Khuddaka, is explaining a verse the brahmin student Mogharāja put to the Buddha. In the course of the gloss it has the Buddha *know* the type of each person before him and match the teaching to the type: to the person of greed-temperament he teaches the talk on foulness; to the person of hate-temperament he points out the development of loving-kindness, and so through the six.[^15] This is the birth certificate of the apparatus. It is ascribed to the Buddha's knowing rather than spoken by him in any Nikāya, and it sits in a layer the field places after the four-Nikāya prose.

What makes the same work the hinge of the whole drift is that the older sense is alive a few pages away, in the same stratum. The Paṭisambhidāmagga, sibling to the Niddesa in the analytical Khuddaka, uses *cariyā* to mean exactly the older thing, a mode of conduct: it lists eight *cariyā*, the conduct of the postures, of the sense-bases, of mindfulness, of concentration, of knowledge, and the rest.[^16] The word had not yet settled on "temperament" even in the layer that first deployed it that way. So the temperament sense is caught here mid-fork, a re-coinage in progress, not a stable canonical meaning the commentary merely tidied up. A study that registered only whether the word was canonical or commentarial would see "the same word" in both places and miss that it is, in sense, two different words.

### IV.2 The far end: temperament read off the body

The drift does not stop at the Visuddhimagga's matrix. The sub-commentary takes one further step, and it is a vivid one. Reading the heart-blood, the Vibhaṅga-aṭṭhakathā says that the blood of a greed-type runs red, of a hate-type dark, of a delusion-type the colour of water in which meat has been washed.[^17] A scheme that began as a way of keying a practice to a defilement has become, at this end, a physiology: temperament is now something the body itself displays, diagnosable from its fluids. There is no canonical warrant for this, and none in the old commentary either; it is the apparatus enriching itself past any text it could appeal to. That is the endpoint of the line that the antidote formula began.

In sum, the word that names the matching apparatus did not carry the matching apparatus in the old canon; it acquired the temperament sense late, kept its older conduct sense beside it for a while at the fork, and then drifted on into a somatic diagnostic and, finally, into modern personality psychology. A re-coined word fixed into a six-cell grid is a systematization. Whether it is also *certified* knowledge, knowledge the tradition stakes under its own test for the real thing, is the last question, and the tradition answers it itself.

# V. Systematized, never certified

*What the texts seem to say here, before the detail: the canon stakes its knowing-faculties on a person's capacity and latent tendency, never on the six-temperament grid, and the manual that fixes the grid declines, in its own words, to vouch for it.*

The most consequential thing the tradition does with the matching apparatus is decline to certify it. The canon does have a formula for verified knowledge, and it does apply it to the reading of persons, but the object it reads is not temperament. The faculties it stakes are the knowledge of how mature another's spiritual faculties are, and the knowledge of beings' dispositions and latent tendencies.[^18] These are staked under a knowing-formula, the language the texts reserve for what is directly known rather than merely held; but what they let the knower see is capacity and inclination, not a slot in a six-cell grid. The faculty that the canon certifies reads something the typology is not.

Where the typology and a knowing-formula do turn up in the same canonical row, they turn out to be nowhere near each other. They sit in the same long, undivided Niddesa and Nettippakaraṇa rows but in wholly separate passages, tens and then hundreds of thousands of characters apart, never in the window of a single claim.[^19] The co-occurrence is an artifact of how the text is stored, not a case of the tradition staking the typology as known. There is exactly one place where the matching apparatus and a statement about its certainty sit tight together, and it is decisive because it is the tradition disowning its own construction.

### V.1 The teacher reading the pupil, and the disclaimer

The Visuddhimagga, the manual that fixes the whole apparatus, describes at length how a teacher might read a pupil's temperament. He is to watch how the pupil walks and stands, how he sweeps and dresses, how he eats, what he does with his eyes, what work he is drawn to, even how he sleeps; each is keyed to one of the six types. The diagnostic is built out in careful detail, and then, having built it, the text stops and takes it back. This method of determining temperament, it says, has come down in all its aspects neither in the canon nor in the old commentary; it is stated only following the teachers' opinion; and so it is not to be relied on as authoritative.[^20] The most elaborate part of the apparatus is the part its own apex manual refuses to vouch for. What the text offers instead is honest and modest. A teacher who has the attainment of mind-reading will simply know the pupil's temperament and assign accordingly; lacking that attainment, the text says, he should just ask.[^21]

> **"not to be relied on as authoritative"**: the Visuddhimagga's own verdict (*na sārato paccetabbaṃ*) on the temperament diagnostics it has just set out at length.

This is the epistemic shape of the thing, stated as plainly as the texts state it. The matching apparatus is held as serviceable scholastic guidance, taught and elaborated and kept; it is not held as certified knowledge, and the tradition draws that line itself, at the very point where the apparatus is most developed. None of this licenses the stronger claim that the tradition rejected the typology. It did not; it preserved it for centuries and enriched it. It licenses only the precise claim that the matching was never staked as the kind of knowing the canon reserves for what is directly seen.

### V.2 How far the apparatus reaches across the traditions

One more question bears on how original the apparatus is: whether the matching scheme is shared across the early schools or particular to the Pāli line. The two halves of the material answer differently, and the honest answer turns on a distinction the corpus's parallels table can register only crudely.

The canonical seed reaches widely. The defilement-to-object antidote formula has parallels in the Madhyama Āgama and witnesses in the Udānavarga; the yoke of calm and insight has a Saṃyukta Āgama parallel; the six recollections that furnish the faith-cell its objects have Saṃyukta Āgama parallels too.[^22] The functional core the apparatus is built on survives in lines independent of the Pāli and most likely predates the split into schools. The temperament typology is what stands apart. The one canonical row that carries the full matrix does have external parallels, but they are parallels to the verse the work is glossing, not to the temperament feature itself; the feature is not in the parallel.[^23] Its genuine non-Pāli witness is not a discourse at all but a meditation manual, the Vimuttimagga of Upatissa, preserved only in Chinese, which carries a cognate scheme; and a table of discourse-to-discourse parallels cannot register a manual.[^24] So the right thing to say is not that the typology is a Theravāda invention, but that it is Pāli-local on the linked evidence and awaits a check feature by feature against the manuals and the northern Abhidharma.

A related reading from the earlier study holds under the same lens, and is worth recording because a silent change would have shown up here. The canon presents calm and insight developed together: the word for that yoking is robustly canonical, and across the discourses reached, insight is never assigned alone, as a standing practice, to a named person.[^25] The later split into a "calm-vehicle" and an "insight-vehicle" practitioner, and the figure of the dry-insight worker who is said to reach the goal on insight alone, are barely canonical at all: a handful of rows each, every one of them the Visuddhimagga, against many in the commentaries proper.[^26] Like the carita apparatus, the dry-insight vehicle is a late stratum wearing a canonical tag.

In sum, the matching machinery is systematized but never staked as verified: the canon's knowing-faculties read capacity rather than type, the apparatus and any claim of certainty about it sit far apart wherever they share a row, and the one place they meet is the Visuddhimagga disowning its own diagnostics. That the apparatus is late, re-coined, and uncertified does not make it worthless; it changes what kind of thing it is. What that means for a reader is the closing question.

# VI. What this means, and what it does not

If one opens "the canon" to learn how a person is matched to a practice, this survey suggests the honest answer is that the elaborate machinery one finds, the six temperaments, the closed forty meditation subjects, the technical sense of *kammaṭṭhāna*, is later than the canon and built on a much simpler canonical move. That older move is real and early: the discourses key a practice to a present defilement, foulness against lust, loving-kindness against ill will, the breath against scattered thought, the perception of impermanence against the conceit "I am," and they leave it there. The six-temperament grid is a scholastic construction laid over that seed; its central word acquired the temperament sense late; and the tradition's own apex manual, having set the grid out in full, declines to certify the most detailed part of it. For a reader, this is worth knowing before reaching for "the canonical teaching on temperament types": the weight of that teaching is later, and softer in its own claims, than its canonical shelving implies.

Several limits bound how far that reading can be pressed, and they are better said plainly than smoothed over.

- The dating here is relative order, not calendar date. It rests on standard philological grounds for placing the analytical Khuddaka later than the four-Nikāya prose and the Visuddhimagga later still, and it imports no absolute years. The one claim that does not depend on a register-for-date reading is the load-bearing one: the temperament-sense compound is simply absent from the four Nikāyas and the seven Abhidhamma books, in any register, a hard absence rather than a label converted into a date.[^27]
- The cross-recensional finding is link-level, not feature-level. "No linked parallel" for the typology is a fact about the corpus's discourse-to-discourse parallels table, not affirmative evidence about the Āgama and Abhidharma traditions; the typology's real non-Pāli witness, the Vimuttimagga, is exactly the kind of manual that table cannot register.[^28]
- The character-gap evidence for the epistemic absence is edition-relative. The exact distances are computed over the corpus's long undivided rows as ingested, and a finer segmentation would change the numbers. What does not change is the fact they stand for, that the knowing-formula and the typology never share a single passage, and the Visuddhimagga's disclaimer is edition-neutral.[^29]
- The reach of the count is the matching vocabulary itself. Within the tradition's own closed lists, the six temperaments, the forty objects, the antidote formula, the enumeration is saturated; but saturation inside the closed lists measures structural completeness, not a proof that the open corpus holds no further instance phrased in terms the search did not cover.[^30]
- The analytic words this paper leans on, "temperament," "typology," "personality type," "diagnostic," are a modern frame laid over the Pāli, named as such throughout. Where the corpus carries no English the rendering is my own gloss, checked against the standard translations, with the load-bearing terms quoted in Pāli.[^31]

What the survey cannot do is settle the practical question it sits next to: which object an angry person, or a scattered one, *should* take up. That is a normative question the texts do not adjudicate and, tellingly, do not leave to the practitioner. The matching is teacher-assigned, openly provisional, and in the Visuddhimagga's own marking not to be relied on as authoritative; it is never a thing the canon invites a person to diagnose in themselves and certify. If there is a single thing the texts license here, it is that modest one: a practice keyed by another to what stands in a person's way at the moment, offered as serviceable guidance and named, by the tradition itself, as the teachers' opinion rather than as known. As the Culaniddesa frames even the matrix at its birth, it is the Blessed One who is said to *know* the person, and to point out the practice; the knowing is never handed down to the one who practises.

## Appendix B: Method register

### B.1 The recall ladder, all four rungs

Every count in the paper rests on a measured enumeration. The search of the central term was widened in four rungs; the yields are a measure of the surface string, not a moving target for the census.

| Rung | Search strategy | Yield |
|---|---|---:|
| 1 | Naive surface token: the bare word *carita* as a word-boundary match (the term is almost always compounded) | 5 |
| 2 | Morphological stem *carit-* across mūla rows (over-collects: also catches the verb *carita* and the past participle) | 824 |
| 3 | Sense-disambiguated temperament compound: the stem prefixed by a defilement (*rāga-*, *dosa-*, *moha-*, *vitakka-*, *saddhā-*, *ñāṇa-carita*), grouped by work | 53 (mūla) |
| 4 | Concept-only pass: the canonical seed searched by its function, name-independent (*asubhā bhāvetabbā rāgassa pahānāya* and its companions) | 35 (mūla) |

Rung 3 is the load-bearing one. The gap between rung 1 (which nearly misses the compounds) and rung 2 (which drowns them in the verb) is itself the methodological point. Every load-bearing count below the ladder was reconfirmed by a direct count grouped by textual role, not by the search lane. The rung-4 concept-only pass returns 35 mūla rows, five of them in the Aṅguttara and one in the Udāna: the defilement-to-object function is canonical without the *carita* name, while the typology is the post-canonical overlay.

### B.2 The triage note

This is a category-typology question, so the load-bearing readings coded on every instance were: the chronological stratum (coded from work and position, independently of the structural layer), the semantic-drift strip (the term tracked across strata with a verbatim anchor per stratum), the epistemic marking (whether the matching is staked under the tradition's own formula for verified knowledge), and the cross-recensional reach (whether the seed and the typology have external witnesses). The structural layer-and-voice reading and the recall ladder ran underneath all four. A scope gate ran first: the question coded throughout is descriptive ("how is a person matched to a teaching or object," and how firmly the tradition vouches for it), not the normative practice question ("what object should an angry temperament use"), which the corpus cannot adjudicate and the tradition itself makes teacher-assigned. Readings not coded, because they do not bear here: pre-Buddhist provenance (the apparatus is a Buddhist scholastic construction with no obvious pan-Indian antecedent) and harmonization (the tradition flags the apparatus as unauthoritative rather than working to reconcile it with a canonical tension).

### B.3 Pre-registration

The hypotheses, the null, and the prediction were frozen before coding (2026-06). The working hypothesis was that the matching apparatus is post-canonical against an early, pre-sectarian seed, that its central term is a re-coinage, and that the tradition never stakes the elaborate part under its own verification formula. The null was that the apparatus is uniformly commentarial against a uniformly canonical core, with one stable sense for the central term and the matching staked as vouched-for knowledge. The shape found matches the working hypothesis: the temperament-*carita* compound is absent from the four Nikāyas and the Abhidhamma (a hard lexical absence, not a register label converted into a date); the term drifts across five strata; and the Visuddhimagga itself marks its temperament diagnostics as not to be relied on. Two prior verdicts were re-asserted from the same census rather than restated, so a silent drift would have failed the build: the three-tier headline (below) and the samatha-and-vipassanā reading both held.
