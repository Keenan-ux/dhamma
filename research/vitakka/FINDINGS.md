# The Apparatus of Absorption: is the jhāna meditation-manual vocabulary canonical or commentarial?

*A provenance study by chronological stratum, per character. dhamma-research method; pre-registered
(`RESEARCH-DESIGN.md`, frozen before the sense-audited enumeration); disclosed seed = DR-1 (project memory
`jhana-vitakka-deep-research.md`). Corpus snapshot 194,710 passages, 2026-06-25. v1.0. The live canonical
version is the `VitakkaStudy` page in the admin-gated Research tab; this file is the secondary readable record.*

## Abstract

The commentaries organise jhāna with a technical apparatus the meditation manuals treat as standard: a graded
scale with access concentration (*upacāra-samādhi*) below full absorption (*appanā-samādhi*), and a re-gloss
of the first jhāna factor *vitakka* not as "thinking" but as *abhiniropana*, "the placing of the mind on its
object". By chronological stratum, the per-character measure shows the apparatus to be a post-Nikāya
construction. In the meditative sense *upacāra* is absent from the early canon: all 14 early-canonical
occurrences carry the Vinaya "vicinity, precinct, boundary" sense, and blind coders found none in the
access-concentration sense; per character the term runs about 23 times denser in the commentary. Absorption
(*appanā*) and the *abhiniropana* re-gloss have a single early-canonical anchor between them, the
*Mahācattārīsaka* (MN 117) definition string; *abhiniropana* then appears in the Paṭisambhidāmagga, the
Abhidhamma, and the commentary. The state the apparatus organises is, by contrast, canonical: the
hindrance-free, ready mind the early canon names *vinīvaraṇacitta* and *kallacitta* runs about twice as dense
in the canon as in the commentary. The tradition did not invent the settled pre-absorption mind. It built a
graded scheme and a re-glossed *vitakka* on canonical material that named neither. This settles the
provenance of the apparatus vocabulary, not the meaning of *vitakka* inside the discourses.

## I. The apparatus, and the question

A meditator trained on a modern manual meets a graded scale: the mind settles to *upacāra-samādhi*, "access
concentration", a hindrance-free threshold, then drops into *appanā-samādhi*, full "absorption", the jhāna
proper. On the same scheme the first factor of the first jhāna, *vitakka*, is read not as everyday "thinking"
but technically, as *abhiniropana*, the placing of the mind on its object. This is the working language of the
Visuddhimagga. The question is where it comes from, and it is a question of provenance, asked of the terms by
stratum and per character. It is not a question about what *vitakka* means inside the jhāna formula, a
separate and contested matter the per-character measure cannot settle.

## III. The single early anchor (MN 117)

The apparatus has exactly one foothold in the early canon, and it is a definition, not a practice. In the
*Mahācattārīsaka* (MN 117) right intention is defined with a string of near-synonyms for directed thought:
*takko vitakko saṅkappo appanā byappanā cetaso abhiniropanā vacīsaṅkhāro*. This one sentence carries both
*appanā* and *abhiniropana*, and it is the lone early-canonical occurrence of each in the technical sense.
After it the gloss is taken up in the Paṭisambhidāmagga and the Niddesa, then the Abhidhamma, then the
commentary; the Digital Pāli Dictionary dates the technical sense of the gloss to the Paṭisambhidāmagga.
Every early-canonical occurrence of the three apparatus terms (17 rows) was read and coded by three coders
blind to the thesis, who agreed completely (Fleiss κ = 1.0): 2 are the technical sense, both this one MN 117
sentence; the other 15 are everyday senses, almost all the Vinaya's spatial *upacāra* ("crossing the boundary
of an unenclosed village") plus one *appaṇāmento*, "not dismissing", which only looks like *appanā*.

## IV. The phenomenon is canonical; the label is late

A term being late does not mean the experience it names is late. The phenomenon the access-concentration
scheme organises, a mind cleared of the hindrances and made pliant, is named and described in the early
canon, though without the graded-scale framework: before the Buddha teaches the gradual talk, the listener's
mind has become *kallacitta*, *muducitta*,
*vinīvaraṇacitta*, ready, malleable, free of the hindrances (AN 8.12, AN 8.21).

| Term | Early canon /Mc | Commentary /Mc | early : comm |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| *vinīvaraṇa(citta)* hindrance-free mind | 1.37 | 0.64 | **2.14** |
| *kallacitta* a ready mind | 1.07 | 0.56 | **1.91** |
| *mudu / kammañña* malleable, workable | 1.53 | 3.98 | 0.38 |

The hindrance-free mind and the ready mind are about twice as dense in the canon. The one phenomenon word that
runs the other way is the generic pair *mudu* / *kammañña*, which the commentary uses freely across contexts
and which names no specific pre-absorption state; it is recorded, not leaned on. The narrow reading: the early
canon has the pliant mind without the graded labels, and the commentary supplied the labels and the scale.

## V. What this settles, and what it does not

This settles where the apparatus vocabulary lives: access concentration, absorption, and the *abhiniropana*
re-gloss of *vitakka* are post-Nikāya, with a single early definitional anchor in MN 117, while the pliant
pre-absorption mind they organise is early-canonical. It does not settle what *vitakka* means inside the
jhāna formula. Whether the *vitakka* of the first jhāna is ordinary discursive thinking or already a narrowed
"application of mind" is a live dispute, and a per-character term count cannot decide it. Two limits reinforce
this: the Abhidhamma's high *vitakka* density is a feature of its list-making genre, not evidence of meaning;
and the search matches surface strings, not stems, so every count is a measured floor.
