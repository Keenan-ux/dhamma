# Vitakka and the "application" (abhiniropana) gloss: a corpus density test that comes back null

## Abstract

Theravada commentary settles on a tidy gloss for vitakka: it is the mind's "lifting onto the object" (abhiniropana), paired with vicara as the mind's "moving over the object" (anumajjana). The canonical suttas use vitakka more loosely, inside an open field of thinking, intention, and proliferation (takka, sankappa, papanca, cinta). A natural question follows: can a corpus measurement date when the application reading hardens into a definition? The house thesis (H1) said yes, that within passages co-mentioning vitakka the application lexicon should rise relative to the discursive lexicon as the text moves from canon (mula) to commentary (atthakatha) to subcommentary (tika), and that abhiniropana should acquire definitional-head status first in the commentary layer. The pre-committed null (H0) said the apparent rise would be flat or non-monotonic once length is controlled, and would be matched by an identical rise on a negative control (piti), making it a generic commentary register or length artifact rather than a vitakka-specific change.

The null wins. The measurement does not support the hardening claim, and the negative control moved with the signal. The result is reported as a confirmed null. It is a statement about what a density measurement can and cannot show; it is not a doctrinal claim about what vitakka means.

## The question, and why this is a fair test

The test is built so that the predicted pattern could have appeared and been believed. The primary statistic (Q3) is the within-target ratio of application-bearing passages to discursive-bearing passages, computed only among passages that already mention vitakka or vicara, then read across the three layers. H1 names a specific shape: a monotone rise mula then attha then tika, with the largest jump at the mula-to-attha boundary. H0 names the alternative outcomes (flat, or non-monotonic), and adds a hard condition: a co-present but non-debated jhana factor, piti, must NOT show the same movement. The falsifier was committed in writing before the numbers were read: "If the piti control moves with vitakka, the effect is declared an artifact regardless of the vitakka numbers." That is the fairness of the design. The control can kill the finding on its own.

Magnitudes are per-character throughout, not raw passage counts, because longer commentary passages would otherwise inflate raw co-occurrence counts. The layer label itself carries no evidentiary weight: stratum here is a lookup on the work identifier, a textual-role partition, not a dated chronology. The evidence, if any, lives in the ratio shape and in the negative control, not in the label.

## What the canon shows

In the mula layer, vitakka sits where the suttas put it: in the discursive and intentional field. Among vitakka-mentioning mula passages, discursive co-mentions outnumber application co-mentions by roughly five to one (Q3: 23 application passages, 108 discursive passages, ratio 0.213). The canonical frame that does place abhiniropana beside vitakka is the synonym chain "takko vitakko sankappo appana byappana cetaso abhiniropana vacisankharo" (MN 117 Mahacattarisaka, and the parallel Dhammasangani and Vibhanga magga-lists). In every one of these the term abhiniropana is a list-item glossing sammasankappa (right intention), not a predicate asserted of vitakka as its defining characteristic. The harvest of "defining-frame" mula rows (Q5) bears this out directly: of the 15 mula candidate rows, the seven Dhammasangani and Vibhanga rows are sammasankappa list contexts, two are the MN suttas (MN 117 synonym chain, MN 139), and the rest are very large passages (Culaniddesa at about 128k characters, two Petakopadesa passages at about 36k and 112k, three Patisambhidamga passages between about 63k and 225k) where the three lexica co-occur by passage size, not by predication. None is a vitakka head-noun definition. The Q5 query tests passage-level co-presence, not head-noun predication, so these mula counts are co-mention artifacts.

## What the commentary does, and the falsifiable core

The defining gloss is a real commentary form. "Vitakkassa abhiniropanalakkhanam, vicarassa anumajjanalakkhanam" recurs verbatim across the atthakatha (for example the Digha, Majjhima, and Anguttara commentaries), and the Visuddhimagga develops it with the bell-strike and bird-flight similes. The defining-frame first-appearance question (Q6) is exactly what the deferred blind coding is for, and it is the only narrow claim the study can still support if that coding clears its gate.

But the load-bearing density claim is the ratio shape, and the shape is wrong for H1. The within-vitakka application/discursive ratio is 0.213 in mula, rises to 0.510 in attha, then FALLS to 0.441 in tika (Q3). That is non-monotonic. It peaks at attha and declines into tika. The monotone mula-then-attha-then-tika rise that H1 requires is absent, and the H1 falsifier names this outcome explicitly.

The per-character densities (Q2) show why. Application and discursive densities do not diverge across layers; they move together. Both jump from mula into the commentary layers and both dip from attha into tika. The driver is visible in the denominator: the target-subset character mass collapses from 4.864 Mchar in mula to 0.709 Mchar in attha, then rises to 1.140 Mchar in tika. A register and length shift over the target subset produces this co-movement. It is not a vitakka-specific lexical change.

## The negative-control result

The control settles the verdict on its own. Piti is a co-present jhana factor that is not the subject of an application-gloss debate, so its within-factor application/discursive ratio across layers is the artifact baseline. It shows the same low-peak-then-fall shape as vitakka: mula 0.304, attha 0.813, tika 0.520 (Q4). The piti rise from mula to attha is 0.509, which exceeds the vitakka rise of 0.297. The control did not stay flat, and it moved with the signal, and it moved at least as much. Under the pre-committed falsifier the effect is an artifact.

A second control points the same way. Abhiniropana-style head-noun glossing should not attach to piti at commentary density if the defining-frame skeleton were vitakka-specific. It does attach: the piti defining-frame harvest (Q8) returns mula 11, attha 12, tika 6, other 17. Not empty, not vitakka-specific, and densest at the mula end. The defining-frame skeleton (lakkhana, rasa, paccupatthana, nama, attho) is commentary boilerplate that co-occurs with piti at mula density too. The secondary control fails the same way the primary one does. Because piti and vitakka are co-listed jhana factors, the control is not independent of the signal; its tracking of the signal is itself the artifact evidence and is reported as such, not as a separate confirmation.

## What it means, and what it does not

It means the corpus density measurement does not, on its own, confirm a vitakka-specific hardening of the application gloss across the layers. The apparent attha bump is a register and length effect that the piti control reproduces. The hardening thesis H1 is falsified on two independent grounds, and the pre-committed null is reported as the result.

It does NOT mean the application gloss is canonical, nor that "the thinking reading is original," nor anything about what the Buddha meant by vitakka. The mula abhiniropana hits are uncoded co-mentions, dominated by the MN 117 and Vibhanga sammasankappa synonym chain where abhiniropana is a list-item, not a definition of vitakka. Density cannot distinguish a list-item from a definitional head-noun. That discrimination is the job of the deferred blind coding (Q5, 73 rows, layer-masked), which can at most support a narrow qualitative claim about WHERE abhiniropana first functions as a definitional head-noun for vitakka. It cannot revive the density claim, and it must clear the kappa gate (Cohen kappa at least 0.6 on the definitional_head label) before the first-appearance statement is reported. The blind coders agree at high rates where their codings coincide, but the inter-annotator kappa is computed downstream and the first-appearance claim stands only to the extent that adjudicated agreement supports it.

The doctrinal-historical structure being probed here (canon co-mentions the term; Atthasalini and Visuddhimagga develop it into the defining lakkhana) is already standard in published Theravada scholarship. The original contribution attempted was the corpus-wide density test. That test is null. The honest framing is that density does not independently confirm the known structure.

## Auditability

Every number above re-derives from the two committed files in this study directory. The layer partition, the lexica, and each query string are fixed in `_protocol.json`. The returned counts are in `_raw.json`. Q3 ratios re-derive as 23/108 = 0.213, 50/98 = 0.510, 60/136 = 0.441. Q4 piti ratios re-derive as 17/56 = 0.304, 13/16 = 0.813, 13/25 = 0.520, with the mula-to-attha jumps 0.509 (piti) and 0.297 (vitakka). The Q5 harvest contains 73 rows; by layer they are 28 attha, 20 tika, 15 mula, 10 other, and the 15 mula ids are enumerable from the file. Q8 piti defframe counts are mula 11, attha 12, tika 6, other 17. The per-Mchar densities and target-subset Mchar values are read directly from Q2.

## Scope and limits

This is a corpus-linguistic result about lexical and definitional patterning by textual layer, not a doctrinal or historical-dating result. Three caveats are load-relevant and are stated rather than hidden. First, pli-vism (Visuddhimagga) is shelved as mula by convention but counted as attha here; every "mula" definitional row in the targeted pull (Q7) is in fact pli-vism, and the attha result is partly inflated by repeated near-identical Visuddhimagga jhana-gloss passages that the dedup did not collapse. The pli-vism-excluded sensitivity was not run, so the attha bump should not be treated as independent occurrences. Second, the control-versus-signal comparison rests on small passage counts (for example attha piti is 13 application and 16 discursive) and the protocol's bootstrap confidence intervals were not produced, so the artifact verdict is reported qualitatively (control moves with signal, same shape, comparable-or-larger jump) and the "0.509 versus 0.297" margin is not asserted as a stable point estimate. Third, the Q9 diacritic zero-guard did not complete (statement timeout); non-zero firing of the diacritic-critical stems is only inferred indirectly from the Q5 and Q7 rows that contain them, and the explicit zero-guard must be re-run to completion before the run is declared fully valid, since a silent ASCII-fold null would invalidate the lexica.
