# Vitakka and the "application" (abhiniropana) gloss: does the reading harden by layer?

## Abstract

A long-standing reading holds that vitakka, in the earliest Pali strata, sits in an open field of thinking and intention (sankappa, takka, papanca, cinta), and that the narrower "application of mind to its object" reading, carried by the word abhiniropana and its kin, is a commentarial development first systematized in the Atthasalini and Visuddhimagga. This study asks a corpus-linguistic version of that claim: within passages that mention vitakka or vicara, does the application vocabulary harden relative to the discursive vocabulary as one moves from the canonical mula layer to the commentary (atthakatha) and sub-commentary (tika) layers, and does it do so in a way the control term piti does not share?

The pre-committed test fails. The within-vitakka application-over-discursive passage-presence ratio is 0.213 in mula, 0.510 in attha, 0.441 in tika: a rise into commentary followed by a fall, not the monotone climb the hypothesis requires. The piti negative control, a co-present jhana factor that is not the subject of any application-gloss debate, carries the same low-mula, peak-attha, fall-tika shape (0.304, 0.813, 0.520) with a larger jump from mula to attha (about 0.51 versus about 0.30 for vitakka). A pre-committed falsifier states that if the control moves with the same shape, the effect is declared an artifact regardless of the vitakka numbers. The secondary piti control fails as well: the defining-frame skeleton attaches to piti at mula density (11 rows) rather than being empty. The verdict on the quantitative metric is null.

One axis is held back from the null. Whether the definitional-head form (abhiniropanalakkhano vitakko, abhiniropana predicated of vitakka as its characteristic) appears FIRST in commentary cannot be settled from regex co-occurrence alone. Those rows need blind coding with an agreement check before any first-appearance claim is made or denied, so that question is reported as open, not as part of the null.

## The question and why it is a fair test

The anchor term is vitakka and vicara. All densities are computed only within passages that contain that anchor, so the comparison is internal to the gloss subject. Two competing vocabularies are tracked inside those passages: an APPLICATION field (abhiniropana, abhiropana, aropana, anuropana, anumajjana, anusancarana, vicarana, upanijjhana, patitthapana) and a DISCURSIVE field (papanca, sankappa, takka, vitakka, cinta, cetana). The hypothesis predicts that the application field rises relative to the discursive field across mula, attha, tika.

The test is fair because it is built to fail in three independent ways before it can be believed. First, the layer ratio must be monotone; a flat or non-monotone shape falsifies it. Second, the same measurement is run on piti, a jhana factor that co-occurs with vitakka but is not glossed as application; if piti's ratio rises with the same shape, the vitakka rise is generic register, not a vitakka-specific shift. Third, all counts are per-character over the target subset, so a longer commentary passage cannot inflate a raw count. The layer label itself carries no evidentiary weight: layer is a lookup on the work identifier, so a mula-versus-attha split is true by construction and is never cited as independent evidence.

A diacritic zero-guard was specified to confirm that no search term silently returned zero through an ASCII-folding error. That guard query did not complete (statement timeout). Non-zero hits are confirmed indirectly through the 73 returned defining-frame rows and the per-term counts that appear inside them, but the committed guard remains unrun, so diacritic integrity is only partially verified.

## What the canon shows

In the mula layer the application vocabulary inside vitakka passages is sparse and, where it appears, it appears as a list item, not a definition. The defining-frame harvest returns ten mula co-occurrences, and every one is an enumeration or synonym chain rather than a predication of vitakka. The four Dhammasangani rows are the standard dhamma enumerations (phasso hoti, vedana hoti, ... vitakko hoti, vicaro hoti) and the sammasankappa formula "takko vitakko sankappo appana byappana cetaso abhiniropana." The three Patisambhidamagga rows and the two Petakopadesa rows are large doctrinal blocks. The Niddesa row is a 128k-character commentary-style gloss. MN 117 supplies the one canonical frame where abhiniropana sits beside vitakka, and there too it is one member of a chain, not a statement that vitakka IS application.

The raw within-target densities show the shape clearly. Application per Mchar is 4.729 in mula, jumps to 70.473 in attha, then falls to 52.624 in tika. Discursive per Mchar moves in lockstep: 22.204 in mula, 138.127 in attha, 119.282 in tika. Both fields jump into commentary together and both dip from attha to tika together. That joint movement is the signature of a register and length change over the target subset, not of one vocabulary hardening against the other. The mula target subset averages roughly 8,430 characters per passage (4.864 Mchar over 577 rows) while the attha subset averages roughly 721 characters per passage (0.709 Mchar over 984 rows), so passage-presence counting weights a single giant mula block the same as a one-line attha gloss. The low mula ratio is partly that dilution, not application-gloss rarity.

## What the commentary does, and the falsifiable core

In the commentary layer the definitional-head form is present and stereotyped. The recurring atthakatha line "Vitakkassa abhiniropanalakkhanam, vicarassa anumajjanalakkhanam, pitiya pharanalakkhanam ..." appears verbatim across the Digha, Majjhima, Anguttara, and Khuddaka commentaries, and the fuller Visuddhimagga-style gloss "arammane cittassa abhiniropanalakkhano vitakko" recurs throughout. So the surface pattern that the secondary literature treats as the commentarial gloss is indeed dense in attha and tika. That part is corroboration of a known reading, stated here only in density terms.

The falsifiable core is the ratio shape plus the control. The shape is non-monotone, which the hypothesis names as a falsifier outright. The control then settles it. The pre-committed rule is explicit: if the piti ratio rises with the same slope as vitakka's, the effect is an artifact regardless of the vitakka numbers. Piti's ratio is 0.304, 0.813, 0.520 across the three layers, the same low-mula, peak-attha, fall-tika shape, with a mula-to-attha jump (about 0.51) that exceeds vitakka's (about 0.30). The secondary piti control reinforces this: the defining-frame skeleton was supposed to be empty or flat on piti, and instead it is 11 rows in mula, 12 in attha, 6 in tika. The skeleton attaches to piti at mula density because piti sits inside the same jhana-factor characteristic lists, so the frame is commentary scaffolding rather than a vitakka-specific definitional act.

## The negative-control result

The negative control did not stay flat and it moved with the signal. Per the program rule that a finding stands only if its control did not move with it, the quantitative finding does not stand.

One qualification is owed on how to phrase the control failure. Vitakka does not itself rise monotonically, so the strict slope-match version of the artifact test (a control rising with the same monotone slope as the signal) is not the operative reading; both series are non-monotone. What the piti result actually shows is that the attha bump is not vitakka-specific: the same bump, larger, appears on a term that has no application-gloss debate attached to it. That is enough to deny a vitakka-specific effect, which is all the null needs.

## What it means and what it does NOT mean

It means that, in the Pali held in this corpus, the within-vitakka balance of application versus discursive vocabulary does not harden monotonically by layer, and the commentary-layer bump in application vocabulary is matched (and exceeded) by the same bump on piti. The measurable movement is consistent with commentary being a denser, shorter, more formulaic register over the target subset, not with a gloss shift specific to vitakka.

It does NOT mean the application reading is canonical, nor that it is absent from the canon. The first-appearance question, whether the definitional-head predication of vitakka appears first in commentary, is a separate axis that this run does not settle. In the mula rows returned here every abhiniropana co-occurrence is a list item or synonym chain, while the predicated definitional form clusters in attha and tika; but distinguishing a definition from a co-mention requires blind coding, and the agreement threshold (Cohen kappa at least 0.6) has not been computed, nor has the 50-row application-sense audit that guards against the unrelated senses of aropana (planting, raising, mounting a debate). Until that coding passes, the first-appearance claim is neither confirmed nor denied. It must not be absorbed into the quantitative null.

It also does NOT settle anything doctrinal. This is a statement about where a lexical and definitional surface pattern sits in the held text by layer. It says nothing about what the Buddha meant by vitakka and does not adjudicate the thinking-versus-application interpretive debate.

Two further limits bound the numbers. The attha and tika ratios rest on small denominators (vitakka attha application 50 over discursive 98; piti attha application 13 over discursive 16; piti tika application 13 over discursive 25), so magnitude comparisons such as "0.51 exceeds 0.30" need bootstrap confidence intervals before they carry weight; point estimates alone do not support fine magnitude claims. And pli-vism (the Visuddhimagga) is shelved as mula by work role but is counted as attha here by convention; it contributes 5 of the 25 attha defining-frame hits, so the attha definitional density is partly a convention choice, not a fact about chronology. Without pli-vism the attha defining-frame count is 20 of 25 and the direction is unchanged.

## Auditability

Every count above re-derives from the two committed files. _protocol.json holds the frozen lexica, the layer definitions (mula is work role mula excluding pli-vism; attha is any work slug ending in -attha plus pli-vism; tika is any work slug ending in -tika), the enumeration SQL, the negative controls, and the falsifiers. _raw.json holds the executed results: Q0 layer denominators, Q1 base rates, Q2 within-target per-Mchar densities, Q3 the vitakka ratio shape, Q4 the piti control shape, Q5 the 73 defining-frame rows keyed by passage id, Q6 the per-work defining-frame counts, Q7 the primary-loci pull, Q8 the piti defining-frame control, and Q9 the diacritic zero-guard, which returned a statement-timeout error and did not complete. The 73 Q5 rows are individually inspectable by id (for example cst-s0101a.att-dn1_1_p154 for the stereotyped atthakatha characteristic line, mn117 for the canonical synonym chain, cst-abh01m.mul-016 for a Dhammasangani enumeration). The numbers reported here are read directly from those result rows; none are recomputed or estimated, except the per-passage average sizes, which are the Q1 and Q2 character sums divided by the stated row counts.

## Scope and status

Verdict: null on the quantitative primary metric. The qualitative first-appearance axis is open and requires blind coding with kappa at least 0.6 before any claim. The diacritic zero-guard must be re-run to clear the whole-study-invalid condition before these numbers are reused.
