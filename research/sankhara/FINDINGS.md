# Saṅkhāra and the Translator: does the rendering track the collocation field?

*A study of translation behaviour on identical canonical text. dhamma-research method; pre-registered
(`RESEARCH-DESIGN.md`, frozen before coding) with a disclosed seed (the deep-research report
`research/deep-research/sankhara.md`). Corpus snapshot 194,710 passages, 2026-06-25. v1.0. The live,
canonical version is the `SankharaStudy` page in the admin-gated Research tab; this file is the secondary
readable record.*

## Abstract

The Pāli word *saṅkhāra* is built from *saṃ* ("together") and the root *kar* ("to do, make"), and it
carries an active-and-passive ambiguity by construction: the act of constructing, and the thing
constructed. The canon sets the word in fixed collocation fields that lean different ways. The
dependent-origination link (*avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā*) and the fourth aggregate (*saṅkhārakkhandha*, which
the canon itself glosses as the classes of volition) carry the constructing, kammic sense; the body, speech,
and mind triad a process sense; and the impermanence maxim (*sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā*) the passive,
conditioned-things sense. This study asks whether a modern translator who renders the whole canon
disambiguates *saṅkhāra* by field or holds to one word. On the corpus's two highest-coverage modern
translators the answer is sharp and opposite, and it holds at two levels. Across full coverage, Sujato's
leading word *choices* holds in 317 of his 513 own-text rows but gives way to *processes* for the triad and
*conditions* for the maxim, while Thanissaro's *fabrications* holds in 142 of 155 across every field. On the
smaller set of identical maxim lines the corpus carries for both (Dhammapada 277, SN 22.90, MN 35; five
Sujato rows against three of Thanissaro's) the two sit at opposite poles. Coders blind to the translators
place *choices* as active and *conditions* as passive, so Sujato's switch tracks the active-to-passive
boundary the grammar marks, while Thanissaro carries one active-leaning word into the passive slot. The
finding is a
per-translator consistency-and-disambiguation profile with named exemplars, not inferential statistics: it
settles how each translator behaves and how consistent each is, not which rendering is right.

## I. The word, and the fields it sits in

A single Pāli noun does the work of several English ones. Like the English "building", which names both the
act and the result, *saṅkhāra* points two ways: toward the active constructing volition performs, and toward
the passive result, the conditioned thing put together. The canon does not resolve the ambiguity; it deploys
the word in fixed phrases that each lean one way. In dependent origination, *saṅkhārā* are what ignorance
conditions, the kammic activities that drive rebirth. As the fourth aggregate they are glossed by the canon
as the six classes of volition (*cha cetanākāyā*). In the body-speech-mind triad they are the in-and-out
breath, applied-and-sustained thought, and perception-and-feeling. In the recited maxim *sabbe saṅkhārā
aniccā*, "all *saṅkhārā* are impermanent", the word reaches its widest, passive span: everything
conditioned. The corpus carries 953 own-text rows with the word (80 dependent-origination, 99 aggregate, 156
triad, 30 maxim). The question is what a translator does when one word must cross all of them.

## II. Two translators at opposite poles

The two translators the corpus covers most fully are Sujato, whose complete SuttaCentral canon supplies 513
own-text *saṅkhāra* rows, and Thanissaro, whose Access to Insight corpus supplies 155. Read across the four
fields on matched exemplar passages, they behave in opposite ways.

| Collocation field | Sujato | Thanissaro |
|---|---|---|
| Dependent origination (*avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā*) | choices | fabrications |
| The fourth aggregate (*saṅkhārakkhandha*) | choices | fabrications |
| The body-speech-mind triad (*kāya/vacī/cittasaṅkhāra*) | processes | fabrications |
| The impermanence maxim (*sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā*) | conditions | fabrications |
| **Distinct words across the fields** | **3** (choices, conditions, processes) | **1** (fabrications) |

Sujato's word changes with the field; Thanissaro's does not. Thanissaro's dominant rendering holds in 142 of
his 155 rows; Sujato's leading word *choices* holds in 317 of 513, the remainder being the field switches.

## III. The impermanence maxim, in full (the falsifiable core)

The cleanest test sits at the maxim, because the line is short enough that the word standing for *saṅkhāra*
reads straight off the English. The maxim is also where the active-and-passive split is most exposed: *sabbe
saṅkhārā aniccā* means "all conditioned things are impermanent", the passive sense, and a translator who has
been writing an active word elsewhere must decide whether to keep it.

| Passage | Translator | Renders *saṅkhārā* as | Sense (blind majority) |
|---|---|---|---|
| AN 3.136 | Sujato | conditions | passive |
| Dhp 273-289 | Sujato | conditions | passive |
| MN 35 | Sujato | conditions | passive |
| SN 22.90 | Sujato | conditions | passive |
| Thag 15.1 | Sujato | conditions | passive |
| Dhp 273-289 | Thanissaro | fabrications | active |
| MN 35 | Thanissaro | fabrications | active |
| SN 22.90 | Thanissaro | fabrications | active |
| Dhp 273-289 | Buddharakkhita | conditioned things | passive |
| Thag 15.1 | Olendzki | conceptions | passive |
| Dhp 273-289 | Suddhaso | everything | neutral |

Sujato switches to *conditions* on all five of his maxim rows; Thanissaro keeps *fabrications* on all three
of his. The other translators render the maxim passively or loosely. Thanissaro is the one translator who
carries an active-leaning word into the slot.

One contamination has to be ruled out. A bare count of "condition" would over-count, because *paccaya*
("requisite condition", the dependent-origination connector) and *saṅkhata* ("conditioned") also surface as
"condition" in English, and neither is *saṅkhāra*. The maxim reading is safe from both because it is coded
on the single short line, where the rendered word is the grammatical subject of "are impermanent" and is
therefore *saṅkhārā* with certainty; *paccaya* sits in the dependent-origination phrasing ("from ignorance
as a requisite condition come fabrications"), never in the maxim subject. Each maxim row stores its rendered
English line, so the boundary is checkable: Sujato's five lines all read "all conditions are impermanent",
Thanissaro's three "all fabrications are inconstant". The corpus-wide background count, used only to confirm
the defaults, strips "requisite condition" and "as a condition" before counting for the same reason.

Recorded but excluded from the clean count: the Visuddhimagga's quotation of the maxim (commentary, not
canon), and SN 22.43, where the maxim verse is present in the Pāli but both Sujato and Walshe render the
surrounding per-aggregate prose ("all form", "all bodies"), so no single maxim word is isolable.

## IV. Does the switch track the sense?

That Sujato's word changes by field is a plain observation anyone can re-check from the rows. Whether the
change is principled, tracking the active-and-passive boundary rather than being arbitrary, is a question
about the English words' sense. Five coders classified each rendering from a construction-act-versus-result
codebook, blind to which translator used which word and to the thesis. They agreed at Fleiss κ = 0.69 overall,
across the 14 distinct renderings. Per word, on the two that carry the headline they were decisive:
*conditions* and "conditioned things" were called passive by all five coders, *choices* active by four of
five (the per-word codings are in `_iaa_sense.json`). So Sujato's move from *choices* to *conditions* at the
maxim is a move from the active pole to the passive one, the switch the sense calls for. Thanissaro's
*fabrications* was called active by four of five; carried unchanged into the passive maxim slot, it imports
the constructing sense where the line means the conditioned. The colourless renderings behaved as expected:
"formations" came out neutral, "processes" split between active and neutral. The headline does not rest on
these calls, only on the fact that the words differ by field; the sense coding tells us the difference is the
doctrinally apt one, not noise.

## V. The rest of the panel, and what the corpus cannot place

Beyond the two poles the panel thins quickly. Ñāṇamoli supplies 240 rows, but they are the Visuddhimagga, a
commentary, and his default there is the deliberately colourless *formations* (93 rows); he is profiled for
his word, not entered in the canonical head-to-head. Walshe's Dīgha gives 18 rows, also defaulting to
*formations*, but with no isolable maxim line his field-disambiguation cannot be read. The remaining
translators appear only in single figures; where they reach the maxim they render it passively or loosely
(Buddharakkhita "conditioned things", Olendzki "conceptions", Suddhaso "everything", which flattens even the
maxim's own three lines into one word). Two translators the literature would most want are beyond reach:
Horner, the one translator documented to split *saṅkhāra* by context (MN 44), has no own-text *saṅkhāra* rows
in the corpus at all, and Bodhi, the advocate of one fixed word, has two (SN 5.10, SN 12.23). Placing either
would need their full Saṃyutta and Majjhima ingested; that is named future work, not a precondition for the
Sujato-and-Thanissaro result, which is already saturated.

## VI. What this settles, and what it does not

The finding is narrow by design. It settles three things: each translator's dominant word, how consistently
that word is held, and whether the rendering tracks the collocation field. It does not settle which rendering
is correct. A single-word policy has a real defence, that one Pāli term should answer to one English term so a
reader can follow it across contexts, and the data shows Thanissaro is consistent in holding to it, not that
he is wrong. Sujato is equally consistent in the opposite policy, letting the English follow the contextual
sense at the cost of one-to-one traceability; his three-way split is an observed pattern in the renderings,
reported here as behaviour, not attributed to a stated principle. Nor is this a claim about *saṅkhāra*'s
meaning: the active-and-passive split is the translators', read off their English, and the corpus cannot
adjudicate the underlying semantics. The head-to-head intersection is small, a handful of suttas that carry
the maxim and more than one translator, so the numbers are a demonstration with named exemplars, not
inferential statistics. What holds is the shape: on the same lines, the two translators the corpus covers
most fully render the canon's most-recited maxim at opposite ends of the word's own ambiguity.

## VII. The pre-registered predictions, scored

- **P1 (Sujato disambiguates) — PASS.** His maxim word is *conditions* (passive) on all five clean canon
  maxim rows, distinct from his default *choices* (active, 317/513); his triad word is *processes*.
  Disconfirming "choices"-at-maxim rows: 0 (threshold for failure was 2).
- **P2 (Thanissaro collapses) — PASS.** *fabrications* on all three clean canon maxim rows, his default
  (142/155). Disconfirming distinct-passive-word rows: 0 (threshold was 1).
- **P3 (opposite poles) — PASS.** Sujato 3 distinct field words, Thanissaro 1.
- **P4 (coverage limit) — REPORTED, not scored.** Horner own-text *saṅkhāra* rows: 0 (their full Saṃyutta
  and Majjhima are not ingested, so this is a corpus gap, not a disconfirmed negative); Bodhi: 2. Both too
  thin to place. This is a measured limit, not a pass or fail.

## Appendix — auditability

Every rendering resolves to a real corpus id, opening in the live reader, and is listed in the served dataset
(`public/research/sankhara.json`). The corpus counts re-derive from `_enumerate.py` (committed
query-to-result); the deduped own-text totals (Sujato 513, Thanissaro 155, Walshe 18, Ñāṇamoli 240) reproduce
the prior deep-research probe (`research/deep-research/sankhara.md`) exactly. The collocation field is coded
from the Pāli; the rendering from the English, by independent rules. The sense-family coding is by five blind
coders (Fleiss κ = 0.69 on 14 distinct renderings); both the per-coder codings and the majority are in
`_iaa_sense.json` and the served dataset. The pre-registration (`RESEARCH-DESIGN.md`) was frozen before the
enumeration and coding.
