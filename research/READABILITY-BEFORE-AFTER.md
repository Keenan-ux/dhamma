## 2. AFTER (rewritten to spec)

### From the Abstract → an idea-shaped opening for §I and the §III headline

The abstract gets two jobs split apart: a human opening that meets the subject (per readability element 1), and a clean statement of the corrected finding with all apparatus below the line. Process words ("an earlier pass," "retrofit," "axis," tags, ids) leave the prose.

> **I. The shape of awakening in the texts**
>
> An awakening in the Pāli texts is rarely a private event. Far more often it is something a story reports: a listener's mind comes free at the close of a talk, an old monk sings the moment his striving ended, a redactor notes in passing that a hearer's "dust-free, stainless eye of the Dhamma" had opened.¹ If we gather every such moment in the corpus and ask a plain question of it, *who is awakening, when was the passage written, and in whose voice is the claim made*, the answers are not the ones the usual canon-and-commentary division leads us to expect.
>
> The first surprise is about age. The texts the tradition files as canonical are not, for this subject, mostly its old core. Of the 299 awakening events that sit in the canonical division, only a small floor of 38 belong to the early discourses and the Udāna. The other 261 are canonical by shelving but late by composition.² Most of them, by a wide margin, come from one stratum: the Apadāna, the late autobiographical verse in which named elders recount the past deed that ripened into their present release.
>
> **162 of 299** — awakening events that the corpus files as canon but that belong to one late stratum, the Apadāna. The early discourses contribute 38.
>
> The second surprise is about voice. I take it up in §IV; here it is enough to say that these awakenings are, in the main, narrated *about* the awakened rather than spoken *by* the one who awoke. A redactor reports them, or the elder himself recites his own verse. Yet they are not, as a first reading of the data suggested, wholly without the Buddha's own word. Read claim by claim, a small floor of passages is the Buddha recounting his own awakening in the first person, and a second small set is the Buddha declaring that someone else has awoken.³
>
> *This study proceeds as follows:*
>
> - **II. What counts as an awakening, and how it was found.** The stock phrases that mark an attainment, and why every count here is a floor rather than a ceiling.
> - **III. A canonical shelf, a late-canonical text.** How the 299 canonical events sort by date, and why the old division between canon and commentary turns out to run partly *inside* the canon.
> - **IV. Who says it: narrated awakenings and the Buddha's own word.** The four voices that narrate most of these events, and the floor of passages spoken by the Buddha of himself.
> - **V. A worked case: the elder of Candavatī.** One Apadāna awakening told in full, to show what the largest class actually looks like on the page.
> - **VI. What this means, and what it does not.** Why a late and narrated canonical corpus matters for how we read awakening, and the limits of saying so.

> **IV. Who says it: narrated awakenings and the Buddha's own word**
>
> *What the data say here, before any detail: the canonical awakenings are mostly told about someone, by a reporter or by the awakened person himself, and only a thin floor is the Buddha speaking of his own awakening.*
>
> When a text reports an awakening, someone is doing the reporting, and it is worth asking who. A flat sentence that "his mind was freed" has no speaker inside it claiming the event; it is the compiler's note. So the cautious reading counts a passage as the Buddha's own word only when the words themselves are unmistakably his, spoken of his own awakening. By that measure most of the 299 are not the Buddha's word at all. They fall into four ways of telling:
>
> - **The elder's own life-verse (162).** In the Apadāna the awakened monk recites, in the first person, the path from a long-ago good deed to his present release. This is the largest class by far.
> - **The disciple's own report (59).** In the Theragāthā and Therīgāthā the elder sings his attainment, closed by a compiler's colophon naming him.
> - **The compiler's flat note (46).** Across the early discourses, the Udāna, the Niddesa, the Vinaya frame-stories, and the Visuddhimagga, a narrator simply tells us a listener's mind came free.⁵
> - **Lineage and dialogue verse (14).** The Buddhavaṁsa's mass awakenings at the feet of past Buddhas, and the paracanonical exchanges of the Milindapañha.
>
> A first reading of this material recorded something stronger: that the canon never once puts an awakening in the Buddha's own mouth. Read claim by claim, that turns out to be too strong. A small set is different in kind. Here the Buddha narrates his own awakening, speaking as the seeker before the night, declaring the awakening itself, and recounting the last watch to a named listener.⁶ Seventeen passages do this, nine of them distinct once duplicate edition-copies of the same discourse are merged. They are the early-canonical floor of the whole census, and on them three independent coders agreed without exception.
>
> **9** — distinct recollections, across 17 passages, in which the Buddha speaks of his own awakening. The earlier count of zero was wrong.
>
> Nor does the Buddha speak only of himself. The same authoritative voice declares others' attainments: the destinies announced in the "mirror of the Dhamma" at Nādika, and the stream-entry declarations that recur across the canonical rows.⁷ So the cautious summary is double. Most awakenings here are told about the awakened, by a compiler or by the awakened elder. But a real floor of the Buddha's own word survives, both of his own awakening and of others'.
>
> **A worked case: the elder of Candavatī.** It helps to see one of the 162 in full, since the class is easy to count and easy to skip. An elder remembered as the one who "went to the three refuges" tells his story from its root. Lifetimes ago, in the city of Candavatī, he supported his mother and father.⁸ That care for his parents is the seed; the verse follows its fruit across the long round of births down to the life in which, the elder says plainly, "I attained arahantship." The voice is the elder's own, the frame is the compiler's anthology of such lives, and the mood is celebration of a ripened past rather than instruction in a method. This is what the largest single block of "canonical awakenings" actually is on the page: not the Buddha teaching the way to awaken, but a later tradition lovingly cataloguing who had.
>
> In sum, the canonical awakening corpus speaks mostly in the third person and the disciple's first person, with a thin but genuine seam of the Buddha's own first-person word. To the worked example of that elder's verse, and what it shows about the late stratum as a whole, I now turn in §V.
