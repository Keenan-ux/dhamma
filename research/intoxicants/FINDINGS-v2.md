# The Intoxicant Cluster Under the Provenance Signature: Why majja Is an Effect-Based Category, Re-Tested Against the Homograph Trap and the Canon-Commentary Strata

## Abstract

The earlier intoxicants study reached a clean, much-used conclusion: the canon's category for what the fifth precept forbids is not "alcohol" but `majja`, an open class defined by *effect* (whatever intoxicates) rather than by a named list of substances. That finding is correct, and this recoding under the provenance-signature method confirms it. But the method's two always-on disciplines, the recall ladder and the chronological-stratum pass, sharpen it in a way the single canon-versus-commentary axis could not. First, the recall ladder shows that the very terms the count rests on are a homograph minefield: the naive substring `majja` returns 1,455 rows, of which only 227 carry the intoxicant sense and 1,228 are the unrelated lexemes `majjha` (middle), `majjana` / `majjati` (polishing and wiping, from a different root), `miñja` (marrow), and the proper name Majjaka the elder. The liquor word `surā` collides with `sura` (a god), so its 926 substring rows reduce to 648 genuine liquor rows after a 278-row deva-and-name purge; `meraya` alone is clean (279 rows, no homograph), exactly as the prior study had flagged. The homograph-purged, deduplicated cluster net is 840 rows (mūla 273, aṭṭhakathā 294, ṭīkā 206, extra-canonical 67), every count reconfirmed by SQL `GROUP BY work_role`. Second, the chronology pass splits the finding into two strata that the structural layer hid. The named triad and the precept formula `surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī` are early-canonical and pan-Nikāya (AN, SN, MN, DN), and the monastic rule that names `surā` and `meraya` is pre-sectarian, attested across seven independent Vinaya recensions. The operative category-term `majja` is canonical too: the Vinaya rule's own offence-permutations turn on `majje` ("in the case of an intoxicant"), incurring the offence even when the drinker misperceives the substance. What is *commentarial* is the explicit effect-*definition* of that category as an open class. The phrase that states it, "*yaṃ vā panaññampi ... madanīyaṃ*" ("whatever else there is that is intoxicating"), with `surā` and `meraya` named as mere examples (`nidassanamatta`), occurs in exactly two rows, both commentarial, and in zero canonical rows. The category is effect-based, as the settled finding holds; the refinement is that the canon supplies the operative category-term and the effect-rationale piecemeal, while the commentary states the open effect-principle as a definition. The geography of provenance is therefore a pan-Buddhist named prohibition with a Pāli-local commentarial generalisation laid over it, the same template-versus-verdict shape the Uttarakuru recoding found, here read off intoxication instead of cosmology.

## The corrected headline

The earlier study answered a category-typology question (what does the canon classify as `majja`, and how) and concluded, rightly, that the category is gated by effect. The provenance-signature method keeps that conclusion and adds the two coordinates the single axis lacked: how reliably the enumeration is measured, and whether "the canon" and "the commentary" are doing what the contrast implies.

The corrected headline is this. The fifth precept names three things, `surā` (grain liquor), `meraya` (fermented fruit or floral wine), and `majja` (the generic intoxicant), and then frames the whole as `pamādaṭṭhāna`, the occasion of heedlessness (`kp2`, `sn14.25`, `an5.179`, `an8.39`, `dn31`, `snp2.14`) [canon]. That formula is early-canonical and spread across all four primary Nikāyas; it is not a thin seed but a robust canonical fixture. The monastic rule that makes drinking `surā` or `meraya` an offence is older still in the sense that matters most for antiquity: it is attested across seven independent Vinaya lines, Sarvāstivāda, Mūlasarvāstivāda, Lokottaravāda-Mahāsāṃghika, Mahāsāṃghika, Dharmaguptaka, Mahīśāsaka, and Kāśyapīya, so the named prohibition is pre-sectarian, not a Theravāda development (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`) [scholarship]. The operative category-term `majja` is canonical and load-bearing in its own right: the Vinaya rule's word-analysis lists five kinds of `surā` and five of `meraya` (a closed list), but its offence-permutations operate on the general term `majje`, ruling that drinking an intoxicant is an offence whether one perceives it as an intoxicant or not (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`) [canon]. The category does the work; the named substances are its instances.

What the canon does *not* do is state the category's open boundary as a definition. The explicit move, that `surā` and `meraya` are "mere examples" and that `majja` is "whatever else there is that is intoxicating, by drinking which one becomes intoxicated and heedless," is commentarial (`cst-s0506a.att-18_p049`, `cst-s0504a.att-83_p050`, `cst-s0305a.att-sn5_12_p079`, quoted by the sub-commentary `cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030`) [comm]. The effect-definition is the commentary drawing out, as a definition, a principle the canon already operates by, in three canonical places: the early effect-vocabulary `madanīye majjati` ("one is intoxicated by the intoxicating," `an5.81`), the three existential intoxications of youth, health, and life that share the root (`an3.39`), and the medicine ruling whose own criterion is the effect-test, allowing liquor in a decoction only "where the colour, smell, and taste of the liquor are not detectable," that is, only where it can no longer intoxicate (`cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_6`) [canon].

Two things follow that the single axis could not produce. First, the count the finding rests on was never the substring count: the naive `majja` figure overstates the intoxicant rows by more than sixfold, and the recall ladder is what makes the real number, 227 intoxicant `majja` rows inside an 840-row cluster, auditable. Second, "the category is effect-based" is true at two different strata for two different reasons, and only the explicit open-list *definition* is commentarial; the effect-*principle* is canonically operative. The contrast the prior study drew between a canonical precept and a commentarial generalisation is real, and it is now a datable, recension-checked fact rather than an impression.

## Methodology

### The provenance signature and the triage

The method replaces the single canon-versus-commentary axis with a provenance signature, a small triaged set of independent dimensions that together answer where a claim comes from, how original it is, and how literally it was meant. The signature is coded per load-bearing claim, not per row, because a single passage interleaves the named substances, the effect-principle, and the disciplinary ruling.

This study's question is a category typology: what the texts classify as `majja`, and by what criterion. Under the triage that question makes the recall ladder load-bearing (promoted from a discipline to a chapter, because the homograph trap is the study's central technical object), and makes chronological stratum and structured-absence load-bearing (a category whose members are asserted entities, with a "the canon never closes the list" negative at its heart). Cross-recensional reach is promoted because the finding rests on whether the named prohibition is early or pre-sectarian. Epistemic marking is, per the framework, load-bearing on category typologies whose members are asserted entities; here it is coded but it does not move the result, because every load-bearing claim sits at the same epistemic status (flat-background-assertion: a precept, a rule, and a definition are all stated declaratively, none under the canon's verification formula), so the epistemic column is uniform and is reported as a single fact rather than a chapter. The scope gate is honoured: this is a descriptive study of what the sources classify as an intoxicant and how, not a normative drug-ethics claim; where the prior study extended the principle to psychedelics, that extension was already flagged as interpretation, not a canonical ruling, and is not re-litigated here.

Axes not coded, with the one-line warrant the framework requires. Attribution (I.2) is not coded as a chapter because the load-bearing claims are stock formula and disciplinary ruling whose speaker is uniform (the precept is redactor-transmitted training-rule text, the rule is the Vinaya frame); no claim turns on whether the Buddha personally asserted the open category. Pre-Buddhist provenance (I.5) is not coded because the question is the *Buddhist* category's boundary, not whether intoxication-ethics is inherited; a comparative-Indic pass would be a different study. Genre and register (I.3) is coded lightly, only to note that the lay precept (gnomic and prudential), the monastic rule (disciplinary-legal), and the commentarial gloss (scholastic) make their claims in registers that match their strata. Harmonization (I.8) does not fire: the tradition states the effect-definition without strain, with no reconciling apparatus attached.

## The chronology axis: an early-canonical named prohibition, a commentarial open definition

The structural layer says `majja` is "in the canon" and the effect-definition is "in the commentary," and stops. Coded for chronological stratum independently of layer, the picture is sharper, because both ends of the contrast carry information the layer alone does not.

### The named triad and the precept are early-canonical and pan-Nikāya

The precept formula `surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī` is not a marginal or late fixture. Searched as a compound, it returns 161 rows under the strict spelling, and its mūla attestations are dominated by the primary Nikāyas: AN 63, SN 14, DN 12, MN 7, with the Abhidhamma analyses (the Vibhaṅga Sikkhāpadavibhaṅga, the Puggalapaññatti) and the Vinaya following. The lay drawbacks frame is early-canonical prose (`dn31`, the Sigālovāda), the merit frame early-canonical (`an8.39`), the verse rebuke early-canonical (`snp2.14`, in the Cūḷavagga of the Sutta Nipāta, not the archaic Aṭṭhakavagga), and the undertaking-formula appears in the late-canonical Khuddakapāṭha compilation (`kp2`) as a stock item. The named triad is canonical bedrock, and its register is uniform: the harm is `pamāda` and its concrete consequences, stated flat, never as a substance-purity taboo (`dn31`, `snp2.14`) [canon].

> *Per-claim signature (the named triad): [stratum: early-canonical | layer: mūla | speaker: redactor-transmitted training-rule / Buddha-vacana in the sutta bodies | register: prudential-gnomic (lay) and disciplinary-legal (monastic) | epistemic: flat-background-assertion | recension: multi-recensional-pre-sectarian for the rule and the lay register | edition: cst-burmese]. Warrant: `dn31`, `sn14.25`, `an5.179`, `an8.39`, `snp2.14`.* [canon]

### The operative category-term is canonical; the effect-definition is commentarial

The decisive chronology fact is the split between the category-*term* and the category-*definition*. The Vinaya rule's own word-analysis defines `surā` by a closed list (five kinds: flour, cake, rice, yeast-mixed, compounded) and `meraya` by another closed list (five `āsava`: flower, fruit, honey, sugar, compounded), and then states the offence not on those named substances but on the general term `majja`: *Majje majjasaññī pivati, āpatti pācittiyassa ... Majje amajjasaññī pivati, āpatti pācittiyassa* ("drinking an intoxicant perceiving it as an intoxicant is an offence ... drinking an intoxicant *not* perceiving it as an intoxicant is an offence"), the perception-permutation that makes the substance, not the drinker's belief, decisive (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`) [canon]. The category-term `majja` is thus canonical and operative: the canon already treats the named substances as instances of a class.

What the canon never does is state that class as an open category by definition. The explicit definition lives in the commentary: *Majjapānāti majjaṃ vuccati madanīyaṭṭhena surā ca merayañca* ("'drinking an intoxicant': `majja` means, in the sense of being intoxicating, `surā` and `meraya`," `cst-s0506a.att-18_p049`), and, with the closed lists folded into the principle, *tadubhayampi madanīyaṭṭhena majjaṃ* ("both of those, in the sense of being intoxicating, are `majja`," `cst-s0504a.att-83_p050`) [comm]. The open boundary, the move that makes the category effect-gated rather than list-bounded, is the generalisation *yaṃ vā panaññampi surāsavavinimuttaṃ madanīyaṃ* ("whatever else there is, free of `surā` and `āsava`, that is intoxicating"), with the precept's named substances reduced to `nidassanamatta`, mere examples (`cst-s0305a.att-sn5_12_p079`, the Saṃyutta-aṭṭhakathā, quoted by the sub-commentary `cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030`) [comm].

> *Per-claim signature (the effect-definition): [stratum: classical-commentary and sub-commentary | layer: attha and tika | speaker: commentator | register: scholastic gloss | epistemic: flat-background-assertion | recension: pali-only | edition: cst-burmese]. Warrant: `cst-s0506a.att-18_p049`, `cst-s0504a.att-83_p050`, `cst-s0305a.att-sn5_12_p079`, `cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030`.* [comm]

### The effect-concept is canonically rooted, applied to sense-objects before drink

The commentary is not inventing the effect-principle from nothing; it is drawing it out of canonical material that already runs on effect. The verb and adjective of intoxication, `madanīya` ("intoxicating") and `majjati` ("one is intoxicated"), are early-canonical, but in the early Nikāyas their object is overwhelmingly the *sense-pleasures*, not liquor: *madanīye majjati* ("one is intoxicated by the intoxicating") at AN 5.81 takes the five `kāma` as its object, and the same adjective qualifies intoxicating sounds and sights at AN 5.55, AN 8.46, DN 4, DN 10, DN 17, SN 35.246. The canon's own deepest sense of intoxication is existential, the three `madā` of youth, health, and life that share the root `√mad` with `majja` (`an3.39`, systematised into a longer taxonomy in the Abhidhamma Vibhaṅga, `cst-abh02m.mul-150`) [canon]. So the effect-principle is canonical; what the commentary does is weld it onto the drink-category and state it as that category's open definition. This is a textbook drift-by-specification across strata: an early-canonical effect-vocabulary, applied to sense-objects, narrowed and fixed by the commentary into the effect-definition of `majja`.

| Stratum | The effect-principle here | Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| early-canonical | `madanīye majjati`: intoxication by the intoxicating, object = the five sense-pleasures | `an5.81` |
| early-canonical | the three `madā` (youth, health, life): intoxication as existential heedlessness, `√mad` | `an3.39` |
| early-canonical (operative) | the Vinaya rule operates on `majje`; offence regardless of perception | `pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51` |
| late-canonical (criterion) | the medicine ruling's undetectability test: liquor allowed only where it can no longer intoxicate | `cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_6` |
| abhidhamma-canonical | the systematised `madā` taxonomy | `cst-abh02m.mul-150` |
| classical-commentary | the effect-*definition*: `majjaṃ vuccati madanīyaṭṭhena ...` | `cst-s0506a.att-18_p049` |
| sub-commentary | the open category: `yaṃ vā panaññampi ... madanīyaṃ`; named substances `nidassanamatta` | `cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030` |

The honesty guard on this gradient. The split between "effect-concept (canonical)" and "effect-definition (commentarial)" is not a claim that the early canon held a *closed*-list theory the commentary overturned. The canon never states a closed theory either; it states a named precept and operates a general category-term, and the question of the category's boundary simply does not come up in canonical prose as an explicit definition. The commentary answers a question the canon leaves implicit. So the right reading is the same template-versus-verdict shape the Uttarakuru study found: the operative material is canonical and pre-sectarian; the explicit definition that makes the boundary open is a Pāli-local commentarial elaboration of it.

## The structured absence: where the open-list definition is not

The recall ladder rules out the trivial explanations: the cluster is enumerated to its purged net, so the zero is not a missed inflection, and the concept rung was run, so the principle was searched without the names. The bound, stated honestly: the silence licenses that the *explicit open definition* is commentarial; it does not license that the canon lacked the effect-principle, which it carries in the operative `majje` permutations, the medicine criterion, and the existential `madā`.

## The cross-recensional split: a pre-sectarian named prohibition, a Pāli-local open definition

The decisive antiquity question does not depend on contested Pāli-internal dating: it asks whether the load-bearing claims survive in transmissions independent of the Pāli line. The corpus bridges out through its `passage_parallels` table; the container-versus-feature distinction is kept sharp.

The named prohibition is pre-sectarian. The monastic surāpāna rule (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`) resolves to parallels across seven independent Vinaya lines: Sarvāstivāda (`san-sarv-bu-pm-tf11-pc79`), Mūlasarvāstivāda (`san-mu-bu-pm-gbm3-pc79`), Lokottaravāda-Mahāsāṃghika (`san-lo-bu-pm-pc76`), Mahāsāṃghika (`san-mg-bu-pm-pc76`), Dharmaguptaka (`lzh-dg-bu-vb-pc51`), Mahīśāsaka (`lzh-mi-bu-vb-pc57`), and Kāśyapīya (`lzh-ka-bu-pm-pc79`) [scholarship]. A rule attested across this spread of independent Vinayas plausibly predates the sectarian split: the prohibition of `surā` and `meraya`, with the operative category-term, is pre-sectarian Buddhist material, not a Theravāda peculiarity.

The lay register is also multi-recensional. The Sigālovāda drawbacks frame (`dn31`) resolves to Chinese parallels in both the Dīrgha and Madhyama collections (`da16`, `ma135`, `t16`, `t17`) and a Sanskrit fragment (`sf100`) [scholarship]. The existential-intoxication concept resolves out: the three `madā` of AN 3.39 has Madhyama and Ekottara parallels (`ma117`, `ea22.8`) [scholarship]. The effect-*concept*, in both its sense-pleasure and its existential forms, is therefore not Pāli-local.

The explicit effect-*definition* is Pāli-local by construction. It lives in the Aṭṭhakathā and Ṭīkā, which have no Āgama parallels because they are the Theravāda commentarial layer. The honesty guard matters here exactly as it did for Uttarakuru: "Pāli-local commentarial" is not "Theravāda-invented from nothing." The open principle is drawn out of canonically-operative, pre-sectarian material (the rule's `majje` permutations, the early `madanīye majjati`, the telapāka undetectability criterion); what is local is the act of stating it as the category's definition. The split is the finding.

## Verdict

The settled finding, `majja` is an effect-based category, is **confirmed and refined**. It is confirmed: the category is gated by effect, and both the precept and the rule treat the named substances as instances of a class rather than as a closed list. It is refined in two ways the single axis could not state. First, the recall ladder shows the count rests on a homograph minefield and supplies the auditable net (227 intoxicant `majja` rows, 648 liquor `surā` rows, 279 clean `meraya` rows, 840 in the deduplicated cluster), retiring the order-of-magnitude inflation of the naive substrings. Second, the chronology and cross-recension passes split "effect-based" into a canonical, pre-sectarian operative principle (the `majje` permutations, the early `madanīye majjati` vocabulary, the three `madā`, the medicine criterion) and a Pāli-local commentarial *definition* (the open-list `yaṃ vā panaññampi ... madanīyaṃ`, occurring in two rows, both commentarial, none canonical). The category is effect-based throughout; the canon operates the principle, the commentary states it as a definition.

The pre-registered R5 prediction was: "the recall re-check on the `majja`/`meraya`/`surā` homograph cluster changes a count (the known homograph trap); chronology secondary. PASS if the recall-ladder delta is non-zero or the homograph precision is re-confirmed." The prediction scores **PASS** on both legs. The homograph precision is re-confirmed and sharpened (`majja` 1,455 to 227, with 1,228 purged; `surā` 926 to 648, with 278 purged; `meraya` 279 clean; cluster net 840). The recall-ladder delta is non-zero and large: the naive-to-purged delta on `majja` is minus 1,228, a count changed by more than an order of magnitude, exactly the trap the prediction named. The one place the prediction was imprecise is that it called chronology secondary: chronology in fact produced the substantive refinement (the effect-definition is commentarial; the effect-principle canonical), so the secondary axis carried the sharper half of the finding. A prediction that is right on its PASS condition and slightly wrong on its ordering is recorded as such, a finding, not a failure.

Every count is data-bound in `build_dataset.py`, which emits the census `_census_v2.json` and gates on a consistency check (the recall-ladder arithmetic, the per-term layer sums, the commentarial-only status of the open-category rows) plus a zero-em-dash check.
- The base edition is the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana (CST/VRI) as ingested into the corpus; counts and the homograph purge are edition-relative to that segmentation and the per-paragraph commentary granularity. A different edition or segmentation would shift the absolute row counts; what is edition-neutral is the structural fact the counts stand for, that the open effect-definition is a commentarial gloss and the named prohibition is canonical and pre-sectarian.
- The recall ladder's residual risk is the boundary between the token-precise purge and any intoxicant token a tightened regex still drops; the per-term nets were reconfirmed by `GROUP BY work_role` and the `meraya` anchor (no homograph) reconciles with the prior study, which bounds the risk. The `surā` purge may over-remove a small number of rows that carry both a deva-compound and a liquor token (13 such rows were observed); this is noted rather than hidden.
- The cross-recensional codes are link-level from the corpus parallels table, not a reading of the external texts; the recensional-line labels on the linked ids are scholarly identification of the SuttaCentral ids, flagged as such. The "pre-sectarian" reading of the rule rests on the *breadth* of independent Vinaya lines that carry a surāpāna pācittiya, which is strong, but a feature-level confirmation that each external rule names the same closed lists is a further pass not run here.
- The analytic vocabulary, "category", "effect-based", "open versus closed list", "operative term versus definition", is a modern frame laid over a Pāli that says `majja`, `madanīya`, `nidassanamatta`, and `madanīyaṭṭhena`; it is named as such, not smuggled in as neutral.
