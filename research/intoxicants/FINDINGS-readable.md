# The fifth precept and the open category of the intoxicant: how the Pāli texts class what they forbid, from named drinks to whatever intoxicates

## Abstract

The fifth lay training-rule is usually read in English as "no alcohol," and pictured as a list of forbidden drinks. The Pāli names something both narrower and stranger: not a roster of substances but a category, the intoxicant, whose members are picked out by what they do rather than by what they are. This survey traces how that category is handled across the layers of the literature, from the earliest discourses to the commentaries, and how securely the claim can be counted at all. Two of the three drinking-words turn out to be near-twins in spelling for unrelated words, so a raw count is mostly noise, and the obscure wine-word proves the only clean anchor. Once the look-alikes are cleared, a pattern appears: the canon already operates a general class and treats the famous drinks as instances of it, while the sentence that states the open definition outright, "whatever else intoxicates," is the commentary's, not the canon's. The principle is canonical and old; the explicit definition is later and local; both say the same thing.

---

## I. What the fifth precept actually forbids

A lay Buddhist who takes the training rules undertakes, as the fifth of them, to abstain from drink. The natural way to read that, in English, is "no alcohol," and the natural way to picture it is a list of forbidden substances: beer, wine, spirits, the things a temperance pledge would name. The Pāli says something at once narrower and odder. The precept does not forbid a roster of drinks at all. It forbids `majja`, the intoxicant, a word that picks out its members not by what they are made of but by what they do to the one who takes them. The drinks it goes on to name, `surā` and `meraya`, grain liquor and fermented wine, sit inside that category as instances of it, not as its definition. The category is the thing; the named drinks are examples.[^1]

That much is, I think, the settled reading, and it is much repeated: the canon's class for what the fifth precept forbids is an open one, gated by effect rather than by substance. The question I want to put is not whether that reading is right (I take it to be) but where, across the layers of the literature, the texts actually say so, and how securely the claim can be counted at all. Two readings stand against each other. The one I came to favour, and will call the working hypothesis, is that the canon supplies the operative category-term and the effect-rationale piecemeal, in a precept, a rule, and a scattering of effect-vocabulary, while the explicit open definition, the sentence that says in so many words that `majja` is "whatever else intoxicates," is the commentary's. The plainer expectation, which I take as the null, is that the canon does both: that it names the substances and itself states the open effect-definition, so that the definition is canonical rather than commentarial. The pages that follow describe what a marker-bounded count actually shows. They do not prove a case. This is a triage-grade descriptive finding, a survey of how the sources class an intoxicant and where they say what they say, not a normative claim about drug ethics, and where it corrects a looser impression it does so quietly.[^2]

This survey follows the intoxicant category up through the layers of the literature, earliest to latest, watching how each layer handles it:

- **II. Three words, one category: the frame, and how it was found.** What `surā`, `meraya`, and `majja` each mean, where the precept formula sits, and a short note on how the passages behind every count below were found and cleaned.
- **III. In the early discourses.** How the earliest layer already operates a general class, treats the named drinks as instances, and runs on the language of intoxication, while never pausing to define the category's edge.
- **IV. In the late canon.** How, a layer on, the effect-principle hardens from loose vocabulary toward an operative threshold, applied to drink as a working criterion, and how the existential intoxications are gathered into a list.
- **V. In the commentaries.** Where, at the latest layer, the open definition is finally stated outright, "whatever else intoxicates," with the named drinks reduced to mere examples.
- **VI. What this means, and what it does not.** Why the shape matters for a reader who simply wants to know what the texts forbid, and the limits of saying so.

Two of the three words are spelling-traps, near-twins for unrelated words, so the counts below cannot be used naively; they are cleaned first. The cleaning note and the per-word figures sit in II's method note and in the footnotes, so the body need not open on method. With that warning lodged, the place to begin is the frame: the three words the precept actually uses, and what each one means.

## II. Three words, one category: the frame, and how it was found

The fifth precept is a single compounded clause. In the stock undertaking it reads `surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī`, which I would gloss as "refraining from liquor, wine, and intoxicants, the occasion of heedlessness."[^3] The clause names three things and then frames all three under a fourth idea, and it repays taking the four in turn, because the whole question of how the texts class what they forbid is folded into them. This is layer-neutral ground: it is about what the words mean, not yet about how any one layer handles them, and so it comes before the walk through the strata.

The three named things are these:

- **`surā`**: grain liquor, the brewed or distilled drink made from a starch base. The monastic word-analysis will go on to count five kinds of it by the grain or starch it begins from, which already tells the reader the canon was prepared to be exact about substances when it chose to be.
- **`meraya`**: fermented fruit or floral wine, the drink made by fermentation rather than from grain. It is the rarer of the two named drinks, and, as it turns out, the one word in the whole cluster that means only the one thing.
- **`majja`**: the generic intoxicant, the word the whole question turns on. Where `surā` and `meraya` name kinds of drink, `majja` names the class to which an intoxicating thing belongs by virtue of intoxicating. It is the only one of the three that is not a recipe but a property.

The fourth element is not a substance at all. The clause closes by calling the whole `pamādaṭṭhāna`, "the occasion of heedlessness," which tells the reader what is wrong with the three named things in the first place.[^4] They are forbidden not because they are impure but because they are the standing occasion of `pamāda`, the heedlessness that undoes the practice. The precept's own framing, in other words, is already an effect-framing: the harm named is what the drink does to the mind, not what the drink is. This is no marginal fixture. It runs across all four of the primary collections of discourses and recurs in the lay drawbacks-of-drink passages, in the lists of meritorious giving, in verse, and in the undertaking-formula of the later anthologies.[^5]

### How these were found

Every count in this survey rests on a measured search of a single corpus: the live canonical and commentarial collection of 194,710 passages, with the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana recension as ingested as the base edition, including its commentary at paragraph-level granularity. Two cautions govern what the counts can mean. The first is that every count is a floor, not a ceiling: the search was run from the plainest spelling of each word outward, and what a narrow search misses it misses silently, so the figures here are best read as the conservative edge of what the corpus holds.[^6] The second caution is sharper, and for two of the three words it is not a footnote to the count but the count itself: two of these three drinking-words are spelled almost exactly like unrelated words that have nothing to do with drink, so the raw counts had to be cleaned before they meant anything at all. Rather than lean on a single multi-coder agreement score, this survey is checked against itself: the cleaned counts, the per-layer sums, and the commentarial-only status of the defining phrase must all agree before any figure is reported.[^7]

That cleaning is the reason the raw counts cannot be used naively. If you simply search the corpus for the letters of the three drinking-words and gather everything that matches, you get a large number: a de-duplicated union of 2,115 passages.[^8] It looks like a solid base for a survey. It is not. Most of what it catches is not about drink at all, and the trouble is concentrated, awkwardly, in the one word that names the whole category. Two of the three words are homographs, near-twins in spelling for words of entirely different meaning, each with its own everyday life in the texts that has nothing to do with a cup.[^9] The wine-word `meraya`, alone of the three, has no common twin; nothing else in the corpus is spelled like it, and it comes back clean.[^10]

When the look-alikes are removed and counted, the result is not the one the spelling would lead you to expect. The word that looks like the whole category, `majja`, is the least reliable of the three, while the obscure wine-word is the firm anchor the whole count can be hung on.

The liquor-word `surā` is gentler but still noisy, with roughly three hits in ten falling away to gods and radiant adjectives rather than drink. And `meraya`, the word a non-specialist is least likely to know, is the one that comes back without a single false friend. There is a practical lesson buried in this, and it runs against intuition: when one wants to find the intoxicants in this corpus reliably, the word to trust is not the famous category-term but the obscure wine-word, because it is the only one of the three that means just one thing.[^12]

In sum, a raw count of these words measures the language's coincidences, not the canon's concern with drink; only after the look-alikes are subtracted does a real field of 840 passages appear. With that clean field in hand, we can ask how each layer of the literature handles the category, beginning with the earliest discourses.

## III. In the early discourses: the named drinks, the rule, and the effect-vocabulary

At the earliest layer the category is already operative. The named drinks are treated as instances, the effect-principle is already in the canon's vocabulary, and yet nothing here pauses to define the category's outer edge. The first half of that answer is plainer than the second, and it can be read directly off the way the monastic rule is built.

The rule against drinking is a Pātimokkha offence, and like the other rules of that code it carries its own word-analysis, a clause that says what each term in the rule covers.[^13] That analysis is, at first glance, a closed-list document. It enumerates five kinds of `surā` (liquor), counted by what they are brewed from, the flour kind, the cake kind, the rice kind, the yeast-mixed kind, and the compounded kind, and then five kinds of `meraya` (fermented wine), counted the same way, the flower, the fruit, the honey, the sugar, and the compounded.[^14] Ten named things, each one spelled out, with the precision of a brewer's inventory. If the rule stopped there, one could fairly say the canon forbids a list.

It does not stop there. When the rule turns from naming the substances to fixing the offence, it shifts to the general word. The offence is stated not on `surā` or `meraya` but on `majja`, the generic intoxicant, and it is stated as a set of permutations that the tradition uses to test the edges of a rule. The decisive pair runs: drinking an intoxicant while perceiving it as an intoxicant is an offence, and drinking an intoxicant while *not* perceiving it as an intoxicant is also an offence.[^15] The drinker who is mistaken about what is in his cup is caught exactly as the drinker who knows. What this turns on repays dwelling on, because it is the cleanest demonstration the corpus offers that the canon is already thinking in a category rather than in a list.

### A worked case: the drinker who is mistaken

Imagine the monk the rule has in view. He drinks something that is in fact an intoxicant, but he believes it is not one, perhaps a medicinal decoction he takes to be inert, perhaps a drink he has been told is harmless, perhaps something handed to him without explanation. He is wrong about the substance. On a roster theory of the rule, the natural verdict would track his knowledge: if the offence is "drinking one of these ten named things knowingly," a man who did not know what he drank might be expected to fall outside it, or into some lighter category of the unwitting, the way a mistaken act often draws a lighter judgement elsewhere in the disciplinary code. The rule gives the opposite verdict. He has committed the full offence, because what the rule catches is not his belief about the drink but the drink's own property of intoxicating.[^16] The substance decides; the perception does not.

That verdict is only possible if the rule is organised around a class, the class of things that intoxicate, and not around a checklist of named brews. A checklist would have to ask, each time, whether the thing drunk is on the list, and the drinker's grasp of the list would matter. A class defined by effect asks only whether the thing intoxicates, and the drinker's grasp of that is beside the point. So the canon, in the very rule that supplies the closed ten-item enumeration, operates a general term that the enumeration cannot exhaust.

The tradition remembered a particular occasion for this rule, and it is worth letting the scene stand for a moment, because it shows the same logic in narrative form. The elder Sāgata, a monk of great attainment, came to a town called Kosambī and was offered drink by householders who meant only to honour him. He took it, in the way one accepts an ordinary courtesy, and was found afterward lying senseless at the town gate, his head pointed away from the Buddha, his powers undone by the cup. The story is told not to shame Sāgata so much as to fix the point the rule then states flatly: even a practitioner of standing, acting without ill intent, is brought low by the substance itself.[^17] The narrative and the legal permutations say one thing between them. The danger is in the drink's effect, not in the drinker's plan. The offence, the same analysis notes, is incurred at a quantity as small as the tip of a blade of grass dipped in liquor, which is again a statement about a property crossing a threshold, not about a named drink being consumed in any particular measure.[^18]

### The same shape across the lay registers

What the monastic rule does in legal form, the lay material does in several registers at once, and in each the named triad appears as the standing content of the precept rather than as an exhaustive inventory of forbidden things. The formula `surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī`, "refraining from liquor, wine, and intoxicants, the occasion of heedlessness," is not a marginal or late fixture but a robust canonical one spread across all four primary collections.[^19] It surfaces in framings that repay separating, because together they show the precept doing different jobs without ever closing its list:

- **The drawbacks frame.** The lay discourse on a householder's duties counts drink first among the gateways to ruin and sets out its concrete harms in a flat, prudential list: present loss of wealth, the breeding of quarrels, a nest of illnesses, a name brought into disrepute, the indecent exposure of the body, and a wits-weakening that dulls the mind. The register is worldly through and through. The harm named is `pamāda`, heedlessness, and its plain consequences, never a notion of substance-impurity.[^20]
- **The merit frame.** Abstaining from liquor and wine is listed among the great gifts a person can give, ranked with the gift of safety to all beings, because the one who keeps the precept becomes a person from whom no one need fear drunken harm. This casts the precept as a positive act of generosity, something given outward, rather than a taboo to be policed inward.[^21]

Beneath the named drinks, the canon already runs on the language of intoxication, and it applies that language first to things other than drink. The adjective `madanīya`, "intoxicating," and the verb `majjati`, "one is intoxicated," are turned first on the senses. The phrase `madanīye majjati`, "one is intoxicated by the intoxicating," takes as its object the five strands of sense-pleasure, the seen, the heard, the smelled, the tasted, the touched, and not drink at all; a person is drunk on what the eye and ear bring in. The same adjective qualifies intoxicating sounds and intoxicating sights across a spread of discourses, so that the texts treat a captivating form as a thing one can be inebriated by in just the way wine inebriates.[^24]

The canon's deepest sense of intoxication is not chemical at all but existential. It names three `madā`, three intoxications that need no cup: the intoxication of youth, by which the young forget that age is coming; the intoxication of health, by which the well forget that sickness waits; and the intoxication of life, by which the living forget that they will die. Each lulls a person into heedlessness as surely as any drink, and each shares its very root with the word for an intoxicant, so that the canon is, in effect, calling youth and health and life things that go to the head.[^25]

Across the monastic rule, the lay frames, and the effect-vocabulary alike, the pattern holds. The texts name `surā`, `meraya`, and `majja`; they frame the whole as the occasion of heedlessness; and they operate the general term, and the verb beneath it, as the thing that actually decides. Nowhere in this early material, though, does a sentence stop to say what the general term *means*, to draw the boundary of the category as an explicit definition. The category is everywhere in use and nowhere defined.

In sum, at the earliest layer the canon already runs on a category, treats the named drinks as instances of it, and carries the effect-idea in its very vocabulary, yet never pauses to state the category's outer edge. The next layers show the principle hardening, from loose vocabulary toward a rule and a working criterion; to the late canon I now turn.

## IV. In the late canon: the principle hardens into a criterion

A layer on, the effect-idea moves from loose vocabulary toward an operative threshold. The principle that the early discourses carried in a verb and an image is now applied to drink as a working test, the moralising of drink begins to gather, and the existential intoxications are systematised into a list. So when the disciplinary literature reaches for an effect-test, it is not improvising; it is applying to drink a criterion the canon already used for the mind.

The medicine ruling makes this explicit. Liquor is permitted in a medicinal decoction, where a sick monk needs it, but only under a condition that is itself an effect-test: it may be used only where the colour, the smell, and the taste of the liquor are no longer detectable, which is to say only where it can no longer intoxicate. The same substance that is forbidden as a drink is allowed as a medicine precisely at the point where it stops doing the thing the precept forbids.[^27] The criterion that governs the exception is the same criterion the later definition will name, the capacity to intoxicate, here applied as a working threshold rather than stated as a boundary. This is the first time the effect-criterion is brought to bear on drink itself as a usable test, and not merely on the senses or on youth and health.

Two further late items show the same drift. The first is the undertaking-formula. The same fifth item appears as a stock training-rule a layperson recites, in the late-canonical handbook of such formulae, worn smooth by liturgical use until it is less an argument than a thing one simply says.[^23] The second is a verse rebuke. A verse discourse names drink an `apuññāyatana`, a seat of demerit, and warns against the `ummādana`, the maddening, of it, the way it unseats the mind. The placement matters: this sits in the later layer of the verse collection, not its archaic core, which is one early sign of where the explicit moralising of drink tends to gather.[^22]

The existential sense of intoxication, carried in the early discourses as the three `madā`, is at this layer gathered and systematised in the Abhidhamma analysis into a longer taxonomy of such intoxications, the scholastic layer doing in a list what the discourses had done in an image.[^26]

In sum, the canon has now applied the effect-test to drink as a working criterion and systematised the existential intoxications into a list, and the moralising of drink has begun to gather in the later verse. Yet across the whole canonical span no sentence states the category's boundary outright. That step waits for the commentators, and to them I now turn.

## V. In the commentaries: "whatever else intoxicates" stated outright

At the latest layer the boundary the canon had left implicit is finally drawn in words. The sentence everyone quotes when they say the Pāli tradition defines intoxicants by effect rather than by substance is real, and it is faithful, and it is not in the canon. It is the commentary's sentence.

### A worked case: the phrase that opens the list

The keystone is a single move, made in the commentary, and it repays quoting because so much rests on it. Glossing the drink-precept, the Khuddakapāṭha commentary writes that `majja` means, "in the sense of being intoxicating," `surā` and `meraya`, and elsewhere that both of those, "in the sense of being intoxicating," are `majja`.[^28] So far this is exposition: it names the two canonical drinks and says what makes them count, which is exactly the effect the precept itself had pointed at. The decisive step comes in the Saṃyutta commentary, where the boundary is thrown open. After naming the standard substances, the gloss adds: "whatever else there is, free of liquor and ferment, that is intoxicating," and in doing so reduces the precept's own named drinks to `nidassanamatta`, "mere examples." The two famous brews, which the precept had put first, are demoted from the content of the rule to illustrations of it. The category is declared open, and effect is named, explicitly and as a definition, as the thing that fills it. The sub-commentary then quotes the open-list row in turn, carrying it forward into the latest layer of all.[^29]

That is the sentence readers cite. What the count adds is its rarity and its placement. The explicit open-category move, the phrase that says "whatever else intoxicates" and calls the named drinks mere examples, occurs in exactly two rows of the whole corpus, and both are commentarial; it appears in no canonical row at all.[^30]

> **> 2**: the number of passages anywhere in the corpus that state the open "whatever intoxicates" definition. Both are commentary; none is canonical.

The effect-idea, then, runs the whole height of the literature, but only the topmost layer states it as a definition. Laid out from early to late, the pieces read as a single idea coming gradually into focus, and the gradient is the synthesis the whole arc has been building toward.

The filled dots are the canon operating the principle without ever pausing to define it. The two open dots, both at the far late end, are the commentary doing the pausing.

I want to be careful about what that two-against-zero split does and does not mean, because it is easy to over-read. It does not mean the early canon held a *closed*-list theory that the commentary then overturned. The canon states no closed theory either. It names a precept and operates a general term, and the question of the category's outer boundary simply does not arise, in canonical prose, as something to be settled in a definition. The commentary answers a question the canon had left implicit. What is canonical and, on the evidence of the parallels, pre-sectarian is the named prohibition and the operative term beneath it.[^31] What is local to the Pāli commentarial layer is the act of stating the boundary as a definition. The named rule travels across the recensions; the open definition does not, because it belongs to a layer the other traditions do not share.[^32] The effect-principle, in both its sense-pleasure and its existential forms, is itself not Pāli-local; it has parallels in the other collections.[^33] So the right way to put it, it seems to me, is that the canon runs on the effect-principle throughout, and the commentary's contribution is not the principle but its promotion to a definition.

In sum, the famous open definition is the commentary's, and its scarcity (two rows against none in the canon) is exactly what one would expect if the commentators were stating outright a boundary the canon had been content to leave implicit. What that split means for a reader who simply wants to know what the texts forbid, and what it cannot be stretched to mean, is the last thing to settle.

## VI. What this means, and what it does not

If someone asks the plain question, does Buddhism forbid alcohol, the texts give an answer that is both narrower and more interesting than a yes. They forbid `surā` and `meraya` by name, and they frame the prohibition around `majja`, the intoxicant as such, the occasion of heedlessness. The thing the rule actually catches is not a named brew but a property, the capacity to intoxicate, and the canon shows this most clearly where it convicts the drinker who did not know what he drank. The famous open phrasing, "whatever else there is that is intoxicating," with the named drinks called mere examples, is faithful to that canonical habit, but it is the commentary's sentence, not the canon's. The commentary is drawing out, as a definition, a principle the canon had only operated. For a reader that is worth knowing before reaching for the line as if it were the Buddha's own ruling: the principle is canonical and old, the definition is later and local, and both are saying the same thing.

Several limits bound how far that reading can be pressed, and they are better stated plainly than smoothed over.

- The counts are relative to one edition. They rest on the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana text as ingested, with the commentary divided into paragraph-sized passages; a different edition or a different segmentation would shift the absolute row figures. What is edition-neutral is the structural fact the figures stand for, that the open definition is a commentarial gloss while the named prohibition is canonical.[^34]
- Every count is a floor. The look-alike cleanup that makes the figures auditable also carries a residual risk at its own edge, the boundary between the cleaned set and any genuine intoxicant token a tightened filter still drops. The `surā` cleanup in particular may have over-removed a small handful of rows, around thirteen, that carry both a god-compound and a liquor word in the same passage; this is noted rather than hidden.[^35]
- The reach into other textual traditions is a matter of whether a parallel exists, not of reading those parallels line by line. The claim that the named rule is pre-sectarian rests on the breadth of independent monastic codes that carry a drinking offence, which is strong, but a feature-by-feature confirmation that each external rule names the same lists is a further task not undertaken here.[^36]
- The analytic vocabulary is a modern frame. "Category," "effect-based," "open versus closed list," "operative term versus definition," these are my words laid over a Pāli that says `majja`, `madanīya`, `nidassanamatta`, and `madanīyaṭṭhena`. I have tried to name the frame as a frame rather than pass it off as the texts' own.[^37]
- This is a measured, triage-grade description of how the sources class what they forbid, not a proof and not a drug-ethics ruling. Where an earlier reading extended the effect-principle to substances the canon never names, that was interpretation, flagged as such, and I have not re-argued it here.[^38]

What the data cannot do is say why the shape is as it is, why the canon was content to operate a category it never paused to define, and why it fell to the commentators to draw the boundary. One can only guess, and a guess is not a finding. It may be that a living community had no need of a definition: when a question arose, a teacher decided it, and the named drinks plus a shared sense of what intoxicates were enough to go on. A definition is the kind of thing one writes down when the teacher is no longer in the room to ask, when a later reader, at a distance from the practice, needs the boundary fixed in words because it can no longer be fixed in person. On that reading the commentary's open sentence is less an innovation than a transcription, the moment a tacit competence was forced to become an explicit rule. Whatever the reason, it seems to me that to ask how the texts class what the fifth precept forbids is already to find a tradition more interested in the effect than in the substance, naming a few drinks because those are the drinks at hand, and reaching, when pressed, for the wider word. The named drinks are the examples; the intoxicating is the point. The commentary said as much in a single line, and the canon, in its own quieter way, had been saying it all along, in the verb it used for being lost to the senses: `madanīye majjati`, one is intoxicated by the intoxicating.[^39]

## Appendix B: Method register

**The recall ladder.** Every count rests on a measured enumeration of the three-word cluster, two of which are look-alike traps, so the ladder is the methodological heart of the survey. It was climbed in four rungs; the yields measure the surface string, not the final cluster, which settles at the purged net of 840 rows.

| Rung | Search strategy | Yield |
|---|---|---:|
| 1. naive surface | The plainest substring for each word (`majja`, `surā`, `meraya`), unioned | union 2,115 (`majja` 1,455, `surā` 926, `meraya` 279) |
| 2. morphological | The distinct declined and compound forms per stem (`surāmeraya-`, `-majjapāna-`, `surāya`, `merayassa`, the precept compound) | union 2,115 (no new rows: the diacritic-complete substring already holds every inflection) |
| 3. concept / periphrasis | The effect-principle without the names (`madanīy`, `pamādaṭṭhāna`, the effect-definition phrase `madanīyaṭṭhena`, the open-list `yaṃ vā panaññampi ... madanīyaṃ`, the three `madā`) | grounds the effect claim on rationale, not name proximity; the open-list rows are the keystone (2 rows) |
| 4. homograph purge | Intoxicant-sense tokens only, the look-alikes subtracted and counted, reconfirmed by a group-by over the layer column | cluster net 840 (mūla 273, attha 294, tika 206, anya 67); `majja` minus 1,228, `surā` minus 278, `meraya` minus 0 |

The dominant recovery is rung 4, the purge: the naive `majja` substring is more than four-fifths false-positive, the `surā` substring roughly three-tenths noise, and `meraya` clean. Because the base edition's diacritic-complete spelling already holds every inflection of these words, rung 2 added no new rows; the floor caution carries forward for any intoxicant token a tightened filter still drops.

**The triage note.** This is a category-typology question (what the texts class as `majja`, and by what criterion), so the recall ladder was load-bearing rather than a background discipline, the homograph trap being the central technical object; the chronological-stratum pass and the structured-absence check (the canonical zero for the open definition) were also load-bearing. The cross-recensional reach was carried because the antiquity of the named rule rests on whether it is pre-sectarian. The register dimension was noted lightly: the lay precept is prudential-gnomic, the monastic rule disciplinary-legal, the commentarial gloss scholastic. The epistemic status was uniform across every load-bearing claim (each is stated declaratively, none under the canon's verification formula), so it is reported as a single fact rather than developed. Dimensions not carried: attribution (the load-bearing claims are stock formula and disciplinary ruling, no claim turns on a personal assertion); pre-Buddhist provenance (the question is the Buddhist category's boundary, not whether intoxication-ethics is inherited); harmonization (the tradition states the definition without reconciling apparatus).

**Pre-registration.** The hypotheses, the null, and the prediction were frozen before coding (2026-06; see the study's pre-registration record). The working hypothesis was that the canon supplies the operative category-term and the effect-rationale piecemeal while the commentary states the open effect-principle as a definition; the null was that the canon both names the substances and states the open definition itself. The shape found matches the working hypothesis: the open-list definition occurs in two commentarial rows and zero canonical rows, while the effect-principle is canonically operative across several strata. The pre-registered prediction anticipated that the recall re-check on the homograph cluster would change a count; the homograph precision was re-confirmed and the naive-to-purged delta on `majja` is large (minus 1,228). The one imprecision worth recording: the prediction called the chronology pass secondary, but it carried the sharper half of the finding (that the explicit definition is commentarial while the principle is canonical).

## Appendix C: The consistency check

No multi-coder inter-annotator pass was run for this survey; it is single-annotator work. In place of an inter-coder agreement score (κ), the survey is held to a data-bound consistency check: before any count is reported, three things are verified to agree with one another.

- **The cleaned-count arithmetic.** The per-term intoxicant nets (`majja` 227, `surā` 648, `meraya` 279) and their purge deltas (minus 1,228, minus 278, minus 0) must reconcile against the raw substring counts and against the de-duplicated cluster net of 840, given the overlap from compound passages that carry more than one word.
- **The per-layer sums.** The cluster's split by structural layer (mūla 273, aṭṭhakathā 294, ṭīkā 206, extra-canonical 67) must sum to 840 and must reproduce under a direct group-by over the layer column, not only under the search lane.
- **The commentarial-only status of the open-category phrase.** The open-list generalisation ("whatever else intoxicates," with the named drinks called mere examples) must resolve to exactly two rows, both commentarial, and to zero canonical rows, with the bare effect-definition phrase `madanīyaṭṭhena` densely present in the commentary and absent from the four-Nikāya mūla material, so the canonical zero reads as a patterned boundary rather than a gap in the count.

The single load-bearing adjudication is the homograph boundary: which substring hits carry the intoxicant sense and which are the look-alikes (`majjha`, `majjana`, `miñja`, the elder Majjaka; the deva-compounds and radiant adjectives behind `surā`). The clean `meraya` anchor, with no look-alike, reconciles with a prior independent count (283 passages against 279 rows here, the gap being occurrences versus rows), which bounds the residual risk at the `surā` edge noted in §VI. The check is enforced by the data-bound builder (`build_dataset.py`), which emits the census and fails if the three conditions, plus a zero-em-dash check, do not all pass.
