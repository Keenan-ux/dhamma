# The Buddha on Drugs and Alcohol — Findings

A study run against the live Dhamma corpus (dhamma.fly.dev, 194,710 passages) plus targeted
web scholarship for the questions the canon cannot answer from inside itself. Citations below
were re-fetched and checked verbatim at the source after the run (see
[HANDOFF.md](HANDOFF.md) for the verification ledger and the tool-evaluation track).

Confidence tags: **[canon]** = verified verbatim in-corpus; **[comm]** = commentarial
(Aṭṭhakathā/Ṭīkā), verified; **[scholarship]** = external, contested, attributed;
**[extension]** = our interpretive synthesis, flagged as such, not a canonical ruling.

---

## 0. The short version

- The canon's category is not "alcohol" but **`majja`** — *whatever intoxicates*. It is defined
  by **effect**, not by a named list. The fifth precept names `surā` (grain liquor) and `meraya`
  (fruit/floral wine) as **examples**, then generalises: `majja` = "whatever else there is, by
  drinking which one becomes intoxicated and heedless." **[canon/comm]**
- The thing condemned is **`pamāda` (heedlessness)**, not the liquid as a ritual impurity. The
  register is *unwholesome / a field of demerit / six concrete harms* — not a free-standing
  "sin." **[canon]**
- Alcohol **was** permitted medicinally, but only on a precise condition that is itself the
  effect-test: liquor cooked into a medicine **where its colour, smell and taste are no longer
  detectable**. As an intoxicating *drink*, in any quantity, it is always out. **[canon]**
- The canon has **no concept of psychedelics**. Mushrooms appear only as food. A psychedelic is
  unnamed but, by the `majja` effect-test, falls under the same principle. **[canon + extension]**
- **Vedic Soma is essentially absent** from the Pāli canon as a substance; "soma" there is the
  moon, a nun, a queen, a king. Its only ritual footprint is the sacrifice-name `vājapeyya`,
  which a modern translator renders "soma drinking." What soma *was* has no scholarly consensus
  (Ephedra leads; fly-agaric and Syrian rue are the famous rivals; many hold the plant is lost).
  **[canon + scholarship]**
- A drug "ego-death" can **resemble** the not-self characteristic but is not **anattā** in the
  sense that liberates: it is a transient, conditioned *state*, not the uprooting of the "I am"
  conceit (`asmimāna`); and it arises in clouded cognition, degrading the very mindfulness the
  insight is built on — which is exactly what the precept guards. **[canon + scholarship]**

## 1. Every instance / which intoxicants are condemned, and in what moral register

**The drinks named.** The stock formula is `surā-meraya-majja-pamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī`,
"refraining from the occasion of heedlessness that is `surā`, `meraya`, and `majja`." The
dictionaries fix the referents precisely **[canon, DPD/PED]**:
- **`surā`** — "(distilled) grain liquor; spirits" (√su; example given by DPD is literally
  `surāmerayapāne pācittiyaṃ`).
- **`meraya`** — "fermented fruit/floral wine" (Skt *maireya*). The commentary (Vv-a 73) lists
  five kinds: flower-, fruit-, honey-, sugar-, and compounded ferments.
- **`majja`** — "intoxicant; **drug**; alcohol" (√mad → Skt *madya*); the generic category. The
  dictionary's own example sentence for `majja` *is* the fifth precept.

**Where it appears.** The formula recurs as a fixed compound across the canon — the undertaking
formula (`kp2`), the five-precept analyses (`sn14.25` Pañcasikkhāpada; the Abhidhamma `VB 14`
Sikkhāpadavibhaṅga, quoted verbatim), the layperson's training (`an5.179` Gihī), the merit
framing (`an8.39`), the peyyāla titled literally "Beer and Wine" (`sn56.64` Surāmerayasutta),
the lay-ethics discourse (`dn31` Sigālovāda), and the verse rebuke (`snp2.14` Dhammika). *An
exhaustive count is not reliable from the tool* — the search term `surā` collides with `sura`
(deva) and `majja` with `majjhima` (middle), inflating concordance counts by >10× (see HANDOFF
P1/P6). The clean anchor is `meraya` (675 occurrences / 283 passages), which has no homograph.

**The moral register is `pamāda`, not "sin."** The condemnation is consistently consequential
and psychological, never a purity-taboo on the liquid **[canon]**:
- `dn31` (Sigālovāda) gives the **six drawbacks** (`cha ādīnavā`) of habitual drinking:
  `sandiṭṭhikā dhanajāni` (immediate loss of wealth), `kalahappavaḍḍhanī` (breeds quarrels),
  `rogānaṁ āyatanaṁ` (a basis of disease), `akittisañjananī` (disrepute), `kopīnanidaṁsanī`
  (indecent exposure), and `paññāya dubbalikaraṇī` (**weakening of wisdom** — "the sixth thing").
  Drinking is also listed **first** among the six `apāyamukha`, "drains on wealth."
- `snp2.14` (Dhammika): a householder should not drink, "knowing it **ends in intoxication /
  madness** (`ummādanantaṁ`)"; drink is an `apuññāyatana`, "a field of demerit."
- `an5.179`: the drinks `confuse the mind` (`cittamohaniṁ`).
- The flip side is positive: `an8.39` makes *abstaining* from `surāmeraya` one of five **great
  gifts** (`mahādāna`) that give "freedom from fear, enmity, and ill will" to countless beings —
  ethics framed as safety given to others, not self-purification.

So "which did he deem immoral?" — **all intoxicating drink, under the single heading `majja`,**
and the immorality is located in heedlessness and its harms, not in the substance as such.

## 2. The "smaller things": tea, betel, and the deeper sense of intoxication

**The category is open and effect-gated — this is the hinge of the whole study.** The
sub-commentary (`cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030`, quoting the Saṃyutta- and Khuddakapāṭha-aṭṭhakathā)
gives the canonical definition **[comm, verified]**:

> *"…tadubhayameva madanīyaṭṭhena majjaṃ, yaṃ vā panaññampi kiñci atthi madanīyaṃ, yena pītena
> matto hoti pamatto, idaṃ vuccati majjaṃ"* — both [surā and meraya] are `majja` in the sense of
> being intoxicating, **and whatever else there is that is intoxicating, by drinking which one
> becomes intoxicated and heedless — that is called `majja`.** And `surā` "is merely an example"
> (`nidassanamatta`).

So the precept is not a closed list. Anything that intoxicates → heedlessness is in scope; a
stimulant that does not is not. Consequences for the operator's "smaller things":
- **Tea** — a clean negative. `exact/translation "tea"` returns **0** results. Tea (*Camellia
  sinensis*) is East-Asian and post-canonical; it simply is not in this corpus, and on the
  effect-test it would not be an intoxicant regardless. **[canon, confirmed absent]**
- **Betel / areca (`tāmbūla`)** — not cleanly confirmable this session (the `exact/original`
  query timed out on the tool's known latency hotspot). On the principle, a mild stimulant
  chewed for the mouth is not `madanīya` and so falls outside the precept — the standard reading
  — but I am flagging this as **unverified in-corpus** rather than asserting it. **[gap]**
- **The deepest sense of "intoxication" is not chemical at all.** `an3.39` (Sukhumāla) names the
  **three intoxications** (`tayo madā`): `yobbana-mada`, `ārogya-mada`, `jīvita-mada` — the
  intoxication of **youth, health, and life**. Same root √mad as `majja`. The canon's gravest
  "drunkenness" is the heedless intoxication with being young, well, and alive. **[canon, verified]**

## 3. Was alcohol *ever* appropriate as medicine — in any quantity? Tinctures?

**Yes, on a precise condition — and the condition is the effect-test again.** The
Bhesajjakkhandhaka (Mahāvagga VI; `cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_6`) carries the ruling **verbatim
[canon, re-verified at source]**. When a medicinal oil-decoction (`telapāka`) is cooked for the
monk Pilindavaccha's wind-ailment:

> *"Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, telapāke majjaṃ pakkhipitunti"* — "I allow liquor to be put into the
> oil-decoction."

Then, after some monks brew it too strong and get drunk, the allowance is **restricted**:

> *"Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, yasmiṃ telapāke majjassa **na vaṇṇo na gandho na raso paññāyati**,
> evarūpaṃ majjapakkhittaṃ telaṃ pātunti"* — "I allow oil into which liquor has been put to be
> drunk, **provided the colour, smell, and taste of the liquor are not detectable.**"

Over-strength batches (`atipakkhittamajja`) are forbidden to drink and **re-designated for
external rubbing** (`abbhañjana`). So the canonical "tincture" logic is exact: medicinal alcohol
is permitted **only at the point where it no longer functions or registers as an intoxicant.**
The moment it can intoxicate, it is back under the prohibition. There is no permitted *drinking*
of `surā`/`meraya` for its effect, in any quantity — the monastic rule is absolute to the
"blade-of-grass tip" (§4).

Adjacent allowances in the same chapter, none of them intoxicating-as-such **[canon, verified]**:
- The **five basic medicines**: ghee, butter, oil, honey, molasses
  (`sappi, navanītaṃ, telaṃ, madhuṃ, phāṇitaṃ`).
- **`loṇasovīraka`** — a fermented sour brew — allowed to the sick freely, to the healthy only
  diluted with water. The sub-commentary (`cst-s0105t.nrf-75_p005`) draws the line explicitly:
  the offence is *"`Surāpānamevāti majjalakkhaṇappattāya surāya pānameva`"* — drinking `surā`
  that **has reached the defining mark of an intoxicant** (with `meraya` included), whereas
  `sovīraka` is merely `kañjika`, a sour gruel. **[comm, verified]**
- **`guḷa`** (molasses) — `yathāsukhaṃ guḷaṃ paribhuñjituṃ`, freely.

**On Ayurvedic tinctures (`āsava` / `ariṭṭha`):** the classic medicinal alcohol-extracts of
later Indian medicine are **not** found as named allowed medicines in this Pāli Vinaya corpus
(the word `āsava` in-corpus is dominated by its doctrinal sense, "mental taint"). They are a
later tradition; the canon's category is the `telapāka` allowance plus non-intoxicating ferments
like `sovīraka`. **[canon, negative finding]**

## 4. Psychedelics, psilocybin, mushrooms

**The canon has no entheogen category.** There is no Pāli word for "psychedelic," no discourse
on a vision-inducing plant or potion. The only mushroom material is culinary/botanical
**[canon/comm, verified]**:
- `ahicchattaka` ("snake-parasol," from the cap's resemblance to a cobra's hood) appears only in
  Vinaya commentary under the rule against destroying plant life — the sole question is whether
  picking it from a *living* tree damages the tree. It is food.
- `sūkaramaddava`, the Buddha's last meal (`dn16`), is debated by the commentaries as soft pork,
  a milk-rice dish, bamboo-shoot, a *rasāyana* elixir, or **an `ahicchattaka` mushroom grown
  where pigs trampled** — a dispute purely about *diet*, never about a vision.

**The principled extension (flagged as ours, not the canon's):** because `majja` is defined by
**effect** (§2) — `madanīya`, "by drinking which one becomes intoxicated and heedless" — a
mind-altering psychedelic, though entirely unnamed and unanticipated, falls under the *same
principle*: it clouds `sati` and induces `pamāda`. The canon never contemplates psychedelics, so
this is an inference from the effect-test, not an explicit ruling. **[extension]**

**Were psilocybin mushrooms physically around? Almost certainly — but there is no evidence anyone
used them.** Two distinct claims, kept apart **[scholarship, attributed]**:
- *Physical presence — well supported.* Coprophilic (dung-loving) *Psilocybe* species, including
  *P. cubensis* and *P. coprophila*, are documented growing on cattle/buffalo dung in India today
  (Punjab, Kerala, Odisha); the subcontinent's warm, monsoon, cattle-grazing ecology is exactly
  their habitat. So your intuition — that dung-loving psychedelic mushrooms would have been
  present around large herbivores in Iron-Age South Asia — is **biogeographically reasonable**
  (an ecological inference, not a dated find).
- *Documented ritual use — essentially absent.* There is no solid archaeological or textual
  evidence of psilocybin-mushroom use in ancient India, in sharp contrast to the rich, dated
  Mesoamerican record. The one dedicated review (Winkelman, Kuhar et al., *"Does India have
  entheomycology traditions?"*, IJTK 2022) is framed as a **call to research** precisely because
  the evidence is suggestive-at-best. The honest statement: the mushrooms plausibly grew there;
  nobody left a record of using them.

## 5. Soma — his take, and what soma actually is

**In the Pāli canon, Vedic Soma-as-substance is essentially absent.** "Soma" in the corpus is
overwhelmingly a homograph **[canon, verified]**:
- **Somā Therī**, the nun who rebuts Māra's "two-inch wisdom" taunt (`sn5.2`, `thig3.8`);
- **Queen Somā**, sister of Pasenadi's consort (`mn90`);
- **King Soma**, a class of deva, and **the moon** (`soma`/`canda`) in verse.

The *only* footprint of the soma-ritual is the sacrifice-name **`vājapeyya`** inside the stock
five-sacrifice list (`assamedha, purisamedha, sammāpāsa, vājapeyya, niraggaḷa`) at `sn3.9` /
`an4.39` — which Bhante Sujato renders **"the royal soma drinking."** Note carefully: the Pāli
word `soma` never appears there. "Soma" is the **translator's gloss** of `vājapeyya`, not
canonical vocabulary — one of the more interesting philological facts the corpus surfaces, and
exactly the kind the tool currently makes hard to see (HANDOFF F9). The Buddha engages the
brahmanical ritual world not through soma but through his **critique of bloody sacrifice**:
`sn3.9` (Yaññasutta) condemns Pasenadi's mass animal-sacrifice as violent and fruitless, and
`dn5` (Kūṭadanta) reconstructs the "great sacrifice" as a bloodless, slaughter-free almsgiving.
He post-dates the high soma cult; it is simply not his interlocutor.

**What soma *was* (best current scholarship — no consensus) [scholarship, contested]:**
- **Ephedra** is the leading academic candidate (Harry Falk, *BSOAS* 1989; Jan Houben 2003): the
  texts describe a *stimulant* enhancing alertness, not a visionary hallucinogen, and living
  Zoroastrians still use ephedra (`hum`/`homa`) for the cognate Iranian *haoma*.
- **Amanita muscaria** (fly agaric) is the famous Wasson hypothesis (1968, with Wendy Doniger),
  arguing from the Ṛgveda's omission of leaves/roots and Siberian shamanic parallels — sharply
  contested (John Brough 1971: the effects described fit a stimulant, not the deliriant fly agaric).
- **Peganum harmala** (Syrian rue, an MAO-inhibitor) — Flattery & Schwartz 1989; criticised
  because its nausea/delirium conflict with the blissful hymns.
- **Cannabis** — minority, speculative.
- A strong scholarly strand holds the **original plant is simply lost** — left behind in colder
  habitats during the migration into India, or its identity guarded into oblivion. The much-cited
  BMAC "ephedra+poppy+cannabis residue" archaeology was **not** confirmed on re-analysis.
- Lexically, Monier-Williams does carry it: `soma … the juice of the śoma plant, (also) the plant
  itself`; DPD lists a Pāli sense `soma plant; soma extract; soma sacrifice` — *the headword
  exists, but the canon's text never uses it that way.* **[verified]**

So: **his take on soma** ≈ none directly; the soma-*sacrifice* belongs to a ritual world he
critiqued wholesale. **What soma is** ≈ unsettled, with a sober-stimulant Ephedra reading now
ahead of the romantic entheogen readings.

## 6. Ego-death vs anattā — and could a psychedelic ever aid the path?

This is the heart of your question, and the corpus gives a clean four-step scaffold for the
distinction between a drug-induced dissolution of the felt self and `anattā` as the liberating
insight.

**(1) `Anattā` is an analytic insight, not a felt state.** In the Anattalakkhaṇasutta (`sn22.59`,
the second discourse) each of the five aggregates is declared not-self on two **impersonal**
grounds — they are *not amenable to control* ("you cannot compel form: 'may my form be thus'")
and they are *impermanent, suffering, perishable* — and are therefore unfit to be regarded as
`etaṁ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā` ("this is mine, I am this, this is my self"). It is seen
*with right understanding* (`sammappaññā`); it is not the sensation of one's ego dissolving.
**[canon, verified]**

**(2) The decisive line is `asmimāna`, the "I am" conceit.** The Khemaka Sutta (`sn22.89`) is the
keystone: a noble disciple who has *already discarded self-view* (`sakkāyadiṭṭhi`) and the five
lower fetters **still has a residual conceit, desire, and underlying tendency "I am"** (`asmī'ti`)
that "has not been eradicated" — and it is uprooted only by sustained contemplation of the
aggregates' rise and fall (the launderer simile: the lingering scent removed only by further
treatment). `asmimāna` is an `anusaya`, a deep latent tendency. **A drug state can suspend the
sense of self; it cannot reach the `anusaya`.** This is the single most important contrast in the
whole comparison. **[canon, verified]**

**(3) Any profound *produced* state is itself conditioned, an object to see — not a refuge.**
The Aṭṭhakanāgara Sutta (`mn52`) has the meditator reflect of every `jhāna` and every formless
attainment: it is `abhisaṅkhataṁ abhisañcetayitaṁ` ("produced by choices, intended"), and
"whatever is produced and intended is impermanent, of a nature to cease" (`aniccaṁ
nirodhadhammaṁ`). A drug-occasioned state is, on this logic, exactly a `saṅkhata` event — one
*more* impermanent phenomenon to be seen as not-self, not a doorway out. **[canon, verified]**

**(4) The canonical precedent that a sublime altered state is not awakening.** In the
Ariyapariyesanā / Pāsarāsi Sutta (`mn26`) the Buddha *attains* the formless bases under Āḷāra
Kālāma (the base of nothingness) and Uddaka Rāmaputta (neither-perception-nor-non-perception) —
the deepest non-ordinary states the meditation of his day could reach — and **rejects them**: they
do not lead to disenchantment, dispassion, cessation, awakening. The most refined consciousness-
alteration available was personally tested and set aside. **[canon; passage exists and is correctly
cited — its specific rejection clauses were not byte-verified this session, held at medium
confidence, see HANDOFF C7]**

**Putting it to your question.** *If a psychedelic can occasion an experience of the
characteristics, why is it precluded, and could it not aid the path?*

The honest answer is two-sided, and the literature is explicit that this is interpretive, not
settled **[scholarship, attributed]**:

- **The case that it could help.** The resemblance is not merely verbal. Psychedelics suppress the
  brain's default-mode network and "unbind" the self-model (Carhart-Harris's entropic-brain;
  Letheby & Gerrans, *"Self unbound,"* 2017), producing measurable ego-dissolution and durable
  rises in openness and well-being. Modernist Buddhist teachers treat this as a possible catalyst
  or "glimpse" — Robert Thurman calls it "a powerful accelerant"; Jack Kornfield says it "prepares
  the mind." A psilocybin-plus-retreat study (Smigielski et al.) found synergy. And the canon does
  have a concept for a jolting experience that *spurs* practice: **`saṃvega`**, the urgency that a
  sudden confrontation with impermanence can produce. On that reading a one-time glimpse might
  legitimately motivate someone toward the path.
- **The case that the canon precludes it anyway.** (a) It delivers a *transient, reversible state*,
  not the trait-level uprooting of `asmimāna` and craving that liberation **is** — "you saw the
  way" is not "you walked it." (b) The very mechanism *degrades* `sati` and `sampajañña`, the
  clear awareness the insight is built on — which is precisely what the fifth precept guards;
  Thanissaro Bhikkhu's strict line is that what an intoxicant "closes down" is the alertness needed
  to understand a state from within. (c) Calling the drug-state "`anattā`" may be a **category
  error** imported by a perennialist "mystical experience" frame (Robert Sharf; Mosurinjohn,
  Roseman & Girn 2023, who show the standard mysticism questionnaire carries Christian-Protestant
  assumptions) — the experience that *resembles* not-self need not *be* the Dhamma's insight.

**Our synthesis [extension].** Within the Dhamma's own terms, a psychedelic ego-death is a
`vedanā`/`saññā` event: conditioned, impermanent, and therefore itself an instance of the very
characteristics it appears to reveal. That is the resolution of your paradox. It is *not immoral
because the experience is bad* — it is precluded because (i) the substance is `majja` by the
effect-test, clouding the faculty insight depends on, and (ii) the *state* is not the *trait*: it
shows the characteristic without uprooting the conceit, the way a photograph of a far shore is not
the crossing. The tradition's recurring verdict — "a glimpse, not a path" — is not anti-experience;
it is the claim that the freedom lies in the irreversible ending of craving, which no reversible
state can deliver. Most defenders of a legitimate role concede exactly this and place the
psychedelic *before* and *outside* practice (a `saṃvega`-style spur), never as a substitute for it.
