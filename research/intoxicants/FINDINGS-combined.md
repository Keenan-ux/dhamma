# The Buddha on Intoxicants: An Effect-Based Category, an Instrumental Precept, and the Question of Psychedelics

*A gated, interpretive study. It combines the rigorous corpus core of the earlier two versions (the
recall-ladder recoding that established* majja *as an effect-based category) with the broader applications
(the moral register, the medicine ruling, psychedelics, soma, ego-death) and a new empirical core (the
instrumental grounding of the fifth precept), and weighs them against an adversarially-verified scientific
record. Three evidence registers are marked throughout and never blurred:* **[corpus]** *= verified verbatim
in the live corpus (mūla = canon; aṭṭhakathā/ṭīkā = commentary);* **[science]** *= external empirical
findings, attributed, with their contest noted;* **[interpretive]** *= reasoned synthesis, flagged as such,
never a canonical ruling. This study is admin-gated rather than public because its later half is
interdisciplinary and interpretive; the gate is the safeguard that lets it go where the evidence leads. The
corpus claims stay descriptive and rigorous regardless.*

---

## Abstract

The Pāli canon's category for what the fifth precept forbids is not "alcohol" but `majja`, an open class
defined by effect rather than by a named substance list: whatever intoxicates unto heedlessness. That finding,
established under a homograph-controlled recall ladder, is the study's instrument. Applied to the precept
itself, it shows the precept to be instrumentally grounded. The canon's own taxonomy places the fifth precept
in a different register from the other four. The others are courses of action and defilements of deed
(`kammapatha`, `kammakilesā`), the content of bodily and verbal purity; drink is an occasion of heedlessness
(`pamādaṭṭhāna`) and a drain on wealth. The wrong is routed through heedlessness, not through any impurity of
the liquid. The explicit claim that intoxication therefore breaks the other four precepts is the commentary
drawing an architecture out of a canonical verse-seed. The canon permits even liquor in medicine at the point
where it can no longer intoxicate. An instrumentally-grounded precept condemns a substance only insofar as the
heedlessness-cascade obtains. Applied to classic psychedelics, with the actual scientific record, the
alcohol-style cascade that grounds the precept does not transfer, while the precept's effect-test still bites
high-dose recreational use and the path-distinction (a reversible state is not the irreversible cutting that
liberation is) holds regardless.

---

## 1. The category is effect-based: `majja` [corpus]

The fifth training-rule names three things and then frames the whole as the occasion of heedlessness:
`surāmerayamajja-pamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī`, "refraining from the occasion of heedlessness that is `surā` (grain
liquor), `meraya` (fermented fruit or floral wine), and `majja` (the generic intoxicant)" (`kp2`, `sn14.25`,
`an5.179`, `an8.39`, `dn31`, `snp2.14`). The formula is early-canonical and recurs across the Dīgha,
Saṃyutta, and Aṅguttara Nikāyas and the Khuddaka collections, not a thin or late fixture.

Reading the cluster requires care, because two of its three words are homograph traps. The naive substring
`majja` returns 1,455 rows, of which only 227 carry the intoxicant sense; the other 1,228 are unrelated
lexemes (`majjha`, middle; `majjana`/`majjati`, polishing and wiping, from a different root; `miñja`, marrow;
and the proper name Majjaka). The liquor word `surā` collides with `sura` (a god) and leaks mid-word into
`asura`, so its 926 substring rows reduce to 648 genuine liquor rows after a deva-and-name purge. Only
`meraya` is clean (279 rows, no homograph). The look-alike-purged, deduplicated cluster is 840 rows, with its
per-layer counts confirmed independently of the search lane. The number the settled finding rests on was never
the substring count; it is 227 intoxicant `majja` rows inside an 840-row cluster.

What makes the category open is a definition, and the definition is commentarial. The canon supplies the
operative category-term: the monastic rule's word-analysis defines `surā` by a closed list (five kinds) and
`meraya` by another (five `āsava`), then runs its offence-permutations on the general term `majje`, ruling that
drinking an intoxicant is an offence whether one perceives it as one or not (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`). The category
already does the work; the named substances are its instances. The commentary first defines the category by
effect, "intoxicant means, in the sense of being intoxicating, `surā` and `meraya`" (`majjaṃ vuccati
madanīyaṭṭhena surā ca merayañca`, `cst-s0506a.att-18_p049`, `cst-s0504a.att-83_p050`), and then opens it:
`surā` and `meraya` are mere examples (`nidassanamatta`), and `majja` is "whatever else there is that is
intoxicating, by drinking which one becomes intoxicated and heedless." That open generalisation appears in
exactly two rows, both commentarial (`cst-s0305a.att-sn5_12_p079`, the Saṃyutta-aṭṭhakathā, quoted by the
sub-commentary `cst-s0105t.nrf-60_p030`), and in zero canonical rows. The effect-principle the commentary
states as a definition is itself drawn from canonical material that already runs on effect: the verb and
adjective of intoxication, `madanīya` and `majjati`, are early-canonical, but their object in the early canon
is the sense-pleasures rather than liquor (`madanīye majjati` at `an5.81` takes the five `kāma` as its object),
and the canon's deepest sense of intoxication is existential, the three `madā` of youth, health, and life
(`an3.39`, expanded into a longer mada-taxonomy in the Abhidhamma Vibhaṅga `cst-abh02m.mul-150`). The category
is effect-based throughout: the canon operates the principle, and the commentary fixes it as a definition.

## 2. The register is heedlessness, not impurity [corpus]

The condemnation is consistently consequential and psychological, never a purity-taboo on the liquid. The
Sigālovāda (`dn31`) gives the six drawbacks (`cha ādīnavā`) of habitual drinking in a single verbatim list:
present loss of wealth (`sandiṭṭhikā dhanajāni`), breeding of quarrels (`kalahappavaḍḍhanī`), a basis of
disease (`rogānaṁ āyatanaṁ`), disrepute (`akittisañjananī`), indecent exposure (`kopīnanidaṁsanī`), and the
weakening of wisdom (`paññāya dubbalikaraṇī`) as the sixth and culminating item. The same discourse lists drink
first among the six `apāyamukha`, the drains on wealth. The verse rebuke of `snp2.14` says a householder should
not drink, "knowing it ends in madness" (`ummādana`), and calls drink a field of demerit (`apuññāyatana`);
`an5.179` names the drinks as those that "confuse the mind" (`cittamohanī`). The named harm is to the cognitive
faculty and to concrete welfare, not to ritual status.

The canon's gravest "drunkenness" is not chemical at all. The three `madā` of `an3.39`, the intoxication of
youth, health, and life, share the root with `majja`; the deepest intoxication the canon names is the heedless
absorption in being young, well, and alive. Drink is one occasion of a general condition, and the precept
condemns it as such.

## 3. The precept is instrumental: the canon's own two registers, and the circularity [corpus → interpretive]

The sharpest result is that the canon itself categorizes the fifth precept differently from the other four.
This is not a matter of how the precept is described; it is where the precept sits in the canon's own
taxonomies.

In the Sigālovāda the four other precepts are the four `kammakilesā`, the defilements of action (killing,
stealing, sexual misconduct, lying), while drink is a `bhogānaṁ apāyamukhaṁ`, a drain on wealth: distinct
headings in the same discourse (`dn31`). In the Cunda discourse (`an10.176`) the Buddha is asked about purity
(`soceyya`) and rejects the brahmins' ritual washing, reframing purity as the tenfold course of action
(`dasakammapatha`): the three bodily, four verbal, and three mental kinds of conduct, the same conduct-list the
failure-of-action discourse `an3.119` enumerates. The content of the other four precepts is present in that
purity scheme (killing, stealing, sexual misconduct under bodily purity; lying under verbal purity), and drink
is absent from it. A corpus check confirms the structure: `kammapatha` occurs
in 585 rows, only 25 co-occur with the drink cluster, and in none is drink a member of the list; the standard
tenfold `kammapatha` never includes `surāmeraya`. The fifth precept lives in the heedlessness-and-training
register (`pamādaṭṭhāna`, `apāyamukha`, `sikkhāpada`), not the intrinsic-action register (`kammapatha`,
`kammakilesā`, `soceyya`) where the other four sit.

The grammar of the precept carries the same fact. `pamādaṭṭhāna` is not a dedicated drink-word; it is a
productive idiom, "occasion of heedlessness," applied across the canon to gambling (`jūta-ppamādaṭṭhāna`,
`dn1`, `dn2`), to a fine lodging, and to kingship. The commentary spells out the form: "heedlessness stands
here, hence occasion-of-heedlessness" (`pamādo ettha tiṭṭhatīti pamādaṭṭhānaṃ`, `cst-s0101a.att-dn1_1_p251`),
and gives the rationale for avoiding trade in drink in the ablative, "because it is an occasion of heedlessness"
(`majjavaṇijjā pamādaṭṭhānato`, `cst-s0403t.tik-an5_p313`). Of the deduplicated `pamādaṭṭhāna` rows, 33 carry
no drink reference at all: the heedlessness-occasion is read here in gambling, a fine lodging, and kingship,
with drink one instance among them.

The explicit claim that intoxication therefore breaks the other four precepts is the commentary drawing an
architecture out of a canonical seed. The seed is canonical: the verse `madā hi pāpāni karonti`, "for the
intoxicated do evil deeds," sits in the early-canonical Dhammikasutta (`snp2.14`). The architecture is
commentarial: glossing the fifth precept, the Khuddakapāṭha-aṭṭhakathā states that drinking intoxicants is
"precisely what defiles and breaks the preceding precepts too" (`majjapānameva saṃkilesakarañca bhedakarañca`),
and reads "do evil deeds" as "they do killing and all the unwholesome" (`pāṇātipātādīni sabbākusalāni karonti`,
`cst-s0505a.att-31_p031`). The narrative exemplum, the discovery of liquor and the train of harms it brings, is
the Kumbha Jātaka (`ja512`). The drink-and-the-other-precepts co-occurrences in the canon are dense (the
five-vice lists, `an9.63` and kin), but reading them shows they are list-enumeration, not a causal chain; the
explicit "having become heedless, one then acts" construction near the drink cluster does not occur in the
canon.

Two further facts complete the register. The precept's value is framed as other-directed safety: abstaining
from each precept, drink included, is one of five great gifts (`mahādāna`) that give to immeasurable beings
freedom from fear, enmity, and affliction (`abhayaṁ deti averaṁ deti abyābajjhaṁ deti`, `an8.39`). And the
precept is not framed as the drinker's own purification: across 70 rows where a purification word sits beside
the drink cluster, every one is incidental (the purified divine eye, the purified meditative mind, the purities
of recitation), and none makes abstaining-from-drink a ritual or bodily self-purity.

The structured absence is measured, not assumed. The impurity vocabulary is present in the corpus (`asuci` and
kin in 545 rows; the stain words `aṅgaṇa`, `kiliṭṭha`, `upakkilesa` in over three thousand), so its absence
from the drink-category is a patterned boundary. Where an impurity word does sit beside a drink token (31 rows;
57 for the stain words), reading every canonical row shows that none predicates impurity of the drink: they are
the impurity of the body, the corpse, and the cesspit (`dn30`, `dn23`, `mn129`), plus the `surā`/`asura`
substring leak. Where `pāpa` or `akusala` sits near drink, it is predicated of the person or the conduct (the
addict who "digs up his own root," `dhp235-255`), never of the liquid as such. And the canon adjudicates the
substance-versus-conduct question directly: in the Āmagandhasutta (`snp2.2`) the Buddha answers a brahmin's
food-purity scruple by listing killing, theft, lying, and adultery and concluding "this is taint, not the
eating of meat" (`esāmagandho na hi maṁsabhojanaṁ`). Taint is conduct, not substance. The canon is on record
rejecting the very purity-taboo logic the fifth precept is sometimes read to assert.

**The circularity [interpretive].** Put together, these facts ground a diagnosis. The fifth precept is
instrumental: it guards the other four by guarding against the heedlessness that breaks them, and the canon
grounds it consequentially, in the six drawbacks, the weakening of wisdom, and the `pamāda` that the very
compound names. An instrumental precept is contingent on its effect obtaining. To classify a substance as
`majja` is, by the canon's own definition, to claim that it intoxicates unto heedlessness, and the wrong it
names is the heedlessness-cascade, not the substance. To condemn a thing "as an intoxicant" while the
consequential harms that define an intoxicant do not obtain is therefore to assume the conclusion in the
premise. The category cannot be applied by naming; it can only be applied by what a thing does.

## 4. The effect-test as the operative criterion [corpus]

The clearest proof that the prohibition tracks effect, not substance, is that the canon permits even liquor at
the exact point where it can no longer intoxicate. In the Bhesajjakkhandhaka (`cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_6`), liquor
is allowed in a medicinal oil-decoction, then restricted: oil into which liquor has been put may be drunk only
"where the colour, smell, and taste of the liquor are not detectable" (`majjassa na vaṇṇo na gandho na raso
paññāyati`). Over-strength batches are forbidden to drink and re-designated for external rubbing. Permission is
keyed to the effect vanishing. If the prohibition were a purity-taboo, no degree of de-intoxication would
license the liquid.

The monastic rule carries the same logic in its permutations. The offence falls on `majje`, the intoxicant,
whether perceived as one or not, while there is no offence in drinking something that "is not an intoxicant but
has the colour, smell, and taste of one" (`amajjañca hoti majjavaṇṇaṁ majjagandhaṁ majjarasaṁ`,
`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`). What matters is whether the thing can intoxicate, not what it resembles. The
sub-commentary draws the line by the same mark, distinguishing liquor "that has reached the defining mark of an
intoxicant" (`majjalakkhaṇappattāya surāya`) from a non-intoxicating sour brew (`loṇasovīraka`), which is
permitted (`cst-s0105t.nrf-75_p005`).

The origin of the rule is consequential, not declarative. The monk Sāgata, a master of the five higher
knowledges who had subdued a fire-dragon with his own fire-element, is honored, drinks a clear liquor, and is
left lying senseless (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pc51`); the commentary's verdict is that "for one of the five higher
knowledges, drinking intoxicants is unsuitable" (`pañcābhiññassa sato majjapānaṃ nāma na anucchaviyaṃ`,
`cst-vin02a1.att-56_p002`). The rule is laid down because the drink reduced a master of power to a heap, not
because the liquid was impure.

## 5. Psychedelics [corpus + science + interpretive]

**The canon has no entheogen category [corpus].** There is no Pāli word for a vision-inducing plant or potion,
and no discourse on one. The only mushroom material is culinary or botanical: `ahicchattaka` ("snake-parasol")
appears in Vinaya commentary only under the rule against destroying plant life, and the Buddha's last meal,
`sūkaramaddava` (`dn16`), is debated by the commentaries as soft pork, a milk-rice dish, a bamboo shoot, an
elixir, or a mushroom, a dispute purely about diet, never about a vision.

**The principled extension [interpretive].** Because `majja` is gated by effect, a mind-altering substance that
the canon never names is not thereby outside the principle; whether it falls under the precept depends on
whether it does what `majja` is defined by doing. This is an inference from the effect-test, not a canonical
ruling, and it is the inference that the science must inform.

**What the science actually shows [science].** A targeted, adversarially-verified review of the relevant
empirical literature returns four findings, each of which came back "mixed-contested" rather than one-sided,
and each of which bears directly on the effect-test.

1. *Ego-dissolution.* Psychedelics reliably and reversibly relax the brain's self-model: default-mode-network
   desynchronization is large and replicated (Carhart-Harris and Friston's REBUS model; the Siegel and
   Dosenbach precision-fMRI study, Nature 2024), and the acute state returns to baseline within hours.
   Letheby's naturalist account (Philosophy of Psychedelics, 2021) argues that this can be genuine insight
   congruent with not-self, not a wisdom-degrading delusion, and that argument survives its main objection. The
   contested point is metaphysical, not empirical: whether the experience is direct perception of a
   mind-independent reality is a philosophical claim that naturalism cannot settle non-circularly, and a
   practitioner who takes it as such has a different problem (a view, `diṭṭhi`), not a cloudier mind.

2. *The faculty.* The effect on perception and attention is dose- and context-dependent, neither simple
   sharpening nor simple clouding. At low or micro doses there is no sedative-type global clouding, so the
   straight alcohol analogy fails there. At an acute full dose processing speed is impaired in a dose-dependent
   way (the pooled reaction-time effect across doses is large, Hedges' g around 1.13, rising toward 1.79 at high
   doses), consistent with an intoxication-type state. The claim that psychedelics actively sharpen acuity does
   not survive blinding: the best-powered preregistered
   meta-analysis (Pinhas and colleagues, 2026; fourteen studies, over sixteen hundred participants) finds no
   overall cognitive benefit and a robust selective reduction in cognitive control. Reduced top-down control is
   precisely the restraint and vigilance the precept's `paññāya dubbalikaraṇī` names.

3. *Durability.* The effect that defines an intoxicant, heedlessness, is the acute state, and it reverses
   within hours. The claim of lasting personality change (raised trait openness) does not survive controlled
   designs: an active-comparator trial against an antidepressant found no psychedelic-specific personality
   advantage. What does persist is neuroplastic (dendritic and functional changes lasting weeks) and clinical
   (depression remission at months), neither of which is the irreversible cutting that the path is.

4. *Versus alcohol.* On the two limbs most tied to the commentarial rationale, dependence and
   disinhibition-driven violence, classic psychedelics are mechanistically and epidemiologically different from
   alcohol: no withdrawal syndrome and no compulsive self-administration (firm and mechanism-backed), no
   established alcohol-style disinhibition-aggression pathway, and the lowest aggregate-harm ranking where
   alcohol ranks highest (Nutt and colleagues, Lancet 2010). The further observation that psychedelic users
   report less violence is weaker, being cross-sectional and self-selected. The cascade that makes alcohol the
   paradigm `majja`, compulsion plus disinhibition leading to the breaking of the other precepts, does not
   transfer. The overstatement to avoid is "harmless": the supervised trials record a dose-graded
   psychiatric-harm signal (in the COMPASS Phase 2b data, suicidal-ideation and self-injury serious adverse
   events ran around six to eight percent in the active 10 and 25 mg arms against roughly one percent in the 1 mg
   control), acute panic is common, and HPPD exists. The reading the evidence supports is "different and less
   harmful," not "safe."

   Two adjacent findings round this out. The anthropological record from five continents converges on ritual
   entheogen use being structured against heedlessness, not toward it, the opposite of `pamāda`. And the
   "stoned-ape" hypothesis, that psilocybin drove human cognitive evolution, is unsupported and treated as
   pseudoscience; it belongs in the account only as the fringe line it is.

**The effect-test applied to psychedelics [interpretive].** The effect-test does not return a single verdict;
it returns a dose- and context-sensitive one, the verdict a substance list would flatten. For the property that
defines `majja`, intoxication unto heedlessness with a cascade into broken conduct, the alcohol case obtains and
the classic-psychedelic case largely does not: no dependence, no disinhibition-violence, an acute state that
reverses, and at low doses no global clouding. By the precept's own logic that pulls against automatic
inclusion. The test still bites where the effect is present: a high recreational dose demonstrably impairs the
faculty, and the reduction in cognitive control is the restraint the precept guards. None of this makes the
substances "harmless," nor licenses them as aids to practice, which is a separate question taken up in section
7. A high dose impairs the faculty; a microdose does not produce the global clouding the precept names. The
category was never about the substance.

## 6. Soma [corpus + science]

**In the Pāli canon, Vedic soma as a substance is essentially absent [corpus].** "Soma" in the corpus is
overwhelmingly a homograph: Somā the nun who rebuts Māra (`sn5.2`, `thig3.8`), Queen Somā (`mn90`), King Soma
and the moon in verse. The single footprint of the soma ritual is the sacrifice-name `vājapeyya` inside the
stock five-sacrifice list (`sn3.9`, `an4.39`), which a modern translator renders "the royal soma drinking";
the Pāli word `soma` does not appear there, so "soma" is the translator's gloss, not canonical vocabulary. The
Buddha engages the brahmanical ritual world not through soma but through his critique of bloody sacrifice:
`sn3.9` condemns mass animal-sacrifice as violent and fruitless, and `dn5` reconstructs the great sacrifice as
a bloodless almsgiving. He post-dates the high soma cult; it is simply not his interlocutor.

**What soma was [science, contested].** There is no scholarly consensus. Ephedra leads the academic field (Falk
1989; Houben 2003): the texts describe a stimulant enhancing alertness, not a visionary hallucinogen, and
living Zoroastrians still use ephedra for the cognate `haoma`. The fly-agaric hypothesis (Wasson 1968) is
sharply contested (Brough 1971), as is the Syrian-rue reading (Flattery and Schwartz 1989). A strong strand
holds the original plant simply lost. The much-cited Central Asian residue archaeology was not confirmed on
re-analysis. The lexica carry a soma headword, but the canon's text never uses it that way.

## 7. Ego-death and not-self, and the path-distinction [corpus + science + interpretive]

The earlier version of this study argued that a drug-induced dissolution of the felt self is precluded because
it is a transient state rather than a trait. That argument is wrong on its own terms, because the path-moment
(`magga`) is itself a single momentary event; duration cannot be the axis. The right axis is the mode of
abandonment, and the canon and commentary supply it.

**Not-self is an analytic insight, not a felt state [corpus].** In the Anattalakkhaṇasutta (`sn22.59`) each
aggregate is declared not-self on impersonal grounds, that it is not amenable to control and is impermanent,
and is therefore unfit to be regarded as "mine, I am this, this is my self"; it is seen with right understanding
(`sammappaññā`), not felt as the ego dissolving. The decisive residue is the "I am" conceit (`asmimāna`): in
the Khemaka Sutta (`sn22.89`) a disciple who has already discarded self-view still has a residual "I am" that is
an underlying tendency (`anusaya`), uprooted only by sustained contemplation. A produced state can suspend the
sense of self; it cannot by itself reach an `anusaya`. And any profound produced state is itself conditioned:
`mn52` has the meditator see of every attainment that it is "produced and intended" and therefore impermanent,
and `mn26` records the Buddha attaining the highest formless bases of his teachers and setting them aside as
not leading to awakening. The most refined consciousness-alteration available was personally tested and
rejected.

**The mode of abandonment: suppression versus cutting [corpus].** The commentary's threefold taxonomy of
abandonment distinguishes suppression (`vikkhambhana`, reversible, as concentration suppresses the
hindrances), substitution (`tadaṅga`), and cutting-off (`samuccheda`, irreversible, as the path-knowledge cuts
a fetter). The taxonomy is commentarial: the suppression-term `vikkhambhana` occurs in zero early-canonical
rows and densely in the commentary (250 aṭṭhakathā, 363 ṭīkā); `tadaṅga` likewise (3 early); the cutting-term
`samuccheda` has a small canonical toehold (4 early) and is otherwise commentarial. But it is built on
canonical irreversibility seeds, which are robustly early: the stream-enterer who "cannot fall" (`avinipāta`)
appears in 70 early rows; the assured one "bound for awakening" (`sambodhiparāyana`) in 36; the extirpation of
the latent tendency (`anusaya` with `samugghāta`) in 11. The magga's object, `nibbāna`
(`nibbānārammaṇa`), is a commentarial formulation (zero early, 90 aṭṭhakathā, 116 ṭīkā), built on the canonical
fact that the path issues in the unconditioned.

**The object, not only the mode [corpus → interpretive].** The mode-of-abandonment axis answers what follows the
seeing. A finer question is whether the two seeings, the drug-occasioned apprehension of not-self and the
path-moment, are the same cognitive act. The commentary locates the difference one step earlier, in the object
of consciousness (`ārammaṇa`), and the answer is clean: insight (`vipassanā`) takes the conditioned as object,
seeing not-self in formations rising and falling, while the path-moment takes the unconditioned as object. The
`magga`-citta is the one class of consciousness whose object is `nibbāna`. The whole moment-by-moment apparatus
that draws this is post-sutta, and it is built on a canonical seed. The insight that takes formations as object,
`saṅkhārupekkhā`, appears in 0 early-canonical rows and 176 commentarial; the conformity-knowledge `anuloma` in
0 early and 68 commentarial; the path-object compound `nibbānārammaṇa` in 0 early and 206 commentarial; the
pivot-consciousness `gotrabhū` is an Abhidhamma and commentarial term (174 and 339), its 3 early rows a
type-of-person in the offerings lists (`an9.10`, `an10.16`, `mn142`), not the cognitive pivot. The canonical
seed is the plain statement `sabbe dhammā anattā`, all phenomena are not-self (6 early rows), together with the
canonical fact that any produced state is conditioned (`mn52`). The canon locates not-self-seeing on conditioned
phenomena and marks the unconditioned as the path's issue; it does not build the object-switch. The commentary
does.

The hinge is `gotrabhū`, change-of-lineage, the single moment at which the object switches. A Saṃyutta
sub-commentary states it outright: `gotrabhū`, by being included in the conformity-process, may be reckoned as
belonging to insight, or, by having `nibbāna` as its object, as belonging to the path
(`cst-s0303t.tik-sn3_1_p145`); it adds that insight is the near-immediate antecedent of the path
(`vipassanā maggassa āsannānantaraṃ`). So the tradition's own answer to whether the two seeings coexist is that
they are not simultaneous states but adjacent moments in one cognitive process, with `gotrabhū` the bridge that
is insight-like by position and path-like by object. The path contains not-self-seeing of the conditioned as
its approach, and adds the object-switch the approach alone does not make.

This sharpens the precluded-as-a-state reading [interpretive]. A drug-occasioned apprehension of not-self, if
genuine, maps at best onto the conditioned-object side, a `saṅkhārupekkhā`-flavored equanimity toward dissolving
phenomena. It is real, and it is not the path: it has no developed conformity-process to ripen into, no
`gotrabhū`, and no way to take the unconditioned as object, and it arises in a faculty whose top-down control is
measurably reduced, which is the opposite of the concentrated, faculty-balanced consciousness the path-process
requires. The precept's concern with the weakening of wisdom and this object-point are the same point from two
sides: the clarity that would let the seeing mature into the cutting is the clarity the intoxicant relaxes.

**The synthesis on this point [interpretive].** A drug-occasioned dissolution, even a genuine one, is at best a
suppression: the self-model relaxes and then returns, which is `vikkhambhana`, reversible by construction, and
the science confirms the acute state reverses within hours. The path-moment, though momentary, is `samuccheda`,
a cutting that does not return: the stream-enterer cannot fall back. The difference is not duration; it is
whether anything is cut. This is why the tradition's recurring verdict is "a glimpse, not a path." The science
cuts both ways here. Letheby's case means the glimpse need not be dismissed as
"merely resembling" not-self; it can be congruent insight, and the canon has a word for a jolting confrontation
with reality that spurs practice (`saṃvega`). But seeing is not cutting. A reversible relaxation of the
self-model, however vivid and however congruent, is not the irreversible extirpation of the conceit and the
craving that liberation is, and it arises in a faculty whose top-down control is measurably reduced, which is
the restraint the path is built on. Most who argue for a legitimate role concede exactly this and place the
experience before and outside practice, as a spur, never as a substitute.

---

## 8. Synthesis [interpretive]

The study's instrument is the corpus's own finding: `majja` is an effect-based category, and the precept that
uses it is instrumentally grounded. The canon places the fifth precept in the heedlessness register, not the
intrinsic-action register where the other four sit; it routes the wrong through `pamāda` and its consequences;
it never treats drink as an impurity; and it permits even liquor at the point where it stops intoxicating. The
explicit architecture by which intoxication breaks the other precepts is the commentary drawing out a canonical
seed. From this the circularity follows: an instrumental precept condemns a substance only insofar as the
heedlessness-cascade obtains, so to condemn a substance "as an intoxicant" when that cascade does not obtain is
to assume the conclusion.

Applied to the actual evidence, the effect-test yields a dose- and context-sensitive answer, the answer a
substance list would flatten. For alcohol the cascade obtains: dependence, disinhibition, the documented train
of quarrel, disease, and loss, and the weakening of wisdom the canon names. For classic psychedelics that
cascade does not transfer (section 5): the limbs that make alcohol the paradigm intoxicant are absent. The test
still bites high-dose recreational use, where the faculty is demonstrably impaired and cognitive control
reduced, and it does not pronounce the substances harmless, given their own distinct risks. On the deeper
question, whether such a state could be a path rather than a precept-breach, the answer is independent of the
precept: a reversible suppression of the self-model, even a genuinely insight-congruent one, is not the
irreversible cutting that liberation is. The corpus draws the line by effect; the application of that line is
neither a blanket prohibition by name nor an endorsement, but a verdict that turns, as the category always did,
on effect rather than name.

## 9. Method note and honest limits

- The corpus claims are verified verbatim in the live corpus (194,710 passages; CST/SuttaCentral
  segmentation). Counts are deduplicated (one primary edition per passage) and per-character density is
  measured against the deduped denominators; every count a claim rests on was sense-read from a sample before
  being used, with the homograph masks applied (`majja`/`surā` are traps; `surā` leaks into `asura`). Pāli is
  quoted with full diacritics and matches the cited row.
- The instrumental reading of section 3 was framed as a set of explicit expectations, fixed before the passages
  were enumerated, about where the canon would route the wrong: in heedlessness and its consequences, not in any
  impurity of the substance. The enumeration bore them out.
- The exact phrase `paññāya dubbalikaraṇī` is rare as a string; the faculty-harm claim rests on its position as
  the named sixth drawback and its kin (`cittamohanī`, `ummādana`), not on frequency.
- The scientific findings are external and attributed, and each was adversarially checked; all four returned
  contested rather than settled, and the text reports both sides. The application of the effect-test to specific
  substances is interpretive, flagged as such, and is not a canonical ruling.
- The soma identifications and the psychedelic neuroscience are contested fields; claims are attributed to
  their proponents, not asserted.
- This is a descriptive study of what the sources classify as an intoxicant and how, extended by an explicitly
  interpretive analysis weighed against the science. It does not pronounce on what any individual should do.
