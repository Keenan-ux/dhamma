FACET C1-precept — Fifth precept & lay ethics on alcohol

[SUMMARY]
The fifth lay precept is formulated as surā-meraya-majja-pamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇī — abstaining from the OCCASION/ground (ṭhāna) of heedlessness (pamāda) that comes of surā (grain liquor/beer), meraya (fermented/fruit wine, toddy) and majja (intoxicants generally); the framing is explicitly around pamāda, not the liquid as such (kp2: "Surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā veramaṇisikkhāpadaṁ samādiyāmi"). The canon enumerates the harm in DN 31 (Sigālovāda) as the six ādīnavā of habitual drinking — Sandiṭṭhikā dhanajāni (immediate loss of wealth), kalahappavaḍḍhanī (breeding quarrels), rogānaṁ āyatanaṁ (a basis of disease), akittisañjananī (disrepute), kopīnanidaṁsanī (indecent exposure), and paññāya dubbalikaraṇī (weakening of wisdom, "the sixth thing") — and lists drinking first among the six apāyamukha, "drains on wealth." The moral register is overwhelmingly that of pamāda / madness / demerit rather than a free-standing label of "immorality": Snp 2.14 (Dhammika) calls drink ummādanantaṁ ("it ends in intoxication/madness") and an apuññāyatana ("a field of demerit"), and AN 5.179 names the drinks individually in verse — "Merayaṁ vāruṇiṁ jantu, na pive cittamohaniṁ" (a person should not drink meraya or vāruṇī wine, which confuse the mind). Abstention is positively valued: in AN 8.39 giving up surāmeraya is one of five mahādāna ("great gifts") that give "freedom from fear" to countless beings, and AN 5.179 / SN 14.25 fold it into the standard five-precept (pañcasikkhāpada) list whose keeping conduces to a good rebirth and stream-entry. Sujato uniformly renders the surā/meraya/majja triad as "beer, wine, and liquor intoxicants." FACET C2-vinaya — Monastic Vinaya rule on drinking

[SUMMARY]
The Theravāda monastic rule against drinking is Pācittiya 51 of the Bhikkhu Mahāvibhaṅga (Surāpānasikkhāpada, first rule of the Surāpānavagga). The final ruling, fetched verbatim, is "Surāmerayapāne pācittiyan" — drinking surā (grain/fermented liquor) OR meraya (fruit/floral wine, i.e. distilled/infused intoxicants) is an offense entailing confession (pācittiya). The rule covers BOTH classes explicitly: the canonical word-analysis defines surā as drinks made from flour, cookies, rice, yeast, or mixed ingredients, and meraya as āsavas made from flowers, fruit, honey, sugar, or mixtures. The origin story is the elder Sāgata: after subduing a fire-breathing nāga at Ambatittha by "conquering fire with fire" (tejodhātu), Kosambī laypeople (prompted by the group-of-six monks) plied him with kāpotikā liquor in house after house until he collapsed senseless at the town gate and lay with his feet pointing at the Buddha — prompting the Buddha's rhetorical "should one drink that which makes one senseless?" (taṁ pātabbaṁ yaṁ pivitvā visaññī assā). The strictness is canonical, not merely commentarial: the rule's own Padabhājaniya states "antamaso kusaggenapi pivati, āpatti pācittiyassa" — even what fits on the tip of a blade of grass incurs the offense; and uniquely among perception-permutations, drinking actual liquor is a full pācittiya even when the monk believes it non-alcoholic (majje amajjasaññī pivati, āpatti pācittiyassa), so honest mistake gives NO exemption once the substance is truly liquor. The only exemptions (anāpatti) are genuinely non-alcoholic drinks that merely look/smell/taste like liquor, liquor cooked into curry/meat/oil/myrobalan-syrup, non-alcoholic ariṭṭha, the insane, and the first-offender. Versus the lay precept (the 5th sikkhāpada, voluntary undertaking, no formal sanction), the monastic rule is far more absolute: a fixed offense class requiring formal confession, with the blade-of-grass quantum and the no-exemption-for-misperception clause. Commentary (Samantapāsādikā "Surāpānasikkhāpadavaṇṇanā", Sp §56) and sub-commentary (Sp-ṭ) are present in the corpus and gloss the āsava categories (pupphāsava, phalāsava, madhvāsava, guḷāsava). The bhikkhunī parallel exists as a shared rule but its SuttaCentral id is NOT pli-tv-bi-vb-pc51 (that id resolves to the unrelated Ārāmapavisanasikkhāpada — bhikkhunī pācittiya numbering diverges from the bhikkhu count); app instability blocked verbatim confirmation of the bhikkhunī surāpāna id.

FACET C3-medicine — Alcohol as medicine — ever permitted, any quantity?

[SUMMARY]
Yes — the Theravāda Vinaya does permit alcohol medicinally, but narrowly and never as a drink for its intoxicating effect. The Bhesajjakkhandhaka (Mahāvagga VI; CST Vin 6 = cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_6) contains the canonical "tincture" ruling verbatim: when oil is cooked as medicine (telapāka) for the monk Pilindavaccha's wind-ailment, the Buddha first allows liquor (majja) to be added — "Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, telapāke majjaṃ pakkhipitunti" — then, after the chabbaggiya monks brew over-strong oil and get drunk, restricts it to the case where the liquor is undetectable: "Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, yasmiṃ telapāke majjassa na vaṇṇo na gandho na raso paññāyati, evarūpaṃ majjapakkhittaṃ telaṃ pātunti" ("I allow oil into which liquor has been put to be drunk, provided that in the oil-decoction the colour, smell and taste of the liquor are not evident"). Over-dosed batches "atipakkhittamajjaṃ telaṃ" are forbidden to drink and re-designated for external use (abbhañjana). The crucial "colour/smell/taste not evident" criterion is thus IN THE ROOT TEXT, not merely commentarial. Distinct from this are named fermented/sweet preparations allowed as food-medicine: loṇasovīraka (a fermented sour brew) is allowed to the sick freely and to the healthy diluted with water ("Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, gilānassa loṇasovīrakaṃ; agilānassa udakasambhinnaṃ..."), and guḷa (molasses) is allowed freely ("Anujānāmi, bhikkhave, yathāsukhaṃ guḷaṃ paribhuñjituṃ"). The sub-commentary (Sv-pṭ §75.5 = cst-s0105t.nrf-75_p005) draws the line explicitly: the offence is "surāpānameva" — drinking surā that has reached the defining mark of an intoxicant (majjalakkhaṇappattāya surāya), with meraya included — whereas loṇasovīraka/kañjika is a permissible sour brew. So: alcohol as a non-detectable component of a cooked medicine = allowed; surā/meraya as an intoxicating drink (Pācittiya 51) = always prohibited.

[SUMMARY]

(1) Anattā is an analytic insight, not a felt state: in the Anattalakkhaṇasutta (SN 22.59) each of the five khandhas is declared not-self on two impersonal grounds — they are not amenable to control ("you can't compel form: 'May my form be like this'") and they are impermanent, suffering, and perishable, therefore unfit to be regarded as "This is mine, I am this, this is my self" (etaṁ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā). (2) The decisive distinction between a temporary ego-loss and awakening is the asmimāna ("I am" conceit), a deep anusaya. The Khemakasutta (SN 22.89) shows that even a noble disciple who has discarded self-VIEW and the five lower fetters "still has a lingering residue of the conceit 'I am', the desire 'I am', and the underlying tendency 'I am' which has not been eradicated" — uprooted only by sustained contemplation of the rise and fall of the aggregates (the launderer simile). A drug state cannot touch this anusaya; it suspends, not eradicates. (3) MN 22 (Alagaddūpama) frames right view as the abandoning of self-view and clinging itself (the raft is for crossing, not grasping). (4) A profound altered state is itself conditioned and so is an object to be seen, not a refuge: MN 52 (Aṭṭhakanāgara) has the meditator reflect of every jhāna and formless base, "this too is produced by choices and intention (abhisaṅkhataṁ abhisañcetayitaṁ); but whatever is produced by choices and intention is impermanent and liable to cease (tadaniccaṁ nirodhadhamman)" — and MN 26 (Pāsarāsi/Ariyapariyesana) records the Buddha attaining and then rejecting the formless bases of Āḷāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta as not leading to awakening. Because insight requires sammappaññā / yathābhūta seeing, and an intoxicant degrades the very sati-sampajañña that the insight is built on, the substance is precluded: it produces a saṅkhata state to be contemplated, not the contemplating faculty itself.

TOPIC W1-soma-id: Vedic Soma identity (entheogen-identity debate) and the plausibility of Psilocybe/psilocybin mushroom presence and use in ancient South Asia (~5th c. BCE)
Two linked questions, both with honest caveats. (1) SOMA IDENTITY: There is NO scholarly consensus on the botanical/biological identity of Vedic Soma / Iranian Haoma. The leading academic candidate today is Ephedra (Harry Falk 1989; Jan Houben 2003), which is what living Zoroastrians/Parsis still use and which fits a stimulant (not visionary) reading of the texts. The most famous rival is R. Gordon Wasson's Amanita muscaria / fly agaric (1968), backed by Wendy Doniger O'Flaherty but attacked by John Brough (1971) and others. Other proposals: Peganum harmala / Syrian rue + harmaline (Flattery & Schwartz 1989), cannabis (Bennett, Crowley), Psilocybe (McKenna), and assorted plants (Sarcostemma/Cynanchum, Nelumbo/lotus, ephedra-poppy-cannabis mixtures). A substantial body of scholarship holds the original plant is simply unknown/lost — abandoned during migration into India, gone extinct in its source habitat, or kept so secret the priesthood lost it. The BMAC/Sarianidi archaeological "ephedra+poppy+cannabis residue" claim (1990s) was NOT confirmed by independent re-analysis (Nyberg 1995; three teams 2002-03 found no traces), so it does not settle the question. Entheogen-identity claims here are genuinely contested and should be flagged as such. (2) PSILOCYBE IN ANCIENT INDIA: Two distinct sub-claims must be kept apart. (a) PHYSICAL PRESENCE — well supported: coprophilic (dung-loving) Psilocybe species, including P. cubensis, P. coprophila, and others, are documented growing on cattle/buffalo dung in India today (Punjab, Kerala, Odisha/Orissa), and the subcontinent's warm, monsoon, cattle-grazing ecology is exactly their habitat. So the operator's intuition that dung-loving psilocybin mushrooms would have been physically present around large herbivores in 5th-c.-BCE South Asia is biogeographically reasonable. (b) RITUAL/DOCUMENTED USE — essentially absent: there is NO solid archaeological or textual evidence of psilocybin-mushroom use in ancient India, in sharp contrast to the rich, well-dated Mesoamerican record (mushroom stones ~1000 BCE–900 CE; teonanácatl). The one scholarly review devoted to the question (Winkelman, Kuhar et al., "Does India have entheomycology traditions? A review and call to research," 2022) is framed as a CALL TO RESEARCH precisely because the evidence is suggestive-at-best, speculative, and undeveloped; the few cited items (Kerala "mushroom stones," Khajuraho temple carvings, Buddhist mushroom iconography) are contested, not confirmed entheogenic-use evidence. A scholar of Indian religion (Bagaria) flatly states "nobody in India thinks [Soma is] a mushroom."

TOPIC W2-egodeath: Psychedelic ego-dissolution / "ego death" compared with the Buddhist teaching of anattā (not-self): phenomenology and neuroscience, the Letheby–Gerrans philosophical bridge and its critics, Buddhist-tradition stances, and the strongest case on both sides for whether a psychedelic could be a legitimate aid.
Across the literature, scholars and contemplatives agree there is a genuine PHENOMENOLOGICAL resemblance between psychedelic ego-dissolution and anattā, and a plausible MECHANISTIC story for it: psychedelics suppress the default-mode network and "unbind" the brain's self-model (Carhart-Harris's entropic-brain/REBUS framework; Letheby & Gerrans's predictive self-binding account), producing experiences researchers index with the Mystical Experience Questionnaire (MEQ30) and ego-dissolution scales. The deepest disagreement is interpretive, and it tracks the distinction the operator names: a temporary STATE of self-loss versus a trait-level, liberating INSIGHT. The philosophical case (Letheby's "Philosophy of Psychedelics") naturalizes no-self and treats ego-dissolution as evidence that the self is a useful "fiction" the brain binds together. Critics from several directions argue this conflates categories: (a) the drug occasions a passing, reversible experience, whereas anattā in the suttas is a sustained contemplative apprehension that uproots the "I am" conceit (asmimāna) and craving, not merely a glimpse; (b) the "mystical experience" construct used to measure these states carries perennialist/Christian-Protestant biases (Stace, Pahnke's Good Friday experiment) and so may not be measuring "Buddhist insight" at all (Mosurinjohn/Roseman/Girn; Robert Sharf on the rhetoric of experience); (c) Buddhist tradition itself precludes intoxicants under the fifth precept precisely because they cloud the mindfulness (sati) and heedfulness (appamāda) that insight depends on. On the other side, modernist engagements (the "Zig Zag Zen" anthology; Jack Kornfield, Bob Thurman) and recent empirical work treat psychedelics as a possible catalyst, glimpse, or "helicopter view" — most first-generation Western teachers came to the Dharma through them, and a psilocybin-plus-retreat study found synergy. The recurring tradition verdict is "glimpse, not path": the experience can show the way but cannot walk it, and the Ram Dass / Neem Karoli Baba acid anecdote (later admitted by Ram Dass to be embellished) was itself used to make that very point — that stable realization, not a drug state, is the goal. Both sides are interpretive; positions below are attributed, not asserted.
[FINDINGS]
  - [high] Psychedelic ego-dissolution is mechanistically linked to suppression of the default-mode network (DMN). Carhart-Harris's 'entropic brain' hypothesis holds that psychedelics collapse the normally highly organized DMN activity and decouple it from the medial temporal lobes; decreased DMN integrity/connectivity quantitatively predicts self-reported ego-dissolution. The 2019 REBUS ('relaxed beliefs under psychedelics') / 'anarchic brain' model frames this as a relaxation of high-level priors (including the self-model).  (src: Default Mode Network Modulation by Psychedelics: A Systematic Review (PMC); The entropic brain: a theory of conscious states informed by neuroimaging research with psychedelic drugs (Blossom Analysis summary); Carhart-Harris & Friston 2019 – REBUS and the Anarchic Brain (Qualia Computing))
  - [high] Researchers operationalize these states with validated instruments: the 30-item revised Mystical Experience Questionnaire (MEQ30; Barrett, Johnson & Griffiths 2015), covering unity, transcendence, noetic quality, sacredness, positive mood, and ineffability (a 'complete' mystical experience = 60%+ on all subscales), and the Ego-Dissolution Inventory (EDI; Nour et al. 2016). MEQ30 scores positively predict persisting changes in attitudes, behavior, and well-being.  (src: Validation of the revised Mystical Experience Questionnaire in experimental sessions with psilocybin (Barrett, Johnson, Griffiths 2015); Ego-Dissolution and Psychedelics: Validation of the Ego-Dissolution Inventory (EDI) (PMC))
  - [high] Letheby & Gerrans's 'Self unbound' (2017) is the central philosophical bridge to anattā. They argue, via predictive processing, that self-awareness is the integrated functioning of a hierarchical predictive 'self-model' that posits a persisting entity to bind autobiographical, embodied, and salient experience; psychedelics degrade this self-binding so that cognition is experienced decoupled from self-representation — ego-dissolution is 'best understood as a phenomenon of unbinding.' They endorse fictionalism/eliminativism: the self-model is real and useful but refers to a 'Cartesian fiction,' and they note explicitly that 'apprehending the non-existence of the individual self is a central goal of Buddhist meditation.'  (src: Self unbound: ego dissolution in psychedelic experience (Letheby & Gerrans, Neuroscience of Consciousness 2017); Symposium on Letheby and Gerrans, 'Self unbound' (The Brains Blog))
  - [high] Letheby's book 'Philosophy of Psychedelics' (OUP 2021) and his 'Naturalizing Psychedelic Spirituality' program argue the traditional view of psychedelics as agents of insight and spirituality can be reconciled with naturalism: lasting therapeutic benefit comes from disrupting the neurocognitive self-model and revising pathological self-representations, so a naturalized 'no-self' insight is coherent without any supernatural metaphysics.  (src: Philosophy of Psychedelics – Chris Letheby (Oxford University Press); Naturalizing Psychedelic Spirituality (Zygon: Journal of Religion and Science))
  - [high] The core critique the operator names — a temporary STATE of ego-loss is not the trait-level liberating INSIGHT — is voiced from within contemporary Buddhism as the 'glimpse, not a path' distinction. Harrison Rappaport's metaphor: a psychedelic 'lets you take a little helicopter up above the tree line and see the path... but you have to come back down... how do you get there? And that's what practice is.' Rev. Kokyo Henkel contrasts meditation's nondual insight in 'a very calm, very stable mind' with the 'a little bit chaotic' conditions of psychedelic insight, implying the STABILITY of realization differs fundamentally.  (src: Psychedelic Insight (Jennifer Keishin Armstrong, Lion's Roar); The New Wave of Psychedelics in Buddhist Practice (Lion's Roar))
  - [high] Anattā in the Theravāda framing is explicitly two-tiered, which sharpens why a state is not the insight: first the abandonment of self-VIEW (sakkāyadiṭṭhi, identity-belief — uprooted at stream-entry), and then, far deeper, the eradication of the conceit 'I am' (asmimāna), which persists even after the intellectual view is gone and is only fully removed at arahantship. The drug-occasioned experience, on this reading, at most loosens the felt sense of self transiently; it does not uproot asmimāna or the craving (taṇhā) that binds one to saṃsāra.  (src: Selves & Not-self: The Buddhist Teaching on Anatta (Thanissaro Bhikkhu, Access to Insight); The Three Basic Facts of Existence: III. Egolessness (Anatta) (Access to Insight, Wheel 202))
  - [high] Drug-induced ego-dissolution is, by the empirical literature's own account, always temporary (minutes to hours) and reversible, though it can produce lasting trait changes (notably increased Openness, reduced narrative-self identification). This 'reversible state with possible downstream trait effects' profile is the empirical anchor for critics who say it is categorically unlike a permanent uprooting, and for proponents who point to the measurable lasting changes.  (src: Ego Death with Psychedelics: The Good, the Bad and the Ugly (Evolute Institute); Characterization and prediction of acute and sustained response to psychedelic psilocybin in a mindfulness group retreat (Scientific Reports / Smigielski et al.))
  - [high] A scholarly-critique line argues the very category 'mystical experience' used to assimilate psychedelic states to contemplative insight is methodologically loaded. Mosurinjohn, Roseman & Girn (2023) show the MEQ inherits perennialist 'common-core' assumptions (W.T. Stace) and a Christian-Protestant frame (Pahnke's Good Friday Experiment at Harvard Divinity School), defines mysticism as exclusively positive (excluding malevolent or nihilistic dissolution), and recommend decomposing it into specific constructs (ego dissolution, connectedness) rather than treating it as a culture-transcending essence. Robert Sharf's broader work argues 'experience' is a modern rhetorical construct and that Buddhist meditation was not historically aimed first at inculcating specific mystical experiences — so equating drug-states with Buddhist insight is suspect without cultural context.  (src: Psychedelic-induced mystical experiences: An interdisciplinary discussion and critique (Mosurinjohn, Roseman, Girn; PMC); Robert H. Sharf, 'Experience' from Critical Terms for Religious Studies (Cambridge))
  - [high] The tradition's precept-based rejection: the fifth precept (against intoxicants/surāmeraya) is read as protecting the mindfulness (sati) and heedfulness (appamāda) on which insight depends. Teachers quoted in Tricycle frame it as 'Intoxicants take you away from reality; meditation takes you toward reality' (Lama Yeshe) and 'intoxication obscures the clarity of mind — the clarity to understand and rest in one's true nature' (Lama Palden); Allan Badiner reframes the real issue as 'heedlessness.' Thanissaro Bhikkhu holds an especially conservative line — that what psychedelics typically 'close down' is the mindfulness and alertness needed to understand the nature of bliss, making them incompatible with serious practice.  (src: The Fifth Precept (Tricycle); Six Teachers on the Fifth Precept (Inquiring Mind); Psychedelics and Buddhist meditation – fastest path to awakening or water and oil? (Evolute Institute, summarizing Thanissaro))
  - [high] Modernist engagement is best represented by the anthology 'Zig Zag Zen: Buddhism and Psychedelics' (ed. Allan Hunt Badiner, art-edited by Alex Grey, foreword Stephen Batchelor, preface Huston Smith). It documents that, with one exception, every American Buddhist teacher interviewed had taken psychedelics before coming to Buddhism, and that many who once traveled that route now advise against it — capturing the tradition's ambivalence rather than a single verdict.  (src: Zig Zag Zen (Tricycle review); Zig Zag Zen: An Interview with Author Allan Badiner (MAPS))
  - [medium] The Ram Dass / Neem Karoli Baba anecdote is the tradition's emblematic 'glimpse vs. attainment' parable: Ram Dass reportedly gave his guru a very large LSD dose (commonly told as ~900+ micrograms) with no visible effect, the implied lesson being that one already established in the realized state is untouched by the drug — i.e., stable realization, not a chemical state, is the goal (the central theme of 'Be Here Now'). Notably the story's authenticity is disputed: Terence McKenna doubted it pharmacologically, and Paul Krassner reported (2001) that Ram Dass admitted he made it up — which scholars treat as itself revealing about how the 'drugs only glimpse' message gets constructed.  (src: Baba Ram Dass and the Tale of the Acid-Gobbling Guru (John Horgan, Scientific American); Ram Dass Gives Maharaji the 'Yogi Medicine' (ramdass.org))
  - [medium] The strongest 'legitimate aid' case in the literature: psychedelics as catalyst/accelerant rather than path. Bob Thurman: 'Psychedelics can be a very powerful accelerant of what otherwise takes long years of meditation.' Jack Kornfield says LSD 'prepares the mind for Buddhism' and that the field is 'on the cusp of something revolutionary,' but must be done 'with the boundaries the Buddha offered regarding ethics.' Proposed mechanisms include a visceral first-person experience of impermanence/interdependence/not-self, psychedelic-induced neuroplasticity easing integration, and shared DMN-targeting with meditation. Empirically, the Smigielski et al. psilocybin-plus-mindfulness-retreat study found meditators who received psilocybin had greater ego-dissolution and larger positive psychosocial changes at 4-month follow-up — evidence of possible synergy when paired with practice and framework.  (src: The New Wave of Psychedelics in Buddhist Practice (Lion's Roar — Thurman, Kornfield); Characterization and prediction of acute and sustained response to psilocybin in a mindfulness group retreat (Smigielski et al., Scientific Reports); Can psychedelic use benefit meditation practice? Examining individual, psychedelic, and meditation-related factors (PMC))
  - [medium] Synthesis of the operator's actual question — why preclude something that can occasion the three characteristics? The strongest PRO-aid case: the resemblance is not merely verbal (shared DMN/self-model mechanism, measurable ego-dissolution, durable openness and well-being gains, teacher testimony that it 'opened the portal'); used skillfully within an ethical, integrative practice it can be a catalyst or 'helicopter view' that motivates the path. The strongest PRECLUSION case: (1) it delivers a transient, reversible STATE, not the trait-level uprooting of asmimāna and craving that anattā-as-liberation requires — 'you saw the way' is not 'you walked it'; (2) the mechanism degrades the very faculty (sati/appamāda) that insight is built on, which is exactly what the fifth precept guards, so reliance on it risks substituting a manufactured experience for cultivated discernment; (3) calling the drug-state 'anattā' may be a category error imported by a perennialist 'mystical experience' frame rather than a finding about the Dhamma. Most teachers land on 'at best a glimpse, not a path,' and several caution it can be of little benefit or even harmful. The two sides are not symmetrical empirical claims but differ on what counts as the relevant END — a noetic experience vs. an irreversible transformation of craving.  (src: Psychedelic Insight (Lion's Roar); Self unbound (Letheby & Gerrans, OUP); Psychedelic-induced mystical experiences: an interdisciplinary critique (PMC))
[CAVEATS]
  - This is interpretive/comparative scholarship, not settled fact. All positions above are attributed to their proponents (Letheby & Gerrans, Carhart-Harris, Sharf, Mosurinjohn et al., Thanissaro, Kornfield, Thurman, etc.), not endorsed as the assistant's own view; the two 'sides' frame an ongoing debate.
  - There is no single 'Buddhist position.' Stances range from Thanissaro Bhikkhu's strict precept-based rejection to Bob Thurman's 'accelerant' openness, and Western convert/modernist Buddhism (where most engagement happens) is not representative of traditional Theravāda, Tibetan, or Zen monastic norms. The fifth-precept reading itself is debated (heedlessness vs. any substance use).
  - The Ram Dass / Neem Karoli Baba anecdote is of disputed authenticity (McKenna's pharmacological doubt; Krassner's report that Ram Dass admitted embellishing it). Treat it as an influential PARABLE about glimpse-vs-attainment, not a documented event.
  - The empirical claims (DMN suppression predicting ego-dissolution; MEQ30 predicting well-being; psilocybin-retreat synergy) come largely from small samples, self-report instruments, and a research culture that critics (Mosurinjohn, Roseman & Girn) argue carries perennialist/Christian-Protestant measurement bias — so 'mystical experience' may not cleanly map onto 'Buddhist insight.'
  - The state-vs-trait / asmimāna point is doctrinally robust in Theravāda sources but the inference 'therefore psychedelics cannot contribute to the trait change' is contested; proponents cite measurable lasting trait shifts, while critics note these are not shown to be the specific uprooting of craving that liberation (nibbāna) denotes.
  - Several sources are secondary (Lion's Roar, Tricycle, Evolute Institute summaries) standing in for primary teacher statements and the full Letheby monograph; for a scholarly write-up, the OUP book 'Philosophy of Psychedelics,' the primary Letheby & Gerrans 2017 paper, and Sharf's original essays should be consulted directly. Some search-engine summaries paraphrase rather than quote, so verify exact wording before citing as direct quotation.
