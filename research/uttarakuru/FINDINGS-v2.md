# Uttarakuru Under the Provenance Signature: How a Northern Continent Deepens With Lateness While the Canon Never Stakes It as Verified Knowledge

## Abstract

The northern continent Uttarakuru, the ownerless rice-eating land of the Buddhist four-continent map, was read in an earlier pass on a single axis: a small canonical frame, a large commentarial superstructure, with the commentary supplying the soteriological verdict. That reading is true on its own axis and false as a history. Recoded under the provenance-signature method (which separates structural layer from chronological stratum, epistemic marking, communicative register, cross-recensional reach, and pre-Buddhist provenance), the place stops being a flat canonical block. Of the 26 structurally canonical (mūla) rows, only 6 are early-canonical, and those 6 are not uniform. Two of them, AN 3.80 and AN 10.29, are the bare-name cosmology-list uses, where Uttarakuru is one entry in a thousandfold enumeration (`an3.80`, `an10.29`) [canon]. The third source, AN 9.21, is something else: an early-canonical three-superiorities comparison that already states the soteriological asymmetry (Jambudīpa's third superiority is that "the holy life is lived here"), yet still without describing, measuring, or visiting the place (`an9.21`) [canon]. The other 20 rows carry the mūla tag while coding late-canonical, paracanonical, or commentary-era on time. That disagreement is the finding: the literal-place reading deepens with lateness, and the deepening begins inside the late canon, before any commentary measures it, though that gradient must be read with the circularity caveat set out below. The continent goes from a named entry (early Nikāya prose) to a described place (DN 32, a late paritta, `dn32`) [late-canon], to a visited place (Apadāna verse and Vinaya frame-narrative, the Buddha flies there for alms, `pli-tv-kd1`) [late-canon], to a measured place (the para-canonical Visuddhimagga, 8,000 yojanas, `cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`) [comm]. The ethnographic template is pan-Buddhist bedrock, attested across recensions before the schools divided (the Aggañña golden-age rice, `dn27`, with Mahāvastu, Mūlasarvāstivāda, Chinese, and Sanskrit-fragment parallels) [scholarship]. The specific soteriological verdict that turns the cosmos's most fortunate humans into its worst-placed for awakening has, on the linked evidence, no non-Pāli parallel, and is held as Pāli-local-pending-verification, not as Theravāda invention [scholarship]. Across every stratum one epistemic constant holds: the canon states the geography flat, as assumed background, and never once places it under its own formula for directly-verified knowledge. The divine-eye formula, attested on the order of 124 times, verifies rebirth-by-kamma and never the continents (nearest in-row co-occurrence 4,909 characters, the farthest 227,578) [canon, SQL-confirmed]. The continent becomes more concrete with lateness without ever becoming more epistemically warranted. Materialisation and verification are different axes, and only the first moves.

## The corrected headline

The most important correction this study makes is not a count but a coordinate change. The earlier reading scored each feature on whether a canonical row warranted it, and concluded that a thin canonical sketch was built out by a thick commentary. That axis is preserved here as one row of a fuller signature. What it could not see, because the mūla tag is a *position* in the edited corpus and not a *date*, is that the canon itself is stratified, and that Uttarakuru deepens through those strata in a gradient, subject to the register-relative caveat developed in the chronology section.

The corrected headline is this. A tiny early-canonical seed, a named continent and a propertyless long-lived people with no measure and no figure (`an3.80`, `an10.29` for the bare-list cosmology, `an9.21` for the three-superiorities comparison) [canon], is materialised into a described place inside a late-canonical protective chant (`dn32`) [late-canon], into a visited place inside the Apadāna and the Vinaya frame-narratives (`pli-tv-kd1`, `tha-ap3`) [late-canon], and only then into a measured place at the para-canonical and commentarial boundary (`cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`, `cst-s0404a.att-an9_p063`) [comm]. The ethnographic template the seed draws on is pan-Buddhist and pan-Indian, shared furniture attested across recensions before the sectarian split [scholarship]. The specific soteriological use Theravāda makes of that furniture, ranking the Uttarakurukas above the gods and above us yet barring them from the path, is, on every parallel the corpus can link, Pāli-local, and even within Pāli its sharpest form is commentarial rather than canonical [scholarship]. And at no rung of the gradient does the canon submit the geography to its own test of knowledge: it is stated flat, never verified [canon].

Three things follow that the single axis could not produce. The materialisation the earlier reading credited to "the commentary" already begins inside the late canon, with the qualification that "begins inside the late canon" is a statement relative to the corpus's stratum map and not a demonstrated date. The epistemic status of the geography is uniform across the gradient and is uniformly *background*, not *verified*, which is a separate fact from how concrete it gets. And the one genuinely distinctive contribution in the Pāli treatment is not the ethnography, which is inherited, but the soteriological reading of the ethnography, which is late and local.

## Methodology

### The provenance signature

The method replaces the single canon-versus-commentary axis with a provenance signature: a small triaged set of independent dimensions that together answer where a claim comes from, how original it is, how authoritative it is, and how literally it was meant. The full framework carries eleven axes; the four that do the load-bearing work in this study are named in the triage below, and every signature block in the paper draws on the same eleven-axis schema. The axes that carry this study are chronological stratum (coded independently of structural layer), attribution (who the text puts the claim in the mouth of), epistemic marking (whether a claim is staked under the tradition's own verification formula or stated flat), genre and register (a protective chant and a doctrinal analysis make claims at different literalness), cross-recensional reach (attestation in lines independent of Theravāda), harmonization status (whether the tradition itself flags and reconciles a tension), structural layer (the mūla/attha/ṭīkā position, whose disagreement with stratum is the disagree-flag), pre-Buddhist provenance, and the structured-absence protocol (treating a patterned non-occurrence as data). The signature is coded per load-bearing claim, not per row, because a single passage interleaves speakers, registers, and epistemic statuses. Every claim in the paper carries a compact signature at the point of use and a confidence tag, and resolves to a cited corpus row.

A standing caveat governs the whole paper. The analytic vocabulary, "ethnography", "cosmology", "literal versus symbolic", "plane versus realm", is a modern frame laid over the Pāli; it is named as such, not smuggled in as neutral. The base text throughout is the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana (CST/VRI) edition as ingested into the corpus; counts and character-gaps are edition-relative to that segmentation. Where the corpus carries no English (the Aṭṭhakathā, Ṭīkā, and the Pali-only Visuddhimagga rows), the rendering is the present author's gloss checked against the standard translations; load-bearing terms are quoted in Pāli with English as gloss. Recurrence of a commentarial formula across the network is counted as deployments of one stock gloss, a transmission fact, not as many independent endorsements.

### The triage

The framework is a diagnostic rather than a checklist; coding every axis on every instance would bury the study. The triage maps the research question's load-bearing claims to the axes they actually depend on. For Uttarakuru the question is double, where the literal-place reading comes from and whether the soteriological verdict is original, so the four load-bearing axes are chronological stratum (the place-materialisation gradient), epistemic marking (the verified-versus-assumed asymmetry), cross-recensional reach (the template-versus-verdict split), and the structured-absence protocol (the rebirth machinery that never touches the continent). These four are the same four the Discussion generalizes from. Genre, attribution, structural layer, provenance, and harmonization are coded where they bear on those four.

### The recall ladder

Every claim rests on a measured enumeration, not an assumed one. The first pass on this material searched the surface string with a short final *u* and silently dropped the long-*u* declined forms `uttarakurūnaṁ / uttarakurūsu` that carry the four-continent thousandfold cosmology. The honest stem `%uttarakur%` catches all 47 distinct surface forms (including the ṁ/ṃ variants and the compound forms) and returns **161 rows** (mula 26 / attha 69 / tika 51 / anya 15). The search-depth ladder is exact-FTS 15 → short-*u* substring 144 → honest stem 161. The bare token `kuru` blurs three referents and was excluded with the count reported: 976 bare-*kuru* janapada rows and 26 *kurudhamma/kuruvatta* observance rows are not the continent and were set aside. The no-warrant ethnographic features were additionally searched concept-first, without the name, so the absence verdicts rest on concept searches and not only on name proximity. Recall is therefore a measured variable: every absence below is a measured zero inside a climbed ladder, not an unsearched one.

### Inter-annotator agreement

The features were coded blind by three independent coders on the signature axes, and the codings reconciled. Agreement was high on the load-bearing axes: chronological stratum unanimous on all but two of the 16 features, attribution unanimous on all but three, epistemic marking unanimous on all but one, and the hypothesis verdict under chronology unanimous on all but three. The residual disagreements are informative rather than noise and are reported where they fall; the feature schema carries 16 features in all (A1 through A10, B1 through B5, and C1), of which A1 and A9 receive no standalone prose because their content folds into neighbours, so the 16-feature denominator is the full schema rather than the prose count. The chronological split on A2 (self-ripening rice) is the cleanest: two coders fixed the row's stratum to its early template (the Aggañña container), one to the late paritta that pins the template to the named continent; both are correct on their chosen referent, and the paper reports the template as early and the placement as late rather than averaging them. The attribution split on A10 (the wheel-turner's conquest) turns on whether to weight the early canonical frame or the commentarial naming; it is coded split. These are visible seams in a stratified object, not coding error.

## The within-canon chronological gradient: a place that deepens with lateness

### The six early-canonical seeds

The early-canonical floor is small but not homogeneous in shape. Three suttas, each attested twice in the corpus (once under a SuttaCentral id, once under its CST sibling), give six census rows: AN 3.80 Cūḷanikāsutta (`an3.80`, dup `cst-s0402m2.mul-an3_2_3`), AN 10.29 Paṭhamakosalasutta (`an10.29`, dup `cst-s0404m3.mul-an10_1_3`), and AN 9.21 Tiṭhānasutta (`an9.21`, dup `cst-s0404m2.mul-an9_1_3`). All six code early-canonical with `layer_stratum_disagree: false` [canon].

In two of the three the name is doing the least possible work. AN 3.80 and AN 10.29 are the thousandfold-cosmology lists, where Uttarakuru is one entry in an enumeration: a thousand Uttarakurus, a thousand Jambudīpas, the northern continent named off as one of the four around Sineru and nothing more (`an3.80`, `an10.29`) [canon]. It is a name in a list, not a described place. The third source, AN 9.21, is not a list use at all. It is the early-canonical three-superiorities comparison, and it already carries the soteriological asymmetry on which the whole study turns: the Uttarakurukas surpass the Tāvatiṃsa gods and the Jambudīpa humans in three respects, while the Jambudīpa humans surpass both in three respects of which the third is `idha brahmacariyavāso`, the holy life is lived here. Even here, though, the inhabitants are given only bare predicate-words. The CST text frames "three particulars" (`tīhi ṭhānehi`) but enumerates four nominatives, `amamā, apariggahā, niyatāyukā, visesaguṇā` (variant `visesabhuno`; `an9.21`) [canon]; the commentary and the standard English bundle `niyatāyukā` with `visesaguṇā` so that the count of *particulars* stays three while the count of *words* is four. The fourth word, `visesaguṇā` ("of distinctive quality," DPD `visesagū` "attained distinction"; Sujato "distinctive nature"), strengthens rather than weakens the point that something substantive is said of the inhabitants, and it is acknowledged here precisely so the paper does not repeat the drop-the-fourth-form error it indicts the first pass for committing on the declined surface strings. Crucially `niyatāyukā` carries no number: the canon warrants that the span is fixed, never how long (`an9.21`) [canon]. The early canon gives a named continent, a propertyless people, a fixed-but-unmeasured span, and the bare soteriological ranking. Nothing is located precisely, measured, visited, or described.

### The twenty disagreement rows: the literal place materialises inside the late canon

Above that floor the literal-place reading deepens with lateness, and it does so, on the corpus's stratum map, before any commentary speaks. How much weight that "before any commentary speaks" can bear is the subject of the chronology caveat that closes this chapter; the descriptions below are register-relative.

**Described place (late-canonical paritta).** DN 32, the Āṭānāṭiya, is structurally mūla but codes late-canonical with `layer_stratum_disagree: true` (`dn32`, CST dup `cst-s0103m.mul-dn3_9`) [late-canon, contested-confidence on the late assignment]. Its register is the load-bearing fact: the Āṭānāṭiya is a protective chant, not doctrinal-analytical prose, so its vivid concretisation is the sort of thing a ritual text will tolerate and a doctrinal text will not. Here Uttarakuru is no longer a list-entry but a place, located by Mount Neru, peopled by humans "born there, without mine-making, without possessions" (`dn32`: *Yena uttarakuruvho ... mahāneru sudassano; Manussā tattha jāyanti, amamā apariggahā*) [late-canon], and given the self-ripening unploughed rice (`dn32`: *Na te bījaṃ pavapanti, napi nīyanti naṅgalā; Akaṭṭhapākimaṃ sāliṃ, paribhuñjanti mānusā*) [late-canon]. The description is real, and it is in the canon, but it is in the *late* canon by register and in the chant register.

**Visited place (late-canonical Apadāna and Vinaya frame-narrative).** One rung deeper the place is not just described but travelled to. The Apadāna rows `tha-ap3` and `tha-ap407` both code late-canonical, disagree-true, and carry Uttarakuru as a name in a verse list of lands arahants reach (*Aññe ca uttarakuruṃ, esanāya durāsadā*) [late-canon]. The Vinaya frame-narratives stage the visit as event. In the Mahākhandhaka the Buddha himself flies there for alms: *bhagavā ... uttarakuruṃ gantvā tato piṇḍapātaṃ āharitvā anotattadahe paribhuñjitvā*, "the Blessed One went to Uttarakuru, brought back almsfood from there, ate it at Lake Anotatta" (`pli-tv-kd1`, CST dup `cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_1`) [late-canon]. In the Verañjā famine narrative Moggallāna offers to take the whole Sangha there and the Buddha forbids it (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pj1`, CST dup `cst-vin01m.mul-vin1_1`) [late-canon]. All four Vinaya rows code late-canonical, disagree-true, with the lateness flagged contested-confidence because it is a position label, not a date. The reason this is coded late rather than early is positional: the alms-flight sits in a Vinaya nidāna, an occasioning-frame, and the speaker is the redactor narrating the Buddha's act, not the Buddha asserting a cosmography. The continent has become walkable-by-iddhi inside the canon; the geography is used as scene, never staked as doctrine.

**Measured place (the para-canonical / commentary-era boundary).** The final rung inside the mūla set crosses out of the canon proper into the Visuddhimagga, which is structurally tagged mūla in this corpus but codes classical-commentary on stratum and para-canon on voice, again disagree-true (`cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`, `cst-e0101n.mul-73_p054`, `cst-e0101n.mul-6_p029`, `cst-e0102n.mul-4_p050`) [comm]. Here, and only here, the place acquires a number: *Uttarakuru aṭṭhasahassayojanaṃ. Ekameko cettha mahādīpo pañcasatapañcasataparittadīpaparivāro*, Uttarakuru is 8,000 yojanas, each great continent ringed by five hundred small islands (`cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`) [comm]. The measure is para-canonical, not canonical, and the dataset says so plainly. The thousand-year figure for the span follows the same pattern one layer out, in the Aṭṭhakathā: the canon's bare `niyatāyukā` is glossed as "settled at just a thousand years" only in Mp-a (`cst-s0404a.att-an9_p063`: *Niyatāyukāti tesañhi nibaddhaṃ āyu vassasahassameva*) [comm]. The adversarial check confirms the number is not hiding in the canon: the one true-canon row pairing `vassasahassa` with `uttarakuru` is the Apadāna, where the thousand years belongs to the Āsāvatī creeper, not the lifespan [late-canon, SQL-confirmed].

### What the gradient licenses, and what it does not: the circularity caveat

Three honesty guards bound the claim, and the third is the most important guard in the paper.

First, the gradient is relative stratigraphy on consensus Pāli philological grounds (Āṭānāṭiya, Apadāna, and Vinaya-nidāna membership as the late-canonical warrants); it imports no absolute dates, and where a stratum is contested the census codes it explicitly rather than averaging it into "the canon."

Second, "deepens with lateness" is a statement about the literal-place reading, not a claim that the early canon doubted the continent or that the late canon invented it from nothing; the early seed is genuinely present, just thin and unmeasured.

Third, the correlation between lateness and concreteness is partly *definitional* and cannot by itself prove that the canon's conception of Uttarakuru evolved. The work-to-stratum map sorts works using genre and register cues, terse list versus paritta chant versus Vinaya nidāna-narrative versus scholastic measurement, and those very cues co-vary with concreteness. A measuring text is sorted late partly because it measures. So the gradient is, in part, a restatement of how the strata were defined, not an independent diachronic discovery. The data are equally consistent with a second reading: that old material survived in registers (terse doctrinal lists, three-particulars comparisons) that never had occasion to describe the place, while the act of describing it belongs to register-types (protective chant, occasioning-narrative, scholastic measurement) that happen to cluster later in the consensus map. On that reading the canon's *conception* of Uttarakuru need not have changed at all; only the registers that had reason to elaborate it surfaced in later containers. Two of the pivotal "late" assignments are exactly where this bites, and both are coded contested-confidence: DN 32 is "late" by a *paritta* genre label, and the Vinaya alms-flight is "late" by a *nidāna* position label, and a genre or position label is being silently converted into a date. A late container can recite older cosmological content; this is the same template-versus-placement split the paper already applies to the A2 rice, where the rice-template is early and only its welding to the named continent is late. Even the "6 early" floor is not homogeneous: AN 3.80 and AN 10.29's thousandfold nested cosmology is plausibly a *later* systematization than AN 9.21's bare three-superiorities comparison, so concreteness may already be rising *within* the early rows, and these too are coded contested-confidence. The honest statement is therefore register-relative: on the corpus's stratum map the literal-place reading is thinnest in the terse early registers and thickest in the late measuring registers, and the materialisation that the structural axis credited to the commentary is already present in late-canonical containers; whether that pattern reflects a change in the canon's conception or only a change in which registers had occasion to describe is not decidable from relative stratigraphy alone. The place gets more concrete as one moves toward the late registers; it never gets epistemically upgraded, which is the subject of the next chapter.

## The epistemic axis: what the canon stakes as verified, and what it merely assumes

The most consequential thing the canon does with Uttarakuru is *not* describe it. It is the thing the canon never does: it never places the northern continent under its own formula for directly-verified knowledge. The whole study turns on this asymmetry.

### The verification formula and its fixed object

The canon's "directly verified" marker is the divine-eye pericope: *dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena satte passāmi cavamāne upapajjamāne ... yathākammūpage*, "with the purified divine eye surpassing the human I see beings passing away and being reborn ... faring according to their deeds." The stock object of this seeing is rebirth-by-kamma: who is reborn where, low or high, fair or foul, by their conduct. The formula is attested on the order of 124 times across the corpus. Its companion markers, *sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā* ("having realised by my own direct knowledge"), the first-person aorist *abbhaññāsiṃ* ("I directly knew"), *yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti* ("knows as it really is"), are the canon's vocabulary for staking a claim as seen rather than assumed [canon]. The object of that formula is fixed and vertical: it verifies *where beings go*, never *where the continents are*.

### The five co-occurrences are proximity artifacts, not verifications

The honest objection is that the divine eye and Uttarakuru might co-occur, and that such a co-occurrence would license "the canon verifies the continent." That is the proximity illusion: coding a monolithic row "verified" because a verification formula appears *somewhere* in it. The guard requires marker and claim in the same pericope-window, and that the char-gap be reported. Five canonical rows carry both `dibbena cakkhunā` and `uttarakuru`. The gaps between the two are **4,909 / 5,101 / 43,133 / 45,573 / 227,578 characters** [canon, SQL-confirmed]. At a minimum of nearly five thousand characters, the divine eye is verifying rebirth-destiny in a wholly separate pericope; the continent sits chapters away in the same edited volume. There is no window in which the formula takes the continent as its object. Coded result for the verification of continental geography: **zero attestations under the formula**, a positively-coded flat-background-assertion, not an uncoded residue.

### Coding the claims by status

With the verification formula ruled out, each load-bearing claim falls into a positive status, and none is asserted-verified-direct-knowing.

**Flat-background-assertion (the default, coded positively).** The continental geography is stated as assumed furniture, declaratively, with no epistemic formula in-window: the place and its position by Mt Neru (`dn32`); the continent in the thousandfold list (`an3.80`, `an10.29`); the self-ripening rice (`dn32`); the ownerless life `amamā, apariggahā` (`an9.21`, `dn32`); the fixed lifespan `niyatāyukā`, again with no figure (`an9.21`) [canon, late-canon].

**Illustrative-simile.** Where Uttarakuru appears under a simile marker it is hedged by register: the "like Uttarakuru" climate comparison, *uttarakuru viya ... utuādisampannaṃ*, where the continent is the *vehicle* of a comparison about the Kuru country and the sentence asserts nothing about the continent's own reality (`cst-s0201t.tik-mn1_1_p928`) [comm].

**Hearsay / reported-speech / redactor-narrated.** The most-frequent thing the texts do with Uttarakuru, the alms-flight, is framed as narrated event or posed question, never as the Buddha asserting the geography under a knowing-formula. The Milinda frames bodily travel as a question answered in the conditional: *Atthi, mahārāja, yo iminā cātummahābhūtikena kāyena uttarakuruṃ vā gaccheyya*, "there is one who *could* go to Uttarakuru with this very body" (`mil3.7.9`) [late-canon]. The Vinaya alms-flight is told in the redactor's narrative voice (`pli-tv-kd1`) [late-canon].

The contrast class is mandatory and sharp, and the five legs are not equally strong. The thinnest is row e: the rare-human-birth / Suddhāvāsa frame returns pure name-absence with no positive contrast class, so it carries the least weight and is rested on least. When the tradition finally does assign the continent a destiny, the voice is the sub-commentary, and the same scholastic tradition immediately guards against over-reading it: this "fixed destiny" is "not by way of the technical fixed-class" (`cst-abh03a.att-31_p023`, Pañca-a §31.23) [comm] (row d). The commentary supplies the destiny and then polices its own supply.

One canonical verse looks at first like a counter-case to the whole absence finding, and it is named and disarmed here rather than left for an objector to raise. An Apadāna / Buddhavaṃsa kamma-recital has the speaker reborn into the place, *kurūsu ... sahassakkhattuṃ upapajjiṃ*, a thousand times in the Kurus, long-lived and possessionless: a rebirth *into* the land inside a destiny frame, which is exactly the frame the absence table says never names Uttarakuru. The disarming turns on what the canonical surface word actually is. The verse says `kurūsu`, the ambiguous locative that defaults to the Kuru *janapada*, not to the continent; the identification of that `kurūsu` *as* Uttarakuru is supplied only by the commentary (KN-a §36.49, *Kurūsūti uttarakurūsu*, `cst-s0519a.att-36_p049`) and the sub-commentary (KN-pṭ §41.99, `cst-s0501t.nrf-41_p099`) [comm]. So the canon does not *name* Uttarakuru in a rebirth-into frame at all; the commentary reads the continent into an ambiguous janapada locative, which is itself a second instance of the paper's central pattern, the commentary supplying what the canon leaves bare. This verse sits among the 976 bare-`kurūsu` janapada rows the recall ladder set aside, and it is set aside on exactly the same ground.

The bound, stated honestly: the patterned silence licenses the inference that **the tradition treats continental geography as assumed background rather than as directly-verified knowledge, and that its rebirth-opportunity reckoning is absent, by name and at full ladder depth, from every canonical rebirth-destiny / opportunity frame**. It does *not* license the stronger and false claim that the canon *denies* Uttarakuru, *doubts* it, or *disqualifies* it. The divine-eye formula is silent on the continents, not negative about them; the akkhaṇa list omits the continent, it does not exclude it by name; the heaven-destiny is unstated in canon, not refused.

## The cross-recensional split: a pan-Buddhist template, a Pāli-local verdict

The decisive question this study raises is not when the Uttarakuru material entered the Pāli line but whether it entered the Pāli line at all: whether the load-bearing claims survive in transmissions independent of Theravāda. Cross-recensional attestation is the strongest proxy for antiquity that does not lean on contested Pāli-internal dating: a formulation found in the Chinese Āgamas, a Sanskrit fragment, the Mahāvastu, or the Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya plausibly predates the sectarian split, while a Pāli-only formulation is, at best, undated [scholarship]. The bridge is the corpus's own `passage_parallels` table (4,420 Chinese `lzh` + 773 Sanskrit `san` + 148 Prakrit `pra` rows). The container-versus-feature distinction is kept sharp throughout, and any feature-level claim the linked table cannot confirm is coded `untested` rather than read as present or absent. One labeling caveat applies to the witnesses below and is mirrored in Limitations: the source-tradition names attached to the linked ids (Lokottaravāda-Mahāsāṃghika, Mūlasarvāstivāda, "Sanskrit fragment") are the present author's identification of the SuttaCentral ids, not the corpus link table's own coding, which tags all four witnesses `parallel_lang = lzh`. The link evidence carries the *existence* of a parallel; the recensional-line and language labels are imported scholarly identification, flagged as such.

### The ethnographic template is pre-sectarian (multi-recensional)

The Uttarakuru ethnography is not a free-standing northern-continent invention. Its defining motif, a propertyless people who neither sow nor plough and eat rice that ripens of itself (`akaṭṭhapāka-sāli`), is the Aggañña golden-age rice template recapitulated. The DN 32 Uttarakuru verse (`dn32`) carries the *identical* formula as the cosmic golden age of `dn27` (Aggañña: *akaṭṭhapāko sāli pāturahosi akaṇo athuso suddho sugandho taṇḍulapphalo*), and the commentary glosses Uttarakuru's rice with the very Aggañña wording, huskless, chaffless, fragrant, at `cst-s0103t.tik-dn3_9_p023` [comm]. The same idyll wanders again to the Vessantara forest hermitage (`ja547`) [late-canon]. The Uttarakuru ethnography is one deployment of a mobile canonical template, not a continent-specific datum.

Cross-recensionally that template is bedrock. The bridge resolves `dn27` to four linked external parallels: the Mahāvastu (`san-lo-mvu32`), the Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya (`san-mu-kd17`), a further witness (`t10`), and a fragment (`sf277`) [scholarship]. Four linked witnesses place the golden-age-rice template firmly before the sectarian split. The recensional-line labels for these ids are the author's identification, not the table's coding (see the caveat above and Limitations); the link table tags all four `lzh`.

> *[cluster: ethnographic template | feature: A2 self-ripening rice (`dn32`/`dn27`) | layer: mūla | stratum: late-canonical Uttarakuru deployment over a pre-sectarian template | register: gnomic-verse + golden-age narrative | recension: multi-recensional-pre-sectarian (`dn27` → `san-lo-mvu32`, `san-mu-kd17`, `t10`, `sf277`; line-labels author-identified, table-lang lzh) | edition: cst-burmese].* [scholarship]

Whether each independently attaches that rice to a northern continent named Uttarakuru is a feature-level claim the linked table does not by itself settle. So the conservative coding is: the template is multi-recensional-pre-sectarian; the four-continent geographic frame is inherited-background and broadly attested in Sarvāstivāda Abhidharma and Chinese sources; and the precise welding of the Aggañña rice onto the named northern continent in this verse form is pali-plus-external-partial until a feature-level check on each witness is run. The study does not over-read "the Aggañña has Āgama parallels" into "the Uttarakuru ethnography is proven ancient in every detail."

### DN 32 Āṭānāṭiya is multi-recensional (the descriptive container travels)

The *described* place, Uttarakuru as a concrete locale with Mount Neru and the self-ripening rice, sits at `dn32`, the Āṭānāṭiya, a late paritta. The cross-recensional question is whether that descriptive container is Pāli-local or travels. The bridge resolves `dn32` to a Chinese parallel (`t1245`): the descriptive frame is pali-plus-external, not a Theravāda hapax [scholarship]. The reading this licenses is precise: the *descriptive* Uttarakuru, the one a reader pictures, is not a Pāli invention; it has an external witness. But its epistemic force is still flat-background inside a protective chant, never staked under the verification formula, and the external parallel does nothing to upgrade that force. Cross-recensional attestation buys antiquity, not epistemic warrant.

The soteriological load of the whole study rests on a single sutta. AN 9.21, the Tiṭhānasutta, is the triangular three-superiorities frame: the Uttarakurukas surpass the Tāvatiṃsa gods and the Jambudīpa humans in three respects (`amamā, apariggahā`, and the `niyatāyukā` / `visesaguṇā` pair counted as one particular), while the Jambudīpa humans surpass both in three respects of which the third is `idha brahmacariyavāso`, the holy life is lived *here* (`an9.21`) [canon]. This is the hinge on which the corrected headline turns: the most fortunate humans in the cosmos hold precisely the comforts of virtue, and the path is reserved for Jambudīpa.

The cross-recensional finding is that this load-bearing sutta has **no non-Pāli parallel** in the linked table, returning only a Pāli "mentions" relation. The correct code is therefore `cross-recensional-status: unconfirmed / pali-local-pending-verification`, never `pali-unique` and emphatically not `original`.

## Cosmology: a continent, not a realm

The most common error a modern reader brings to Uttarakuru is to file it with the heavens and hells, as one more *plane* in the vertical ladder of rebirth. The texts do not place it there. Uttarakuru is a **continent**, not a **realm**: the same human plane as ours, the people on it plain `manussā`, set off from us by an ocean and reached only by psychic power. This distinction is the hinge on which the epistemic finding turns, because the horizontal four-continent map and the vertical rebirth ladder are two different pieces of furniture, and only one of them carries the machinery of direct verification.

### The four mahādīpa as one world-system

The frame is canonical and early. AN 3.80 and AN 10.29 set Uttarakuru inside the four-continent schema as a bare member of a list: a thousand Jambudīpas, a thousand Aparagoyānas, a thousand Pubbavidehas, a thousand Uttarakurus (`an3.80`, `an10.29`) [canon]. Both rows code mūla / early-canonical; they hold the continent as a name in a comparison, nothing more, with no measure, shape, or ethnography at this stratum.

The four continents ring Mount Sineru (Neru) and together make one world-system, a `cakkavāḷa`. DN 32 gives the spatial setting in verse: *Yena uttarakuruvho, mahāneru sudassano; manussā tattha jāyanti, amamā apariggahā*, "where Uttarakuru is, and Mount Neru fair to see; humans are born there" (`dn32`) [late-canon]. The word that matters is `manussā`. Those born in Uttarakuru are *humans*, the same category-word the canon uses for us. They are not a deva-class, not a `peta` order, not a denizen of any vertical destiny. The continent is a region of the human plane (`manussa-loka`).

Measure and shape are not canonical. The 8,000-yojana figure is para-canonical, supplied by the Visuddhimagga (`cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`) [comm]; the square (`pīṭha`-shaped) continent, with inhabitants' faces matching the continent's outline, is later still in the sub-commentary (`cst-vin01t1.tik-9_p230`, `cst-e0103n.att-75_p096`) [comm]. The cosmography therefore deepens toward the late registers exactly as the soteriology does, and the reader should not let the Visuddhimagga's tidy yojana-table read back onto the Nikāya, which gives no number.

### Plane versus continent: the load-bearing distinction

The cleanest canonical statement that Uttarakuru is a continent on the human plane, not a realm above or below it, is AN 9.21, read here for its cosmological grammar. The sutta ranks three groups, and the three are not three rungs of a vertical ladder: the Uttarakuru humans (`uttarakurukā manussā`), the Tāvatiṃsa gods (`devā tāvatiṃsā`), and the Jambudīpa humans (`jambudīpakā manussā`) (`an9.21`) [canon]. Two of the three terms are `manussā`. The gods are named separately, as a contrasting class. The structure is one human plane spread across continents, with the deva-worlds stacked vertically above it, not Uttarakuru stacked anywhere. This is why the rebirth apparatus excludes the continents: the divine-eye formula verifies where beings are reborn by kamma, the vertical destinies, and the four-continent geography is a different axis of the cosmos, the horizontal one, which the formula never ranges over [late-canon → comm]. A reader who has collapsed continent into realm reads the absence as a puzzle; once the distinction is held, the absence is a category fact. This distinction rests where it is secure, on the `manussā` category-word shared by AN 9.21 and DN 32 and on the horizontal four-mahādīpa frame set against the vertical gati ladder, and not on the bodily-access motif treated next, which by itself does not cleanly separate continent from realm.

### Ocean-separated, crossed only by iddhi

If Uttarakuru is the same human plane, why can no one walk there? Because the continents are separated by `samudda`, the great ocean, and the crossing is made only by psychic power. This is the dominant thing the texts actually do with Uttarakuru, and it is canonical. In the Vinaya Mahākhandhaka the Buddha makes the crossing himself, brings back almsfood, and eats at Lake Anotatta (`pli-tv-kd1`) [late-canon]. In the Verañjā famine Moggallāna offers to take the whole Sangha there and the Buddha forbids it (`pli-tv-bu-vb-pj1`) [late-canon]. The Apadāna verse has arahants going there, "hard to reach in the quest for alms" (`tha-ap3`) [late-canon]. The motif is "a continent one *flies* to," not "a far country one walks to."

One honest qualification disarms an over-reading of this motif. It might seem that the bodily-travel question is asked of Uttarakuru *because* it is a continent and never of a heaven, so that the question itself sorts continents from realms. The Milinda does not support that clean a use. Milinda asks whether one could go *iminā ... kāyena*, "with this very body," and Nāgasena answers yes, by psychic mastery, but the question pairs Uttarakuru with the Brahma world in the same breath, *uttarakuruṃ vā ... brahmalokaṃ vā gaccheyya*, and Nāgasena affirms bodily travel to both (`mil3.7.9`) [late-canon]. So the iddhi-access motif is not by itself a continent-versus-realm discriminator: the text asks the bodily-travel question of a heaven too. The continent-versus-realm distinction therefore rests on the category-word `manussā` and the horizontal four-mahādīpa frame, as set out above, not on the bodily-access asymmetry, which `mil3.7.9` partly defeats. What the alms-flight material does establish is the weaker and secure point: Uttarakuru is a place behind an ocean on the same earth, reachable only by flying, and the texts use it as scene rather than staking it under any verification formula.

The wheel-treasure of the cakkavatti dramatises the same ocean. In DN 17 and DN 26 the divine wheel rolls to the eastern ocean and plunges in (`ogāhati`), then emerges, and rolls on to the four quarters, the monarch's dominion reaching to all four oceans (`dn17`, `dn26`) [canon]. The northern quarter the wheel conquers is named, by the commentary, as the 8,000-yojana Uttarakuru (`cst-s0102a.att-dn2_4_p026`) [comm]. The canon gives the ocean-bounded frame and the plunging wheel; the commentary fills in which continent the north is.

## The philology of the barrier: salt water, not space

A reader who meets Uttarakuru in English, already carrying a modern picture of worlds scattered through the void, is tempted to read the gap between the four continents as empty space and the continents as something like planets. The Pāli does not support that reading. The word that separates Uttarakuru from Jambudīpa is `samudda`, and `samudda` is salt water.

The continents sit *in* an ocean, not *across* a void. The grammar of "islands ringing a continent" (`cst-e0101n.mul-73_p057`) is the grammar of land in water. The morphology carries the same load: `samudda` is `saṃ-` (gathered) plus `udaka` (water), the gathered waters, and the canon's stock simile for the ocean is the one-taste-of-salt image, as the great ocean has but one taste, the taste of salt, so the Dhamma has but one taste, the taste of liberation [canon]. The verb the texts use for entering it confirms it once more: one does not cross the gap as one crosses open air; one *plunges into* it, `ogāhati`, the ordinary verb for wading or diving into water, as the wheel-treasure does (`dn17`) [late-canon]. Where a modern reader supplies "ocean of space," that is a translation-frame imported onto a Pāli that says `samudda` and means brine.

It would be wrong to say the tradition has no concept of empty dark space; it does, but that concept is a different word for a different gap. The empty-dark-space concept is the `lokantarikā`, the inter-world space, qualified as `aghā`, the dark voids *between* world-systems where the light of sun and moon does not reach, named in the birth-marvel pericope where a measureless light floods even the pitch-dark gulfs at the Bodhisatta's conception (the underlying frame early-canonical, the marvel-deployment late-canonical) [late-canon]. The two gaps are not the same gap. The `samudda` is the salt water *inside* one world-system, holding up the four continents around a single Sineru. The `lokantarikā aghā` is the dark void *between* whole world-systems. One is brine within a cosmos, the other darkness between cosmoses. To read the inter-continental barrier as "space" is to borrow the `aghā` concept and misapply it to the `samudda`, importing the right idea at the wrong scale.

One part of this cosmology genuinely reaches toward cosmic scale, and it must be credited precisely. The nested macro-structure of AN 3.80 sets a base unit of one sun and one moon, a `cakkavāḷa`; a thousand of those make a `sahassī cūḷanikā lokadhātu`; a thousand of those a millionfold system; a thousand again the billionfold, worlds nested by powers of a thousand (`an3.80`) [canon]. AN 10.29 enumerates the contents as "a thousand Uttarakurus, a thousand Jambudīpas" (`an10.29`) [canon]. This is part of the early-canonical seed, and it is the bare-name-in-a-list use the recall correction recovered: the short-*u* search dropped the long-*u* declined forms that carry this thousandfold cosmology, and only the honest stem caught them. Recovering it is a finding about the geometry of the cosmos *and* about search. But the credit stops at the right line. What anticipates cosmic scale is the macro-structure: many worlds nested in great multiples, dark space between them. What does *not* map onto a solar system is the local structure within any one `cakkavāḷa`: a central Sineru, four flat shaped continents in a salt sea, one disc-sun, Uttarakuru a square slab of self-ripening rice whose inhabitants' faces are square to match (`cst-vin01t1.tik-9_p230`, `cst-e0103n.att-75_p096`) [comm]. The macro-frame reaches toward cosmic scale; the local frame is inherited pan-Indian mythic geography that the commentary then measures, and that the texts never stake under any verification formula. A note on this section's own vocabulary: "salt water," "space," "cosmic scale," and "mythic geography" are modern analytic frames laid over a Pāli that says `samudda`, `aghā`, `lokadhātu`, and `cakkavāḷa`; the argument is that the Pāli words draw a line the modern words blur.

## The ethnography under the signature

The earlier reading scored each ethnographic feature canonical-seed, commentarial-detail, or commentarial-innovation. The recoding adds the chronological stratum of the warrant, the cross-recensional reach of the template, the pre-Buddhist provenance of the furniture, and the epistemic force of each claim. The picture is not "a canonical skeleton, a commentarial body." It is a thin early-canonical seed, a late-canonical materialisation of that seed into a described and visited place, and a commentarial measurement that supplies almost all the vivid detail. Across the whole ethnography one epistemic constant holds: the texts state it flat, as background, never under the verification formula.

**The self-ripening rice (A2).** The strongest early seed, and where the cross-recensional control fires hardest. The DN 32 property (`dn32`: *Na te bījaṃ pavapanti ... The Uttarakuru rice is a redeployment of a stock golden-age template, not an independent datum; the commentary confirms the reuse by glossing it with Aggañña's own words (`cst-s0103t.tik-dn3_9_p023`) [comm]. ## The soteriological bar: automatic virtue and the closing of Uttarakuru

The whole study turns on one move. The canon ranks the Uttarakurukas above the gods and above us in three respects (`an9.21`) and reserves the holy life for Jambudīpa with a bare deictic, *idha brahmacariyavāso* (`an9.21`). What the canon never does is say *why* the northern continent is shut out, or name the disability that shuts it. That naming is the work of two features, the bar (B3) and automatic-virtue (B4), and reading them together shows a trajectory the single axis could not see: not a lenient canon hardened by a harsh commentary, but a canon that *debates and partly softens* the question for the gods, and a commentary that *re-targets and hardens* it against Uttarakuru.

### The deictic and its narrowing (B2)

The canon (`an9.21`, early-canonical buddha-vacana) supplies only the bare *idha brahmacariyavāso*, the holy life is lived *here*. The narrowing of "here" to Jambudīpa, on the ground that Buddhas and Paccekabuddhas arise only there, and the inference "therefore no path in Uttarakuru," is classical-commentary explication (Mp-a, *jambudīpe buddhapaccekabuddhānaṃ uppajjanato*, `cst-s0404a.att-an9_p063`) [comm]. Split: early-canonical deictic seed, commentarial geographic pin. [canon → comm]

### The category is canonical; the identification is not (B3)

The disqualifying vocabulary is old and canonical, but it is Abhidhamma-canonical, not Nikāya-early, and it is not spoken of Uttarakuru. The `acchandika` ("lacking the wholesome desire-to-act") and `abhabba niyāmaṃ okkamituṃ` ("incapable of descending into the path's certainty") category lives in the Kathāvatthu's Suddhabrahmacariya-kathā (`kv1.3` / `cst-abh03m3.mul-026`). Its controverted point is *natthi devesu brahmacariyavāso*, is there no holy life among the *gods*? The proponent deploys the `acchandika / abhabba` list inside a *reductio*: if you deny the gods the holy life you are forced into the absurdity of calling them all desireless, incapable, matricides. The Kathāvatthu then *softens* AN 9.21's `idha` for the devas, since a non-returner reborn in the Pure Abodes completes the path *there*. The debate leaves Uttarakuru standing on the no-`brahmacariya` side, but the relief it carves is for the gods, and the Uttarakurukas are never named in it.

> *[stratum: abhidhamma-canonical | layer: mūla | speaker: scholastic dispute | register: scholastic-polemic | epistemic: debated-controverted | harmonization: reconciled-by-reductio, softens idha for the devas | recension: untested]. Warrant: `kv1.3` / `cst-abh03m3.mul-026`, layer and stratum disagreeing.* [late-canon]

The structured-absence control confirms the identification is supplied from outside the canon: the eight-`akkhaṇa` list (`an8.29`, `dn33`) names hell, animal, ghost, long-lived deva, border-region, wrong-view, dullness, no-Buddha, and does **not** name Uttarakuru; the six mūla rows where `akkhaṇa` and `uttarakuru` co-occur have gaps of 930 to 126,000 chars, distance artifacts [canon, SQL-confirmed]. The nearest canonical foothold is the long-lived-deva slot, a being so comfortable that urgency never arises, and it is by analogy to *that* that the commentary later assimilates the Uttarakurukas.

The identification, "these people *are* in the `acchandika` class," is first stated positively in the Aṭṭhakathā. Vibh-a §129.14 (`cst-abh02a.att-129_p014`): *Acchandikāti kattukamyatākusalacchandarahitā. Uttarakurukā manussā acchandikaṭṭhānaṃ paviṭṭhā ... Abhabbā niyāmaṃ okkamituṃ* [comm]. That sentence is the hinge: the canonical category (from `kv1.3`) and the canonical name (from `an9.21`) are joined for the first time, and the joining is commentarial. The recurrence of the gloss across the network (Sv-pṭ, Ps-pṭ, Spk-pṭ, Mp-pṭ, KN-a) is one stock formula deployed many times, not many independent endorsements.

The sub-commentary then makes the move that gives the study its shape. Where the Kathāvatthu had *softened* "here" for the gods, Abh-pṭ §73.3 re-hardens it against Uttarakuru: *uttarakurukānaṃ pana visesānadhigamabhāvo* (`cst-abh05t.nrf-73_p003`), and Vism-mhṭ §67.11 groups them *mārādayo viya*, like Māra (`cst-e0103n.att-67_p011`) [comm]. The devas keep the path-relief the Kathāvatthu granted them; Uttarakuru is denied it in the same breath. The trajectory is canon-debates-and-softens (for the gods) → commentary-identifies → sub-commentary-re-hardens (against Uttarakuru) [late-canon → comm]. A flat mūla-versus-attha split averages this into "the commentary supplies the verdict" and loses the direction of travel.

### Why the bar is "automatic virtue cannot be cultivated" (B4)

B4 is the thematic key that makes the bar intelligible rather than arbitrary. The canon gives only the *fact*: the Uttarakurukas are `amamā, apariggahā` (`an9.21`, `dn32`). They do not transgress because there is nothing to transgress over and no craving to drive a transgression. The Visuddhimagga reclassifies that fact as a kind of virtue: *Uttarakurukānaṃ manussānaṃ avītikkamo pakatisīlaṃ*, their non-transgression is natural virtue (`cst-e0101n.mul-6_p029`) [comm]. The sub-commentary specifies "natural" and draws the fatal consequence: *Tatrūpapattiniyataṃ hi sīlaṃ uttarakurukānaṃ*, their virtue is fixed by being reborn there (`cst-e0103n.att-7_p020`), framed as `dhammatā-siddha`, accomplished by nature (`cst-s0103t.tik-dn3_9_p025`) [comm].

The link is the commentary's own logic, assembled across strata. Virtue that is `tatrupapatti-niyata` (fixed by rebirth) and `dhammatā-siddha` (accomplished by nature) is virtue that is never *undertaken*: there is no `samādāna`, no act of taking-on, because there is nothing to take on. A path is a thing one *cultivates* (`bhāveti`): it requires the very `chanda`, the wholesome desire-to-act, that the `acchandika` designation denies them. So a perfection that arrives automatically with the address is a perfection that cannot be made into a path. The fortune is the disability. The reasoning runs **effortless ⇒ uncultivable ⇒ pathless**, and every step after the first is supplied above the early-canonical line. (The syllogism is the commentary's reasoning, reconstructed: the texts supply `pakati-sīla`, `tatrupapatti-niyata`, `dhammatā-siddha`, and `acchandika / abhabba` as separate glosses; the inference that joins them into "fortune ⇒ disability" is the present author's synthesis of the commentary's parts, flagged as such.) [scholarship over comm]

### Fixed heaven-destiny (B5)

No canonical passage predicates heaven-destiny of the Uttarakurukas (`sagga/devaloka` + `uttarakuru` in true-canon = 5 rows, smallest gap 4,302 chars, spurious) [comm, SQL-confirmed]. The earliest warrant is Mp-a, *tato cavitvā saggeyeva nibbattanti* (`cst-s0404a.att-an9_p063`) [comm], with a sub-commentarial supply at Sv-pṭ (`cst-s0103t.tik-dn3_9_p025`). The same scholastic tradition then guards its own supply: Pañca-a §31.23 distinguishes this "fixed destiny" from the technical niyata-class of the ariyas and anantarika-doers, *na sa niyatadhammavasena* (`cst-abh03a.att-31_p023`) [comm]. Commentarial, with a self-correcting scholastic guard.

## Disambiguation, pre-Buddhist provenance, and reception

### Disambiguation: three referents named "kuru"

The bare token `kuru` blurs three referents, and the disambiguation is itself a finding. There is the continent Uttarakuru; there is the Kuru janapada (976 bare-`kuru` rows, set aside); and there is the `kuru-vatta` / `kuru-dhamma` observance (26 rows, set aside). The tradition itself fuses the three by a commentarial genealogy (C1), and that fusion has no canonical warrant. The earliest warrant is classical-commentary (Sv-a, Ps-a: the Mandhātu legend settles Uttarakuru migrants who name the Kuru country) and sub-commentary (Sv-pṭ fuses their five natural precepts into the `kuru-vatta`, illustrated by the Kurudhamma Jātaka) (`cst-s0103a.att-dn3_9`, `cst-s0103t.tik-dn3_9`) [comm]. The disambiguation IS the finding: the tradition harmonizes its own homonyms by deriving the janapada and its observance from continent-migrants. Flat etymological narration. *[stratum: classical-commentary | layer: attha + tika | epistemic: flat-background | harmonization: reconciles-a-tension by genealogy | recension: untested].* [comm]

### Pre-Buddhist provenance

Two provenance verdicts must not be conflated. The *place*, Uttarakuru as a blessed northern land, one of four continents around a central mountain, is inherited pan-Indian and pre-Buddhist cosmological furniture; it appears in Vedic/epic geography and shares the Hyperborean type. The four-mahādīpa schema that frames the whole ethnography is the same inherited skeleton, and Uttarakuru is the same human plane (`manussa-loka`) as ours, a different *continent*, not a different *realm* (`an9.21`). The golden-age rice-template is likewise inherited and then repurposed (`dn27`, multi-recensional). But the *ethnographic detail* the commentary hangs on this inherited place, the wish-tree slot, the perpetual youth, the communal children, the thousand-year number, the eightfold hair, is not inherited furniture and not canonical; it is Pāli-commentarial elaboration. The inherited skeleton cannot bear the weight of "Buddhism teaches the Uttarakuru ethnography"; what Buddhism *added* is the soteriological reading of that ethnography, not the ethnography itself. [scholarship]

### Reception

Two standing reception cautions. First, the standard English renders `lokadhātu` (world-system / world-element) as "galaxy," a modern frame the Pāli does not carry; the nested macro-structure superficially resembles a plurality of worlds in dark space, but the Pāli word is "world-system," not "galaxy" (`an3.80` rendered through Sujato) [reception]. Second, the verification formula is the warrant, quoted from the Pāli (`dibbena cakkhunā`, `abbhaññāsiṃ`); the English "I have seen / directly knew" is gloss, not the warrant, and the structured-absence finding rests on the Pāli marker's fixed object, not on any translator's word-choice. The analytic vocabulary of this paper, "salt water," "space," "cosmic scale," "mythic geography," "plane versus realm," is named throughout as a modern frame laid over `samudda`, `aghā`, `lokadhātu`, `cakkavāḷa`. [scholarship]

## Discussion

The corrected headline is sharper than its predecessor, and the sharpening comes from refusing to let one axis stand for the whole provenance question. On the structural axis alone, Uttarakuru reads as a small canonical frame built out by a large commentary. That sentence is true, and it conceals three facts that change its meaning.

First, the materialisation is internal to the canon, on the corpus's stratum map and subject to the circularity caveat. The place a reader pictures, located, peopled, visited, is not the commentary's gift; it is already present in the late-canonical containers, in the Āṭānāṭiya paritta and the Vinaya frame-narratives, before any aṭṭhakathā measures it. The commentary's distinctive contribution is *measurement*, the 8,000 yojanas, the thousand years, the eightfold-split hair, the metrological impulse that turns a described place into a furnished one. The named-to-described-to-visited-to-measured gradient tracks lateness on the consensus map and crosses the canon-commentary boundary in the middle, not at the start. Whether that gradient reflects a change in the canon's *conception* of the place or only a change in which registers had occasion to describe it is not settled by relative stratigraphy, because the stratum map sorts works by register cues that themselves co-vary with concreteness; the finding is register-relative, and the two pivotal "late" assignments (DN 32's paritta label, the Vinaya nidāna's position label) are coded contested-confidence for exactly that reason.

Second, the deepening is on a different axis from the verification. The continent becomes more concrete without ever becoming more warranted. The canon reserves its strongest epistemic mark, directly-seen-with-the-divine-eye, for the vertical kamma-and-rebirth axis it actually teaches, and lets the horizontal map stand as unexamined background. The structured absence, present by name and at full ladder depth across every rebirth-and-opportunity frame and not only the divine eye, is the strongest form of the epistemic finding: it is a measured zero inside a climbed ladder, with a sharp and densely-attested contrast class in its strong leg (the divine eye, 124 attestations, object always rebirth-by-kamma) and a thinner leg in its weakest (the rare-human-birth / Suddhāvāsa frame, pure name-absence), licensing the bounded inference that the tradition assumes the geography rather than staking it, and licensing nothing stronger.

Third, the one genuinely distinctive move in the Pāli treatment is soteriological, not ethnographic, and it is late and local. The ethnography is inherited pan-Indian and pan-Buddhist furniture, the rice-template attested across recensions before the schools divided. What Theravāda did with that furniture, turning the cosmos's most fortunate humans into its worst-placed for awakening by reasoning from an effortless virtue to an uncultivable one, has on the linked evidence no non-Pāli parallel, with the standing caveat that the likeliest parallel sits in the Abhidharma and cosmology genre that the sutta-to-sutta link table under-covers, so the code is pali-local-pending-verification rather than any cross-Buddhism origination claim. Its sharpest form is commentarial. The trajectory is not lenient-canon-to-harsh-commentary; it is a canon that debates and softens the question for the gods, and a sub-commentary that re-hardens it against Uttarakuru exactly where the canon had relieved the devas. That direction of travel is invisible to a structural split and visible only once the chronological, epistemic, and harmonization axes are coded independently.

The methodological lesson is that "canon versus commentary" is one projection of a richer object, and that for any question whose load rests on *when* (chronological stratum), *how verified* (epistemic marking), *how original* (cross-recensional reach), or *how patterned its silences are* (the structured-absence protocol), the single projection will mislead in a predictable direction: it will credit to the commentary what the late canon already did, and it will read flat assertion as either doctrine or denial. Those four are the load-bearing axes named in the triage, and they are the four the signature recovers the gradient with.

## Limitations

The study carries six honest limitations, each stated rather than smoothed.

The chronological gradient is partly definitional and cannot by itself prove a diachrony of conception. The work-to-stratum map sorts works by genre and register cues, terse list, paritta chant, Vinaya nidāna-narrative, scholastic measurement, that co-vary with concreteness, so part of the lateness-tracks-concreteness correlation is baked into how the strata were drawn. The data are equally consistent with old cosmological material surviving in registers that never had occasion to describe the place, in which case the canon's *conception* of Uttarakuru need not have changed at all. Two pivotal "late" assignments are genre or position labels silently converted into dates and are coded contested-confidence: DN 32 is "late" by its *paritta* register, the Vinaya alms-flight by its *nidāna* position, and a late container can recite older cosmological content (the same template-versus-placement split the paper applies to the A2 rice). The "6 early" floor is also not homogeneous: AN 3.80 and AN 10.29's thousandfold nested cosmology is plausibly a later systematization than AN 9.21's bare comparison, so concreteness may already rise within the early rows. The "deepening begins inside the late canon, before any commentary measures it" claim is therefore demoted to a register-relative statement about the corpus's stratum map, not a demonstrated diachrony.

The cross-recensional finding is link-level, not feature-level. The corpus bridges out through a `passage_parallels` table, not through the external texts themselves. "No linked parallel" for AN 9.21 is evidence about the link table, not affirmative evidence about the Āgama traditions; and because that table is sutta-to-sutta, it under-covers exactly the Abhidharma and cosmology genre (Sarvāstivāda Lokaprajñapti, Abhidharmakośa cosmology) in which an Uttarakuru three-distinctions analogue would most plausibly sit, so the `unconfirmed` code reflects a known genre blind-spot rather than a positive empty search. The verdict is coded `pali-local-pending-verification`, never `original`, and the one distinctive move is called distinctive within the Pāli treatment, not original across Buddhism. A second labeling caveat lives here too: the source-tradition names attached to the linked Aggañña witnesses (Lokottaravāda-Mahāsāṃghika for `san-lo-mvu32`, Mūlasarvāstivāda for `san-mu-kd17`, "Sanskrit fragment" for `sf277`) and the count of "independent lines" are the present author's identification of the SuttaCentral ids, not the corpus link table's own coding, which tags all four witnesses `parallel_lang = lzh`; the link evidence carries the existence of a parallel, not its recension or source-language. The split, template-pan-Buddhist versus verdict-Pāli-local, is the strongest available reading under the linked evidence and would be hardened or dissolved by a staged Āgama-text pass that reads `t1245`, the Mahāvastu and Mūlasarvāstivāda Aggañña parallels, the Saṃyukta/Ekottara Āgamas, and the Sarvāstivāda cosmology texts directly.

The char-gap evidence is edition-relative. The gaps (4,909–227,578 for the divine eye; 930–126,000 for the akkhaṇa; ~21,000 for the wish-tree) are computed over CST monolithic rows as ingested; under a finer segmentation the absolute numbers change. What does not change is the structural fact the gaps stand for: in no edition does the verification pericope take the continents as its object, and in no edition is Uttarakuru a member of the eight-akkhaṇa list. The numbers are edition-relative; the boundaries are edition-neutral.

The "automatic virtue cannot be cultivated" syllogism is reconstructed. The texts supply `pakati-sīla`, `tatrupapatti-niyata`, `dhammatā-siddha`, and `acchandika / abhabba` as separate glosses across strata; the inference that joins them into "fortune ⇒ disability" is the present author's synthesis of the commentary's parts, and is flagged as scholarship over commentary rather than presented as a single canonical or commentarial statement.

The chronological stratigraphy is relative, not absolute. It rests on consensus Pāli philological grounds (Āṭānāṭiya, Apadāna, and Vinaya-nidāna membership as late-canonical warrants, the Kathāvatthu as a parallel late Abhidhamma branch). It imports no absolute dates, and where a stratum is contested the census codes it explicitly. The residual coding seams (A10's attribution, the A2 template-versus-placement stratum, and the AN 3.80/AN 10.29-versus-AN 9.21 ordering inside the early floor) are reported as visible seams in a stratified object, not resolved by fiat.

The analytic vocabulary is a reception layer. "Ethnography," "cosmology," "plane versus realm," "salt water versus space," "literal versus symbolic" are modern frames laid over the Pāli, named as such throughout. The base edition is CST/VRI; the diacritic convention follows the corpus; where the corpus carries no English the rendering is the present author's gloss checked against the standard translations, with load-bearing terms quoted in Pāli.
