# Uttarakuru in the Pāli Canon and Commentaries: how the northern continent is described, and what the texts decline to verify about it

## Abstract

North of the world we stand on, in the geography the Pāli texts take for granted, lies Uttarakuru: a continent where no one owns anything, where rice ripens unsown, and where people live a fixed, untroubled span. It is easy to file this with the heavens and hells, but the texts place it on the human plane, a far country reached only across an ocean. This survey gathers every passage in the corpus that names the continent and asks where, across the layers of the literature, the picture of it actually sits, and how firmly the texts commit to it. The shape that emerges qualifies the familiar division between a thin canon and a thick commentary. The picture grows more concrete as the texts grow later, the deepening already under way inside the late canon rather than waiting for the commentary; the geography is stated as assumed background and never staked under the canon's own test of directly-verified knowledge; and the ethnography is inherited and broadly shared, while the soteriological judgement built on it appears, on the evidence to hand, only in the Pāli. The paper describes that shape and marks the limits of saying so.

---

## I. The northern continent and the question to put to it

In the world-picture the Pāli texts take for granted, the earth we stand on is not the whole of the human world. It is one of four great continents (`mahādīpa`, "great continent") set around the central mountain Sineru, and the one to the north is Uttarakuru: a land where no one owns anything, where rice ripens of itself without ploughing, and where people live out a fixed, untroubled span and then pass on. It is easy, reading this in English, to file Uttarakuru with the heavens and hells, as one more rung in the vertical ladder of rebirth. The texts do not place it there. Uttarakuru is a continent, not a realm: the same human plane as ours, its people called by the plain word for human, set off from us only by an ocean that ordinary feet cannot cross.[^1] It is, in short, a fortunate country, and the most striking thing the tradition will eventually say about it is that its very good fortune is what shuts its people out of the path to awakening.

The question I want to put to this material is narrow. It is not whether Uttarakuru is real, a question the texts do not argue and I do not raise. It is where, across the layers of the literature, the picture of the continent actually sits, and how firmly the texts commit to it. An earlier description of this material read it on a single contrast: a thin canonical sketch, filled out by a thick commentary, with the commentary supplying the sharp soteriological verdict.[^2] That reading is faithful on its own terms. What it could not see is that the structural label "canonical" marks a position in the edited corpus, not a date, and that the canon is itself layered. Two readings then stand against each other. The one the data led me toward is that the literal-place reading deepens as the texts grow later, the deepening already beginning inside the late canon, while the texts' epistemic stance stays flat throughout: the geography is stated as assumed background and never once staked under the canon's own test of directly-verified knowledge. The plainer expectation, which I take as the null, is the earlier single-contrast picture, a thin canonical block built out by commentary, with the dividing line falling neatly at the canon-to-commentary boundary. What follows sorts and describes a marker-bounded count; it is a descriptive finding about relative textual strata, not a dated proof, and where it qualifies the earlier impression it says so quietly.[^3]

The first thing the count shows is that the canonical label conceals a split. Of the twenty-six structurally canonical rows that name the continent, only six belong to the early canon; the other twenty carry the canonical tag while reading late-canonical, paracanonical, or commentary-era by composition.[^4]

> **6 of 26**: structurally canonical rows that are early-canonical. The other twenty are canonical by shelving but later by composition, and that disagreement is the finding.

This study proceeds as follows:

- **II. What kind of place Uttarakuru is, and how it was found.** The doctrinal ground (a continent on the human plane, not a realm) and a short note on how thoroughly the corpus was searched for it.
- **III. A place that deepens with lateness.** How the picture of the continent thickens across the layers, from a name in a list to a measured place, and the caveat that bounds that gradient.
- **IV. What the canon stakes as verified, and what it merely assumes.** The one test the canon applies to knowledge it has seen for itself, and the fact that the geography is never placed under it.
- **V. An inherited template and a Pāli-local verdict.** How much of the Uttarakuru material is shared across Buddhist traditions, and how much is, so far, found only in Pāli.
- **VI. What this means, and what it does not.** Why a late and assumed geography matters for how the continent is read, and the limits of saying so.

Before tracing how the continent changes across those layers, it is worth being clear about what kind of place the texts take it to be, and how thoroughly they were searched for it; to that I turn first.

## II. What kind of place Uttarakuru is, and how it was found

The most common error a modern reader brings to Uttarakuru is to imagine it as a separate world floating somewhere in space, or as one more destiny in the cycle of rebirth alongside the heavens and the hells. The texts draw both lines differently. Uttarakuru is one of four continents set in a single ocean around one central mountain, and the four together make up one world-system. Crucially, the people born there are `manussā`, "humans," the very word the texts use for us; in the comparison that ranks them, the gods are named as a separate, contrasting class, never folded in with the continent-dwellers.[^5] So the cosmos the early texts describe has a horizontal axis and a vertical one. The horizontal axis is the spread of the human plane across the four continents in their salt sea; the vertical axis is the ladder of rebirth-destinies, the heavens stacked above and the lower states below. Uttarakuru sits on the horizontal axis. It is a far country on the same earth, reached only by psychic power across the water, and this placement, not any later detail, is what the early canon actually warrants.

What the early texts give, then, is spare:

- a named entry in a list of four continents, set inside the larger nested cosmology of a thousand such world-systems;[^6]
- humans (`manussā`), not a class of gods or of any other rebirth-destiny;
- a people without possessions and with a fixed life-span;
- and, at this stratum, no measure, no figure for the span, no shape, and no location more precise than "by Mount Neru."

Everything more concrete than this, the eight-thousand-yojana width, the square shape, the exact thousand-year life, belongs to the later layers, and the reader should be careful not to let the later tidiness read back onto the early texts, which give no number.[^7]

The plainest search, an exact phrase the way a non-specialist would type it, recovers fifteen rows; a glyph- and inflection-tolerant search recovers a hundred and forty-four; the honest stem on the word recovers a hundred and sixty-one.[^9] The first, narrowest attempt misses most of the continent, and not because the material is absent: it slides off the long declined forms that carry the four-continent cosmology, the very forms an early pass had silently dropped. ## III. A place that deepens with lateness

*What the data say about age, in one line: the picture of the continent is thin in the early texts and grows steadily more concrete as the texts grow later.*

### III.1 The early seeds

The early-canonical floor is small, six rows in all, and it is not uniform.[^13] Three discourses carry it, each attested twice in the corpus, once under its SuttaCentral handle and once under its CST sibling. In two of the three the name does the least possible work. The Cūḷanikāsutta and the Paṭhamakosalasutta (AN 3.80, AN 10.29) are the great nested-cosmology lists, where Uttarakuru is one entry in an enumeration: a thousand Uttarakurus alongside a thousand Jambudīpas and the rest, the northern continent named off as one of the four and nothing more.[^14] It is a name in a list, not a described place.

The third discourse is different in kind. The Tiṭhānasutta (AN 9.21) is an early-canonical comparison of three groups, and it already carries the soteriological asymmetry on which the whole question turns. The Uttarakuru humans surpass both the gods of the Thirty-Three and the humans of our own continent in three respects, while our continent surpasses both in three respects of which the third is `idha brahmacariyavāso`, "the holy life is lived here."[^15] Yet even here the inhabitants are given only bare predicate-words, "without mine-making, without possessions, of fixed life-span, of distinctive quality," and the span, though fixed, is given no figure.[^16] So the early canon offers a named continent, a propertyless people, a fixed-but-unmeasured life, and the bare soteriological ranking. Nothing is located precisely, measured, visited, or described.

### III.2 The gradient

Above that floor the literal-place reading thickens, and on the corpus's stratum map it begins to thicken before any commentary speaks. The deepening runs through four rungs:

- a **named** entry in the four-continent list, in early Nikāya prose;
- a **described** place, located by Mount Neru and peopled by humans who eat rice that ripens unsown, in a late protective chant (the Āṭānāṭiya, DN 32);[^17]
- a **visited** place the Buddha flies to for alms, in late verse and in the Vinaya's frame-narratives;[^18]
- and a **measured** place, eight thousand yojanas across, each continent ringed by five hundred small islands, in the para-canonical Visuddhimagga.ᶜ[^19]

The exact figure for the life-span follows the same path one layer further out: the canon's bare "fixed span" is glossed as "settled at just a thousand years" only in the commentary, and an adversarial check confirms the number is not hiding in the canon, since the one canonical row that pairs "a thousand years" with the continent assigns the thousand years to a flowering creeper, not to the people.ᶜ[^20] What the early text leaves open, the later text closes to a figure.

### III.3 What the gradient licenses, and what it does not

Three honesty guards bound this reading.

First, the gradient is relative stratigraphy on consensus philological grounds, the late membership of the Āṭānāṭiya, the Apadāna, and the Vinaya frame-narratives; it imports no absolute dates, and where a stratum is contested the census codes it so rather than averaging it into "the canon."

Second, "deepens with lateness" is a statement about the literal-place reading, not a claim that the early canon doubted the continent or that the late canon invented it. The early seed is genuinely present, only thin and unmeasured.

Third, and most cautiously, the correlation between lateness and concreteness is partly definitional, and cannot by itself prove that the canon's conception of the continent changed over time. The map that sorts works into strata leans on genre and register cues, terse list against protective chant against frame-narrative against scholastic measurement, and those very cues co-vary with concreteness: a text that measures the place is sorted late partly because it measures. So the gradient is, in part, a restatement of how the strata were defined, and the data are equally consistent with a second reading. On that reading the canon's conception of Uttarakuru need not have changed at all; old material simply survived in registers (terse lists, a three-way comparison) that never had occasion to describe the place, while the work of describing it belongs to register-types (the protective chant, the occasioning-narrative, the scholastic measurement) that happen to cluster later in the consensus map. Two of the pivotal "late" assignments are exactly where this bites, and both are coded at lower confidence: DN 32 is "late" by a chant-genre label, and the Vinaya alms-flight is "late" by its position in an occasioning-frame, and a genre or position label is not the same thing as a date.[^21] Even the six-row early floor may not be uniform, since the nested thousandfold cosmology of AN 3.80 and AN 10.29 is plausibly a later systematisation than the bare comparison of AN 9.21, so concreteness may already be rising within the early rows. The honest statement is therefore register-relative: on the corpus's stratum map the picture of the continent is thinnest in the terse early registers and thickest in the late measuring ones, and the materialisation an earlier reading credited to the commentary is already present in late-canonical containers; whether that reflects a change in the canon's conception, or only a change in which registers had occasion to describe, is not decidable from relative stratigraphy alone.

So the place grows more concrete as the texts grow later. What is striking is what does not grow alongside it: at no rung does the canon submit the geography to its own test of knowledge. To that asymmetry I now turn.

## IV. What the canon stakes as verified, and what it merely assumes

*What the rows show here, before any detail: the picture of Uttarakuru grows more concrete as the texts grow later, yet at no point in that growth does the canon submit the geography to its own test of knowledge.*

The most consequential thing the canon does with Uttarakuru is not to describe it. It is the thing the canon never does. The texts run an elaborate machinery for grading where a being is reborn and for marking whether such knowledge is directly seen or merely assumed, and the northern continent is never once placed inside that machinery as a thing that has been verified. It is stated, declaratively, as background furniture, and left there. The continent becomes walkable, measurable, peopled, and ranked across the layers, and through all of it the geography keeps the same flat epistemic key. Across the rows, the texts do four distinct things with Uttarakuru, and only the fourth is the one that would carry weight:

- they state it flat, as assumed background (the place, the four-continent list, the self-ripening rice, the ownerlessness, the fixed lifespan), which is the default;
- they use it as the vehicle of a simile (the "like Uttarakuru" climate comparison, where the continent illustrates something about the Kuru country and asserts nothing about itself);[^22]
- they narrate it as a reported event or a posed question (the Buddha's alms-flight, the Milinda traveller's question);
- and, under the canon's own formula for directly-verified knowledge, never.

### IV.1 The formula and its fixed object

The canon has a precise marker for a claim staked as seen rather than supposed: the divine-eye pericope, in which the speaker reports seeing, with the purified eye that surpasses the human, beings passing away and being reborn, "faring according to their deeds."[^23] The object of that seeing is fixed and it is vertical: who is reborn where, low or high, fair or foul, by their conduct. The formula does not range over geography. Its companion phrasings, the having-realised-by-my-own-direct-knowledge, the first-person "I directly knew," the "knows as it really is," are the canon's vocabulary for the same act of staking a claim as known.[^24] The pericope is densely attested, on the order of 124 times across the corpus, and in all of them its slot is rebirth-by-kamma. It verifies where beings go. It never verifies where the continents are.

The honest objection is that the divine eye and Uttarakuru might still turn up in the same passage, and that such a meeting would license reading the continent as verified. That is the proximity illusion, and the guard against it is to require the marker and the geographic claim inside one window of text. Five canonical rows carry both the divine-eye formula and the name Uttarakuru. In every one of them the two sit far apart, the nearest separated by nearly five thousand characters and the farthest by more than two hundred thousand; the divine eye is verifying rebirth-destiny in one pericope while the continent sits chapters away in the same edited volume.[^25] There is no window in which the formula takes the continent as its object. The result, coded as a positive finding and not as an unsearched residue, is a measured zero.

> **0**: claims about the continent's geography placed under the canon's formula for directly-verified knowledge, across roughly 124 uses of that formula.

### IV.2 The patterned silence

The sharpest evidence for this reading is not anything the texts say but a thing they decline to do, repeated across every frame where one might expect the continent to appear. The tradition asks, in several distinct registers, where beings are reborn and whether that reckoning is verified, and Uttarakuru is absent by name, at full search depth, from each of them. Five such silences carry the finding, and they are not of equal strength.[^27]

- The strongest is the divine eye itself. The formula is densely attested, its object is always rebirth-by-kamma and never the four continents, so it furnishes a positive contrast class against which the continent's exclusion reads as a measured zero rather than a gap in the search.
- The eight inopportune births, the list of conditions under which the holy life cannot be led, is a finite enumerated set, and Uttarakuru simply is not one of its members; the disqualification a reader may have in mind is supplied later, by the commentary, not by this canonical list.[^28]
- The category of those who lack the wholesome desire-to-act and are incapable of entering the path is canonical, but it lives in a scholastic debate that deploys it against the gods, in a reductio, and never names the Uttarakurukas; the category is canonical, the identification is commentarial.[^29]
- No canonical row predicates a fixed heaven-destiny of them; that destiny, like the identification above, is the commentary's supply.[^30]
- The thinnest is the rare-human-birth and Pure-Abodes frame, which returns pure name-absence with no positive contrast class, so it is rested on least.

The bound on all this must be stated carefully, because the silence is easy to over-read. What the patterned absence licenses is the modest inference that the tradition treats the continental geography as assumed background rather than as directly-verified knowledge, and that its rebirth-opportunity reckoning is absent, by name and at full search depth, from every canonical rebirth-destiny frame. It does not license the stronger and false claim that the canon denies Uttarakuru, doubts it, or disqualifies it. The divine-eye formula is silent on the continents, not negative about them; the inopportune-births list omits the continent, it does not exclude it by name; the heaven-destiny is unstated in the canon, not refused. The numbers behind the silences are edition-relative, computed over the rows as ingested; what does not shift between editions is the structural fact they stand for, that in no recension does the verification pericope take the four continents as its object.[^31]

### IV.3 The apparent counter-case

One canonical verse looks, at first, like a direct contradiction of this whole reading, and it is worth naming and disarming rather than leaving for an objector. In a verse of remembered deeds the speaker says he was reborn "a thousand times in the Kurus," long-lived and possessionless: a rebirth into the place inside exactly the destiny frame the silence says never names the continent. The disarming turns on the surface word. The verse says `kurūsu`, "in the Kurus," and that locative defaults to the Kuru country of the Indian plain, the *janapada*, not to the northern continent; the reading of it as Uttarakuru is supplied only by the commentary and the sub-commentary, which gloss the word into the continent.[^32] So the canon does not name Uttarakuru in a rebirth-into frame at all. The commentary reads the continent into an ambiguous place-name, which is itself a second instance of the pattern running through this study, the later layer supplying what the canon leaves bare. The verse sits among the many bare-`kurūsu` country references set aside at the outset, and it is set aside here on the same ground.

In sum, the continent becomes more concrete without ever becoming more warranted: the texts assume the geography rather than staking it, and the one verse that seems to stake a rebirth there turns out to name a country, not the continent. That leaves the sharpest question of all. Of everything the Pāli texts say about Uttarakuru, how much is theirs to begin with? Here a template and a verdict come apart.

## V. An inherited template and a Pāli-local verdict

*What the rows show here, before the detail: the picture of the place is shared furniture, attested across traditions before the schools divided, while the particular judgement the Pāli draws from it has, on the evidence to hand, no parallel outside the Pāli.*

The question this section puts is not when the Uttarakuru material entered the Pāli line but whether it entered that line at all, or whether instead it is shared inheritance held in common with the other early Buddhist traditions. The proxy for that, short of reading every recension directly, is attestation in lines independent of the Theravāda: a formulation found in a Chinese Āgama, a Sanskrit fragment, or one of the non-Pāli Vinayas plausibly predates the sectarian split, while a Pāli-only formulation is, at best, undated. The corpus carries a table of such cross-references, and the rows below lean on it. Three claim-clusters sort out unevenly across that evidence:

- the ethnographic template, the propertyless people who eat rice that ripens unsown, which is inherited and attested across recensions;
- the described place, Uttarakuru as a concrete locale with its mountain and its rice, which also travels, with an external witness;
- and the soteriological verdict, the judgement that closes the path to the continent's inhabitants, which has, in the linked record, no non-Pāli parallel at all.

One caution applies to every witness named below and is repeated in the limitations. The source-tradition labels attached to the linked references, the names of the schools and recensions, are my own identification of the corpus's reference ids, not the link table's own coding; the table records that a parallel exists and tags its language, not which recension it belongs to. The link carries the existence of a parallel, not its lineage.[^33]

### V.1 The template that travels

The Uttarakuru ethnography is not a free-standing invention of the northern continent. Its defining motif, a people who neither sow nor plough and eat rice that ripens of itself (`akaṭṭhapāka-sāli`, "unploughed-ripening rice"), is the golden-age rice of the Aggañña account recapitulated.[^34] The verse that describes Uttarakuru carries the identical formula the Aggañña uses for the cosmic golden age, and the commentary glosses the continent's rice with the very wording of that golden age, huskless, chaffless, fragrant. The same idyll wanders again to the forest hermitage of the Vessantara story. The rice at Uttarakuru is one deployment of a mobile canonical template, not a continent-specific datum.

Cross-recensionally that template is bedrock. The Aggañña account resolves, through the corpus's reference table, to four external witnesses, among them the Mahāvastu, a non-Pāli Vinaya, and a Chinese parallel.[^35] Four linked witnesses place the golden-age rice firmly before the sectarian split. One honesty guard applies even so. The four witnesses attest the container and its rice; whether each independently welds that rice onto a northern continent named Uttarakuru is a finer-grained question the reference table does not by itself settle. The conservative reading is that the template is shared and pre-sectarian, the four-continent frame is inherited background broadly attested in the wider literature, and the precise joining of the golden-age rice to the named continent in this verse form remains to be checked witness by witness. I do not read "the Aggañña has Āgama parallels" into "the Uttarakuru ethnography is proven ancient in every detail."

The described container travels too. The protective chant that gives Uttarakuru its concrete locale, its mountain and its rice, resolves to a Chinese parallel, so the place a reader pictures is not a Theravāda singularity.[^36] But the external witness buys antiquity, not warrant. The described place is still flat background inside a ritual chant, never staked under the verification formula, and a parallel in another recension does nothing to upgrade that epistemic force. Cross-recensional attestation buys age, not knowledge.

### V.2 The verdict that, so far, does not

The whole soteriological load of the study rests on a single sutta, the three-superiorities comparison, where the inhabitants of Uttarakuru surpass the gods of the Thirty-Three and the humans of our own continent in three respects, while our third superiority is the bare deictic that the holy life is lived *here*. This is the hinge: the most fortunate humans in the cosmos hold precisely the comforts of virtue, and the path is reserved for our own continent. In the corpus's reference table this load-bearing sutta has no non-Pāli parallel, returning only a Pāli "mentions" relation. The inference it points to is that the specific ranking, the one that turns Uttarakuru's fortune into a soteriological deficit, is local to the Pāli.[^37]

This is the finding that most needs its guard in print. "No linked parallel" is not "Theravāda-invented." The absence of a parallel row is evidence about the corpus's reference table, not affirmative evidence about the other early traditions. A line of the Āgamas could carry an analogue that was lost, never translated into the surviving recensions, or present but unlinked. There is a structural reason to weaken the inference further: the reference table pairs discourse with discourse, and the likeliest parallel for this material would not be a discourse at all. The three-distinctions schema is stock cosmological and scholastic matter, and a parallel of that kind would sit in precisely the genre this discourse-to-discourse table under-covers. The empty result therefore reflects a known blind spot in the bridge, not a positive search that came back empty. The right reading is unconfirmed, Pāli-local pending verification, never Pāli-unique and emphatically not original. Even were an analogue to surface, the decisive hardening, the identification of the Uttarakurukas with the category of those incapable of the path, is commentarial and sub-commentarial, and so has no claim to pre-sectarian standing whatever the discourse-level question resolves to.

> **Four external witnesses for the rice; none, in the linked record, for the verdict that closes the path to its eaters.**

### V.3 What the texts actually do with the place

The most frequent thing the texts do with Uttarakuru is neither rank it nor measure it, but go there, and three short scenes show what the "visited place" looks like on the page and how far it can and cannot be pressed.

The fullest of them comes from the Vinaya's account of the great chapter. A famine has settled on Verañjā, the rains-residence where the Buddha and the Sangha are staying, and food is scarce enough that the monks are reduced to eating measured handfuls of dried horse-fodder. Against that hardship the elder Moggallāna, greatest of the disciples in psychic power, comes to the Buddha with an offer: let him turn the earth over to reach the nutritive essence beneath it, or, failing that, let him lift the whole community up and carry it to Uttarakuru, where the rice ripens unsown and there would be food enough for all. The Buddha forbids both.[^38] The scene is worth dwelling on because of how it uses the continent. Uttarakuru is close enough, by psychic power, to be a serious proposal from the one disciple who could actually make the crossing, and far enough, by ordinary means, that the proposal needs that power at all. And the food it offers is exactly the effortless, unsown abundance the ethnography promises, set against a famine, and declined. The continent here is not a doctrine being asserted; it is a place a real choice runs through, and the choice keeps the soteriological irony in plain view: the land of effortless plenty is refused in favour of staying where the going is hard.

The companion scene is the bare event the famine narrative presupposes. Elsewhere in the same chapter the Buddha himself crosses the ocean by psychic power, gathers almsfood on the northern continent, carries it back, and eats it at the shore of Lake Anotatta.[^39] The continent here is scenery for an act, walked through rather than argued for.

The third scene carries the honest qualification. Asked whether a person could travel to Uttarakuru "with this very body," the elder Nāgasena answers that one could, by mastery of psychic power.[^40] It is tempting to read the very asking of the question as proof that Uttarakuru is a continent and not a heaven, since one does not ask whether you can walk to a heaven. The text does not draw that line. The same question pairs Uttarakuru with the Brahma world in one breath, and Nāgasena affirms bodily travel to both. The bodily-travel motif, then, does not by itself sort continents from realms; that work is done elsewhere, by the plain category-word for the inhabitants, who are `manussā`, "humans," the same word the texts use for us.

In sum, the furniture is borrowed and broadly attested, while the verdict built on it is, so far as the linked evidence reaches, local to the Pāli and sharpest in its commentary. If the template is inherited and the verdict is local, the remaining question is what the tradition made of the borrowed furniture, and how far the present count can be pressed. That is the last question.

## VI. What this means, and what it does not

Read on the single line that sorts texts into canon and commentary, Uttarakuru is a small canonical sketch built out by a large commentary. That sentence is true, and it hides three things that change its sense. The first is that the place a reader pictures, located, peopled, flown to, is not the commentary's gift; on the corpus's map of strata it is already present in the late layers of the canon itself, in the protective chant and the Vinaya frame-stories, before any commentary measures it. What the commentary distinctively adds is measurement: the eight-thousand-yojana breadth, the thousand-year span, the hair split eightfold, the impulse that turns a described place into a furnished one. The second is that this deepening runs on a different axis from verification. The continent grows more concrete without ever growing more warranted, because the canon keeps its strongest epistemic mark for the vertical question of rebirth-by-kamma that it actually teaches, and lets the horizontal map of continents stand as unexamined background. The third is the one genuinely distinctive Pāli move, and it is not the ethnography, which is inherited, but the use made of it. The trajectory there is not a lenient canon hardened by a harsh commentary. It is a canon that debates the question and softens it for the gods, a commentary that then identifies the Uttarakurukas with the category of those barred from the path, and a sub-commentary that re-hardens the bar against them in the same breath that the canon had used to relieve the gods.[^41]

For a reader who is not a specialist, that last move is the human point. The texts take the cosmos's most fortunate humans, the people with no possessions to quarrel over, a fixed long life, and a beauty that never ages, and make them the worst-placed of all for awakening. The reasoning, assembled across the commentarial layers rather than stated in any one place, runs that a virtue which arrives automatically with the address is a virtue never undertaken, and a virtue never undertaken cannot be cultivated into a path. The fortune is the disability. It is worth saying plainly that this syllogism is my own reconstruction of the commentary's parts: the texts supply the pieces, natural virtue, virtue fixed by rebirth, the lack of the wholesome desire-to-act, as separate glosses, and the inference that joins them into "fortune, therefore disability" is a synthesis, flagged as such and not presented as a single canonical or commentarial statement.[^42]

Several limits bound how far any of this can be pressed, and they are better stated than smoothed.

- The deepening-with-lateness gradient is partly definitional and cannot, by itself, prove that the canon's conception of the place changed; the full statement of that circularity, and the two pivotal assignments held at lower confidence, are in §III.3. The claim that materialisation begins inside the late canon is a statement relative to the corpus's stratum map, not a demonstrated chronology.
- The cross-recensional finding is link-level, not feature-level. The corpus bridges out through a reference table, not through the external texts themselves, and that table under-covers exactly the scholastic and cosmological genre where the likeliest parallel to the verdict would sit. The recension labels on the witnesses are my identification, not the table's. The split, template shared and verdict local, is the strongest reading the linked evidence allows, and would be hardened or dissolved by reading the external parallels directly.
- The character-gaps behind the silences are edition-relative; the boundaries they stand for are not. In no edition does the verification formula take the continents as its object, and in no edition is Uttarakuru a member of the inopportune-births list.
- The "fortune, therefore disability" syllogism is reconstructed, my synthesis of glosses the commentary supplies separately.
- The stratigraphy is relative order, not calendar date, resting on consensus philological grounds and importing no absolute years; the residual coding seams are reported as visible seams in a stratified object, not resolved by fiat.
- The analytic vocabulary throughout, "ethnography," "cosmology," "plane versus realm," "salt water versus space," "literal versus symbolic," is a modern frame laid over a Pāli that says its own words, and is named as such wherever it is used.

What the data cannot do is say why the shape is as it is, or whether the canon's idea of Uttarakuru truly changed or only the registers that had reason to describe it surfaced later. To ask how the Pāli texts describe the northern continent is already to ask about a picture that deepens toward the late layers while its epistemic key stays flat, and about a borrowed ethnography on which one tradition hung a judgement that, on the evidence to hand, is its own. The most one can say with confidence is the smallest thing and the steadiest: the texts grow more sure of what Uttarakuru looks like, and never once claim to have seen it. The comfort is real; the path, they say, is lived *here* (`idha brahmacariyavāso`).[^43]

## Appendix B: Method register

**The recall ladder.** Every count in this paper is a measured floor over the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana corpus as ingested. The search of the continent-name was widened in three rungs; the yields measure the surface string, not a moving census.

| Rung | Search strategy | Yield |
|---|---|---:|
| 1 | Exact phrase, the form a non-specialist would type | 15 |
| 2 | Glyph- and inflection-tolerant short-*u* substring (catches `uttarakuruṃ`, the ṁ/ṃ glyph variants, compound forms) | 144 |
| 3 | Honest stem `%uttarakur%` (catches all 47 distinct surface forms, including the long-*u* declined forms `uttarakurūnaṁ`, `uttarakurūsu` that carry the thousandfold cosmology) | 161 |

The dominant recovery is between rungs 1 and 2: the narrowest exact phrase slides off the long declined forms that an early pass had silently dropped, which is why it recovers only fifteen of the eventual hundred and sixty-one. Two neighbouring word-families were excluded by hand because they are not the continent: 976 rows of the bare Kuru country (the north-Indian `janapada`) and 26 rows of the "Kuru observances" (`kurudhamma` / `kuruvatta`). The no-warrant ethnographic features (the inopportune-births frame, the path-incapacity category, the fixed heaven-destiny) were additionally searched concept-first, without the continent-name, so the absence findings of §IV rest on concept searches and not on name-proximity alone.

**The character-gap log (§IV).** The five canonical rows that carry both the divine-eye formula and the continent-name have in-row gaps of 4,909 / 5,101 / 43,133 / 45,573 / 227,578 characters between marker and name, computed over the CST monolithic rows as ingested. The `akkhaṇa`-plus-name co-occurrences run 930 to 126,000 characters; the path-incapacity category sits about 7,200 characters from the name; the heaven-destiny co-occurrences (5 rows) have a smallest gap of 4,302 characters. The numbers are edition-relative; the structural fact they stand for, that no window places the verification formula over the continent, is not.

**The five silences (§IV.2), in descending strength.**

| Expected frame | What it would show | Result for Uttarakuru | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| The divine-eye pericope | rebirth-by-kamma seen directly | object never the four continents; positive contrast class | strongest |
| The eight inopportune births | the holy life cannot be led here | continent not a member of the finite list | strong |
| The path-incapacity category (`acchandika` / `abhabba`) | who cannot enter the path | category canonical, identification commentarial | medium |
| Fixed heaven-destiny | a settled rebirth-bound | unstated in canon, supplied by commentary | medium |
| Rare-human-birth / Pure-Abodes | the continent placed in a rebirth-frame | pure name-absence, no contrast class | thinnest |

**The triage note.** This is a category-typology question (each row has a stratum, a layer, a speaker, an epistemic status, and a recension status), so those dimensions were coded on every row, with the chronological stratum coded from work and position independently of the structural layer. The recall ladder ran throughout as the standing recall discipline. The dominant move is the separation of structural layer from chronological stratum, which is what surfaces the twenty disagreement rows.

**Pre-registration.** The hypotheses, the null, and the prediction were frozen before coding (see the uttarakuru-study pre-registration). The working hypothesis was that the literal-place reading deepens with lateness, the deepening beginning inside the late canon, while the epistemic stance stays flat; the null was the earlier single-contrast picture, a thin canon built out by commentary with the dividing line at the canon-to-commentary boundary. The shape found matches the working hypothesis on the within-canon gradient and on the flat epistemic key, with the gradient qualified by the register-circularity caveat of §III.3 (it is partly definitional and cannot, alone, prove a change in the canon's conception).

## Appendix C: Inter-annotator agreement

The signature axes were coded blind by independent readers and the codings reconciled. The feature schema carries sixteen features in all; agreement on the load-bearing axes was high.

- **Chronological stratum:** unanimous on all but two of the sixteen features.
- **Speaker / attribution:** unanimous on all but three.
- **Epistemic marking:** unanimous on all but one.
- **Hypothesis verdict under chronology:** unanimous on all but three.

The two stratum disagreements are informative seams rather than coding error. The cleanest is the self-ripening rice: two readers fixed the row's stratum to its early template (the Aggañña golden-age container), one to the late protective chant that pins the template to the named continent; both are correct on their chosen referent, and the paper reports the template as early and the placement as late rather than averaging them. The attribution split, on the wheel-turner's conquest frame, turns on whether to weight the early canonical frame or the commentarial naming; it is coded split. The residual disagreements are reported where they fall, not averaged away; the full per-feature matrix is retained with the dataset.
