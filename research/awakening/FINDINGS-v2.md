# The Awakening Census Under the Provenance Signature: A Canonical Corpus That Is Late and Mostly Narrated

## Abstract

An earlier pass enumerated every awakening event in the live corpus and reported two headline facts: awakening is overwhelmingly an occasioned event rather than the climax of solitary striving, and the narrated awakenings live mostly in the commentaries, not the canon. Of 2,214 events, 299 sit in the canon (mula) and 1,915 in the commentarial and extra-canonical layers. That reading is true on its single axis, the structural canon-versus-commentary split, and it conceals a sharper fact that the structural axis cannot see, because the mula tag is a position in the edited corpus and not a date. Recoded under the provenance-signature method, which separates structural layer from chronological stratum and from attribution, the canonical share stops being an early Buddha-word floor. Of the 299 structurally-mula awakening events only 38 are early-canonical (Nikaya prose and Udana); the other 261 carry the mula tag while coding late-canonical, paracanonical, or commentary-era on the chronological axis. The single largest canonical class is the Apadana (162 events, `tha-ap25`, `cst-s0510m1.mul-kn10_1`) [late-canon], a recognised late-canonical autobiographical-verse stratum; the Buddhavamsa (8, `bv10`) [late-canon] and the Niddesa (3, `cnd5`) [late-canon] are late-canonical too; the Visuddhimagga (17, `cst-e0102n.mul-4_p042`) [comm] is structurally tagged mula yet is classical commentary; the Milindapanha (4, `mil3.7.3`) [late-canon] is paracanonical. On attribution the corpus is overwhelmingly narrated about the awakened rather than asserted by the awakener: 282 of the 299 are redactor-narrated sutta-frame attainments, hagiographic first-person verse (the Apadana speaker recounting his own awakening), or the elder's own Theragatha report with a redactor's colophon. But it is not, as an earlier coding pass wrongly recorded, devoid of Buddha-vacana. Coded per claim rather than by work-class, 17 of the 299 rows, collapsing to 9 distinct deduplicated recollections, are the Buddha asserting his OWN awakening in the first person: the bodhisatta-before-awakening frame (`pubbeva me, bhikkhave, sambodhā anabhisambuddhassa bodhisattasseva sato`), the claim-of-awakening (`anuttaraṁ sammāsambodhiṁ abhisambuddhoti paccaññāsiṁ`), and the bodhi-night autobiography spoken to a named interlocutor (`ayaṁ kho me, brāhmaṇa, rattiyā pacchime yāme tatiyā vijjā adhigatā`), in MN 4, MN 19, MN 36, MN 85, MN 100, AN 8.11, SN 54.8, SN 35.13, and the Vinaya bodhi-narrative [canon, IAA kappa=1.0]. A named search refutes any reading that the Buddha asserts only his own awakening: he also declares others' attainments, the post-mortem destiny declarations of the Nadika mirror-of-Dhamma and the stream-entry formulae appearing across 29 mula rows. The corrected headline is that the canonical awakening corpus is, in the main, a late-canonical and hagiographically-narrated stratum, so the original canon-versus-commentary finding is not overturned but deepened: the contrast it drew is, in part, an early-to-late gradient inside the canon. On attribution the Buddha is both the first-person asserter of his own bodhi and the authoritative declarer of others' awakenings, even as most individual rows are the redactor's or the elder's narration.

## The corrected headline

The most important correction this study makes is not a count but a coordinate change. The earlier reading scored each event on a single structural axis, mula versus the commentarial layers, and concluded that the canon states the Dhamma while the commentaries supply the biography. That axis is preserved here as one row of a fuller signature. What it could not see is that the canon's own share is itself stratified, and that the awakening-event genre is concentrated in the canon's late registers, not its early ones.

The corrected headline is this. The genuinely early-canonical awakening events are a small floor of 38 rows: the Nikaya prose suttas where a listener's eye of Dhamma opens at the conclusion of a graduated talk (`dn5`, `mn74`) [canon], and the Udana frame-narratives (`ud1.10`, Bahiya) [canon]. Above that floor sit 261 mula rows that the structural tag calls canonical and the chronological axis re-codes as late. The largest single class, by a wide margin, is the Apadana, the autobiographical past-merit-to-present-awakening verse of named elders, 162 of the 299 canonical events [late-canon]. The Buddhavamsa's mass-awakenings at the feet of past Buddhas are late-canonical lineage verse (`bv10`) [late-canon]. The Niddesa's awakenings sit in a canonical exegesis of the Sutta-nipata, itself late (`cnd5`) [late-canon]. The Visuddhimagga rows are structurally mula in this corpus and are in fact Buddhaghosa's fifth-century scholastic prose (`cst-e0102n.mul-4_p042`) [comm]. The Milindapanha is outside the Tipitaka proper (`mil3.7.3`) [late-canon]. The early Buddha-word floor is real, but it is thin, and the bulk of what the structural axis filed under canon is a late and a narrated corpus.

Three things follow that the single axis could not produce. First, the canon-versus-commentary contrast the earlier reading drew is, in part, a chronology gradient running through the canon: the awakening-event genre thins toward the early registers and thickens toward the late and the commentarial ones. Second, the canonical awakening corpus is mostly narrated about the awakened, by a redactor or by the awakened elder, rather than asserted by the Buddha as his own claim, but it is not without Buddha-vacana: 17 of the 299 rows, 9 distinct recollections once the duplicate edition-rows are collapsed, are the Buddha asserting his own awakening in the first person, and the same authoritative voice elsewhere declares others' attainments. The earlier coding that found zero Buddha-vacana was a per-work-class artifact, corrected here by per-claim coding. Third, the original finding stands and is strengthened: not only do the awakenings live mostly in the commentaries, but even the canon's share is mostly late and hagiographic, so the early-Buddha-word reading the structural tag invites is an overcount.

## Methodology

### The provenance signature and the triage

The method replaces the single canon-versus-commentary axis with a provenance signature, a small triaged set of independent dimensions. The full framework carries eleven axes; the triage maps a research question's load-bearing claims to the axes they actually depend on, and coding every axis on every instance would bury the study.

This census is a category-typology question: it enumerates awakening events, whose members are asserted occurrences with a who, a when, and a voice. For a category typology the framework makes the recall ladder, chronological stratum, and attribution load-bearing. The three coded axes are therefore the structural layer (retained as the default-core signal, demoted to one row of the signature), the chronological stratum (coded independently of that layer), and the attribution or speaker of the attainment-claim. The recall ladder is run on the attainment markers as the always-on recall discipline.

The axes deliberately not coded, and why, are these. Epistemic marking is excluded because the census records whether an awakening is narrated, not whether it is staked under the canon's verification formula; the load here is on when and by whose voice, which stratum and attribution carry, and an awakening narrated as an event is not the kind of claim the divine-eye formula ranges over. Cross-recensional reach is coded only at link level as a secondary corroboration of the early floor, because the corpus is Pali-only and the Apadana's lateness is settled philologically rather than by a parallel table. Pre-Buddhist provenance is moot, since an awakening event is a Buddhist datum by definition. Harmonization is excluded because no doctrinal tension is being reconciled; the census is descriptive. Reception and edition fire automatically and are reported inside the recall ladder, where the niggahita-glyph cliff is an edition-and-segmentation fact.

### The recall ladder

Every count rests on a measured enumeration, not an assumed one, and the ladder on the attainment markers is itself a finding. The naive surface string a non-specialist would type for the commonest marker, `arah` plus the bar-niggahita glyph (`arahattaṁ pāpuṇi`), returns 2 rows in the whole corpus (rung 1). The reason is an edition fact: the Chattha Sangayana base writes the niggahita as the dotted-m glyph (`ṃ`), so the bar-m surface string finds almost nothing. Normalising the glyph and admitting the declined and sandhi forms of the arahatta-attainment family (the dotted-m, with `pāpuṇi / patto / pattā / āpādi`) lifts the same family to 914 rows (rung 2). A concept-independent union that searches the awakening-event frame without the word arahatta (the mass-awakening formula `desanāpariyosāne`, the verse-conclusion `gāthāpariyosāne`, the liberation-without-clinging `anupādāya āsavehi cittaṃ vimucci`, the stream-entry `virajaṃ vītamalaṃ dhammacakkhuṃ udapādi`, the destroyed-birth `khīṇā jāti`, the declared-final-knowledge `aññaṃ byākāsi`) reaches 1,488 marker-bearing rows (rung 3). Removing the doctrinal and abstract uses of those markers, the ones that do not narrate an event, leaves the census's net 2,214 events (rung 4).

Every load-bearing count below is reconfirmed by a `GROUP BY work_role`, not by the search lane, which over- and under-matches. The regression check is exact: the `GROUP BY work_role` returns mula 299, attha 1,762, tika 118, anya 35, matching the census layer counts to the row.

### The work-to-stratum map

Chronological stratum is coded from work plus position, never from the structural layer, which is the conflation the axis breaks. Each mula event resolves to exactly one work by a rule over its corpus id and `work_slug`, captured serially from the database. The map follows the consensus relative stratigraphy of Pali philology. AN, SN, MN, DN sutta prose and the Udana are early-canonical. The Apadana, the Buddhavamsa, the Maha- and Cula-niddesa, the Vimanavatthu, and the Vinaya frame-narratives (nidana) are late-canonical. The Milindapanha is paracanonical. The Visuddhimagga, structurally tagged mula in this corpus, is classical commentary on stratum, and is flagged where layer and stratum disagree.

Two stratum assignments are coded contested-confidence rather than secure, because they convert a register or position label into a date. The Theragatha and Therigatha (59 events) carry archaic verse, yet the awakening-narration in these rows sits in the redactor-compiled biographical frame attached to the verse, so they are coded `archaic-or-late-canonical` and treated as register-relative rather than securely dated. The Vinaya frame-narratives (6 events) are coded late by their nidana position, a position label and not a secured date. These two are flagged so that no genre or position cue is silently doing duty as a chronology.

### Coding and reliability

Chronological stratum is a deterministic function of the work, reproducible from the resolved id-to-work manifest, and the builder recomputes every count from the data. Attribution is coded per CLAIM, not per work-class. The load-bearing Buddha-vacana category is recall-anchored by a discriminator of strings only ever spoken by the Buddha of his own bodhi (the bodhisatta-before-awakening frame, the `paccaññāsiṁ` claim-of-awakening, the bodhi-night autobiography to a named interlocutor), run over all 299 rows, then each candidate read directly around its attainment marker and coded by three independent blind coders working from the Pali alone. On the 17 Buddha-vacana rows the three coders agreed unanimously (kappa=1.0 on the load-bearing category). A corpus-wide quotative scan corroborates the genre mix: of the 299, 206 carry first-person self-narration markers, 22 carry `bhagavā etadavoca`, and 48 carry the redactor's `evaṃ me sutaṃ` or `tena samayena` frame [canon, SQL-confirmed]. The non-Buddha-vacana classes (the Apadana elder self-narration, the Theragatha and Therigatha disciple self-report, the Buddhavamsa redactor-verse) were spot-read and are class-homogeneous. Where the corpus carries no English, the rendering is the present author's gloss checked against the standard translations, with load-bearing terms quoted in Pali. The base edition is the Chattha Sangayana (CST/VRI) as ingested; counts and the glyph cliff are relative to that segmentation.

### The within-canon chronological gradient

The awakening-event genre is not spread evenly across the canon's strata; it pools in the late ones. The early-canonical floor is the Nikaya prose where a teaching occasions a listener's stream-entry or arahantship, and the terse Udana. That floor is genuine and it is small. The late-canonical bulk is the autobiographical and lineage verse. The Apadana alone, 162 events, is larger than the entire early floor several times over, and it is a stratum the field places late: the past-life-merit-to-present-awakening biography is exactly the kind of hagiographic elaboration that gathers around the bare canonical fact of an awakening. The Buddhavamsa's serial mass-awakenings at past Buddhas' feet are the same impulse one register out. The Visuddhimagga rows, tagged mula by the corpus's structural column, are fifth-century commentary by any chronology.

One honesty guard bounds this. The gradient is relative stratigraphy on consensus philological grounds (Apadana, Buddhavamsa, and Niddesa membership as the late-canonical warrants; Visuddhimagga authorship as the commentary warrant), and it imports no absolute dates. Two assignments are coded contested-confidence because a register or position label is being read as a date: the Theragatha and Therigatha awakening-narration, late by its biographical-frame register rather than by the age of its verse, and the Vinaya alms-flight awakenings, late by their nidana position. Where these are read as merely register-relative, the early floor would grow at most by the Theragatha and Therigatha share; even then the Apadana alone keeps the late bulk decisively larger than the early floor, so the gradient is robust to the contested cells.

The cross-recensional check corroborates the early floor at link level. Several of the 33 early-canonical Nikaya-prose awakening events carry external (non-Pali) parallels in the corpus parallel table (Chinese and Sanskrit), a link-level proxy for a pre-sectarian core of the bare awakening narration; the Apadana and Buddhavamsa carry some external parallels at work level too, but the genre's lateness is a philological fact, not a parallel-table absence, and the container-versus-feature distinction is kept: a work-level parallel does not make the autobiographical-verse feature early. This axis is reported as corroboration, not as a load-bearing finding.

## The attribution axis: a corpus mostly narrated about the awakened, with a real Buddha-vacana floor

The second retrofit finding is on attribution, the illocutionary owner of the attainment-claim. The structural layer encodes none of this, and the binary buddha-voice tag launders every canonical row into the Buddha's voice. The honest default the framework sets is the reverse: a flat narrated awakening with no quotative owner is a redactor-frame claim, not buddha-vacana, and an explicit quotative warrant is required before coding buddha-vacana. An earlier pass applied that default by work-class, assigning every nikaya-prose and vinaya-nidana row to redactor-frame wholesale, and so recorded zero buddha-vacana across the 299. That zero was a coding artifact. Coded per claim, the corpus is overwhelmingly narrated about the awakened but carries a small, real Buddha-vacana floor.

Most of the 299 fall into four narrating voices that are not the Buddha asserting his own awakening.

**Hagiographic self-narration (162, the largest).** The Apadana awakenings are first-person verse in which the named elder recounts his own path from a past good deed to his present arahantship. The Tisaranagamaniya elder begins "In the city of Candavati I was a supporter of my mother... I attained" (`tha-ap25`: *Nagare candavatiya, matuupatthako ahum... arahattamapapunim*) [late-canon]. The owner of the attainment-claim is the awakened elder, and the genre is the redactor's hagiographic compilation; it is not the Buddha staking the event.

**Named-disciple self-report (59).** The Theragatha and Therigatha awakenings are the elder's own verse, "having heard his Dhamma I dwelt nearby, mindful; the three knowledges are attained" (`thag1.66`) [canon, contested-stratum], closed by the redactor's colophon naming the elder. The owner is the disciple reporting his own attainment, framed by the redactor.

**Redactor-frame (46).** Most Nikaya-prose, Udana, Niddesa, Vinaya-nidana, and Visuddhimagga awakenings are flat narrated events: the redactor tells us a listener's mind was freed, with no first-person claim and no Buddha-speech frame asserting the attainment (`ud1.10`, `dn5` where the brahmin Kutadanta's dust-free dhamma-eye arises, `cst-e0102n.mul-4_p042`) [canon, comm].

**Redactor-frame verse (10) and named-disciple dialogue (4).** The Buddhavamsa narrates the mass awakenings at the feet of past Buddhas in lineage verse (`bv10`) [late-canon], joined by the Vimanavatthu; the Milindapanha's 4 are Nagasena's paracanonical dialogue.

But 17 of the 299 are the Buddha asserting his OWN awakening in the first person, and this is the correction the per-claim coding makes. In MN 4 the Buddha tells the brahmin Janussoni "this, brahmin, was the third true knowledge attained by me in the last watch of the night" (`mn4`: *ayaṁ kho me, brāhmaṇa, rattiyā pacchime yāme tatiyā vijjā adhigatā*) [canon]. The same first-person bodhi-night autobiography, spoken to a named hearer, is in MN 19, MN 36 (to Aggivessana), MN 85 (to Prince Bodhi), MN 100 (to Bharadvaja), and AN 8.11 (to the brahmin Veranja, the passage the Vinaya repeats verbatim as its Parajika nidana, `pli-tv-bu-vb-pj1`). SN 35.13 carries the bodhisatta-before-awakening reflection closing in "I claimed to be awakened to unsurpassed perfect awakening" (`cst-s0304m.mul-sn4_1`: *anuttaraṁ sammāsambodhiṁ abhisambuddhoti paccaññāsiṁ*); SN 54.8 the same in the breath-meditation register (`sn54.8`: *anupādāya ca me āsavehi cittaṁ vimucci*); the Vinaya Mahavagga bodhi-narrative repeats the `paccaññāsiṁ` claim from the Dhammacakka (`cst-vin02m2.mul-vin3_1`). These 17 rows collapse to 9 distinct recollections once the CST volume-rows that duplicate the same sutta are deduplicated, and three independent blind coders agreed on every one (kappa=1.0).

A named search settles the relational question the zero invited: does the Buddha assert only his own awakening, never another's? He does not confine himself to his own. The same authoritative voice declares others' attainments: the post-mortem destiny declarations of the Nadika mirror-of-Dhamma (the non-returner who attains nibbana there, the once-returner, the stream-enterer) and the stream-entry declarations appear across 29 mula rows, and the declared-final-knowledge formula `aññaṁ byākāsi` across 10. Within the 299 the Buddha declares a hearer's stream-entry to Kaligodha (`sn55.39`, coded `buddha-declares-another`). The Buddha is therefore both the first-person asserter of his own bodhi and the canonical declarer of others' awakenings; "the canon never puts the awakening in the Buddha's mouth" is false on both halves.

## Discussion

The corrected headline is sharper than its predecessor, and the sharpening comes from refusing to let the structural layer stand for the whole provenance question. On the structural axis alone, the awakening census reads as a canon that states the Dhamma and a commentary that supplies the biography, with 299 of 2,214 events in the canon. That sentence is true, and it conceals two facts that change its meaning.

First, the canon's own 299 are not an early Buddha-word floor. Only 38 are early-canonical; 261 carry the mula tag while coding late-canonical, paracanonical, or commentary-era, and the largest single class, the Apadana, is late. The canon-versus-commentary contrast the earlier reading drew is therefore, in part, a chronology gradient running through the canon: the awakening-event genre pools in the late and the narrative registers, the autobiographical and the lineage verse, and thins in the early doctrinal prose. The materialisation the structural axis credited to the commentary already begins inside the late canon, in the Apadana and the Buddhavamsa, before any commentary speaks. The qualification is that two of the contested cells (the Theragatha and Therigatha register, the Vinaya nidana position) are register-relative rather than securely dated, and they are coded as such; the gradient survives their removal because the Apadana alone keeps the late bulk decisively larger than the early floor.

Second, the deepening runs on a different axis from authorship. On that axis the canonical awakening corpus is mostly, but not entirely, narrated about the awakened rather than asserted by the awakener. The bulk is the redactor's frame or the elder's own hagiographic verse; coded per claim, 17 of the 299 rows (9 distinct recollections) are the Buddha asserting his own awakening in the first person, and the Buddha elsewhere declares others' attainments. This nuance is invisible to the binary buddha-voice tag, which counts every canonical row as the Buddha's voice, and it was also invisible to the first retrofit pass, which coded attribution by work-class and so recorded a false zero. The corrected reading is that the grammatical owner of most rows is the redactor or the disciple, that a small first-person Buddha-vacana floor is real, and that an early-Buddha-word reading of the whole canonical share remains an overcount on both the chronological and the attribution axes.

The methodological lesson matches the one the Uttarakuru retrofit drew on different material. Canon versus commentary is one projection of a richer object, and for any question whose load rests on when a formulation entered (chronological stratum) or in whose mouth a claim is placed (attribution), the single projection misleads in a predictable direction. It will read a late and narrated canonical stratum as an early authored one. The awakening census is a clean case: the structural tag says canon, and the signature says late and narrated.

## Limitations

The study carries five honest limitations, each stated rather than smoothed.

The chronological stratigraphy is relative, not absolute. It rests on consensus Pali philological grounds (Apadana, Buddhavamsa, and Niddesa membership as the late-canonical warrants, Visuddhimagga authorship as the commentary warrant) and imports no absolute dates. Two assignments are coded contested-confidence because a register or position label is being read as a date: the Theragatha and Therigatha awakening-narration, late by its biographical-frame register rather than by the age of its verse, and the Vinaya alms-flight awakenings, late by their nidana position. The early floor would grow at most by the Theragatha and Therigatha share if those cells were read as early; even then the Apadana keeps the late bulk decisively larger, so the gradient is robust to the contested cells.

The census is marker-bounded, and the recall ladder measures rather than eliminates that bound. An awakening narrated with no stock attainment phrase is not captured. The ladder reports the floor (the rung-1 glyph cliff, the rung-3 concept union) and the residual risk between the last rung and true totality is visible rather than assumed; the count is claimed as saturated and measured, not as proven-complete.

The attribution coding is bounded by the original census's recall, not by the canon's full extent. The Buddha-vacana category is coded per individual row, recall-anchored and confirmed by three blind coders, so the count of 17 within the 299 is firm. But the 299 are the rows the first census enumerated, and that census under-captured the Buddha's own-awakening corpus: the bodhisatta-before-awakening first-person frame appears in 48 mula rows corpus-wide, including the classic Ariyapariyesana (MN 26), MN 14, MN 128, and many SN and AN rows that never entered the 299. The within-census figure (17 rows, 9 distinct recollections) is therefore a floor, reported as a measured recall observation rather than a re-enumeration; the regression gate holds the 299 fixed so that this attribution correction is not confounded with a re-census. The non-Buddha-vacana classes (Apadana self-narration, Theragatha and Therigatha disciple self-report, Buddhavamsa redactor-verse) are coded at class level after spot-reading, and are class-homogeneous.

The cross-recensional check is link-level, not feature-level. The corpus bridges out through a parallel table, and the external parallels for the early-canonical floor are evidence about the link table, a proxy for a pre-sectarian core, not an affirmative feature-level reading of the Agama awakening-narrations. This axis is reported as corroboration of the early floor, never as a load-bearing finding.

The base edition and the glyph cliff are edition-relative. The recall ladder's rung-1 figure is a fact about the Chattha Sangayana segmentation and its dotted-m niggahita glyph; under a different edition or input convention the surface-string yield would change. What does not change is the structural fact the cliff stands for, that the attainment markers are densely attested once the glyph is normalised, and that the attribution boundary (zero Buddha-vacana) is edition-neutral.
