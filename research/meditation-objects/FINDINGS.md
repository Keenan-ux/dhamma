# Classes of Individuals, Object-Matching, and the "40 Kammaṭṭhāna" — Findings

A study run against the live Dhamma corpus (serial, gentle) plus web scholarship, answering three
questions: did the Buddha explain how to **identify** the classes of individuals (neyya etc.); did he
explain how to **determine** the meditation object that suits a person; and are the **40 meditation
objects** of the commentaries his own enumeration — and if not, what did he actually give?

Confidence tags as in the intoxicants study: **[canon]** verified in-corpus; **[abhi]** Abhidhamma
(Puggalapaññatti, in-corpus, Pāli-only so English is the agent's gloss); **[comm]** commentarial;
**[scholarship]** external/attributed; **[extension]** our synthesis.

---

## 0. The short version

1. **Identifying the classes: only partly, and not by a test you could apply.** The four persons-by-
   understanding are **named** in a sutta (AN 4.133) with no definitions; the working **definitions**
   live in the **Abhidhamma** (Puggalapaññatti); and actual identification is the **Buddha's own
   perception** (the "Buddha-eye" survey, SN 6.1 / MN 26), not a rubric a teacher or student can use.
2. **Choosing the object: yes in principle, no as a system.** The Buddha repeatedly matches an
   **object to a specific defilement** (foulness for lust, love for ill-will, breath for scattered
   thought, impermanence for the "I am" conceit). But the systematic **six-temperament (carita)**
   selection scheme is **Buddhaghosa's** — the word *carita* never appears in the suttas in that sense.
3. **The "40" is not the Buddha's.** The closed list of exactly forty *kammaṭṭhāna* (and the term
   *kammaṭṭhāna* itself in its meditation sense) is the **Visuddhimagga's** synthesis. The Buddha gave
   **many** discrete objects, several already in closed sub-lists — but never a closed forty, and his
   lists are not even identical to Buddhaghosa's.

## 1. Did the Buddha explain how to identify the classes of individuals?

**The four types are named in one sutta, and only named.** AN 4.133 (Ugghaṭitaññūsutta) gives the bare
list and nothing more **[canon, verified verbatim]**:

> *"Cattārome, bhikkhave, puggalā santo saṁvijjamānā lokasmiṁ. … Ugghaṭitaññū, vipañcitaññū, neyyo,
> padaparamo."* — "These four people are found in the world: one who understands immediately, one who
> understands after detailed explanation, **one who needs to be guided** (*neyya*), and one who merely
> learns the words by rote (*padaparama*)."

**The definitions are Abhidhamma, not Sutta.** The operative definitions are in the Puggalapaññatti
(Catukka §§148–151), each keyed to *how* the breakthrough to the Dhamma (*dhammābhisamaya*) is reached
**[abhi, verified; id `cst-abh03m2.mul-014`]**:
- *ugghaṭitaññū* — the moment it is stated (*saha udāhaṭavelāya*);
- *vipañcitaññū* — when a brief saying is analysed in detail (*vitthārena atthe vibhajiyamāne*);
- *neyya* — **only gradually** (*anupubbena*), through recitation, questioning, wise attention, and
  **attending on good friends** (*uddesato paripucchato yoniso manasikaroto kalyāṇamitte sevato*);
- *padaparama* — for whom, however much is heard, recited, memorised, or taught, there is no
  breakthrough **in that very life** (*na tāya jātiyā dhammābhisamayo*).

**Identification itself is the Buddha's perceptual prerogative, not a stated method.** These
definitions are *retrospective markers* (who in fact penetrates, and under what conditions), not a
diagnostic anyone can apply in advance. The only "telling apart" the canon describes is the **Buddha's
survey of the world** with the Buddha-eye in SN 6.1 (= MN 26, Brahmā's request) **[canon, verified]**:

> *"…bhagavā buddhacakkhunā lokaṁ volokento addasa satte apparajakkhe mahārajakkhe tikkhindriye
> mudindriye … suviññāpaye duviññāpaye…"* — beings "with little dust" or "much dust in their eyes,"
> with keen or weak faculties, easy or hard to teach — illustrated by the lotuses at three depths.

It is the **commentary** (Sāratthappakāsinī on SN 6.1; Papañcasūdanī on MN 26) that explicitly maps the
lotus-stages onto the four understanding-types and says the Buddha "saw all the modes"
(*sabbākāratova addasa*) **[comm]**. So even the link between the famous lotus image and the four-type
scheme is commentarial. *(Note the arithmetic is uneven — three lotus depths, four person-types — which
the commentary smooths over; flagged as a soft spot.)*

**A second, distinct typology by teachability.** AN 3.22 (Gilānasutta) classes hearers by analogy to
three patients — one recovers regardless, one never recovers, one recovers **only with care** — and
says Dhamma-teaching is "prescribed" (*anuññātā*) chiefly for that contingent third **[canon, verified]**.
This is a three-fold scheme by outcome, separate from the four understanding-types.

**Bottom line.** The Buddha **named** the classes and the conditions that lead each, but gave **no
applicable identification criterion**. Identification is, in effect, a function of the *indriya-
paropariya-ñāṇa* — the knowledge of others' faculties that the canon lists among the **powers of a
Tathāgata** (MN 12) — i.e. a Buddha's faculty, not a teacher's checklist. **[extension, flagged]**

## 2. Did the Buddha explain how to determine the object that suits an individual?

**Yes — as object-to-defilement antidote pairing.** The clearest case is the Meghiya discourse
(AN 9.3 = Ud 4.1): after Meghiya is harassed by sensual, malicious, and cruel thoughts, the Buddha
prescribes four things "to be developed further" **[canon, verified verbatim]**:

> *"Asubhā bhāvetabbā rāgassa pahānāya, mettā bhāvetabbā byāpādassa pahānāya, ānāpānassati bhāvetabbā
> vitakkupacchedāya, aniccasaññā bhāvetabbā asmimānasamugghātāya."* — **foulness** to give up **lust**,
> **love** to give up **ill-will**, **breath-mindfulness** to cut off **scattered thinking**,
> **perception of impermanence** to uproot the **conceit "I am."**

The Mahārāhulovāda (MN 62) spells the same antidote logic out object by object for Rāhula — foulness vs
lust, love vs ill-will, compassion vs cruelty, joy vs discontent, equanimity vs aversion, impermanence
vs the "I am" conceit — plus the elements and a full sixteen-step breath practice **[canon, verified;
the per-object pairings were confirmed against the live MN 62]**. The Girimānanda Sutta (AN 10.60)
shows person-fitting of a different kind: **ten perceptions** taught (via Ānanda) to a gravely ill monk,
who recovers on hearing them — an object-set matched to the person's *situation* **[canon, verified]**.

**But the systematic temperament scheme is commentarial.** The Visuddhimagga's matrix — six *caritas*
(greedy / hating / deluded / faithful / intelligent / speculative) each matched to objects (the ten
foulnesses + body-mindfulness for the greedy; the four divine abidings + four colour-kasiṇas for the
hating; breath for the deluded and the speculative; the six recollections for the faithful; death,
peace, food-repulsiveness, and element-analysis for the intelligent; the rest suitable for all) — is
**Buddhaghosa's** (Vism Ch. III) **[scholarship]**. The decisive canonical check: the word **`carita`
in the suttas is never the temperament term** — every mūla hit is the verb *caritā* ("having practised/
wandered," from *carati*: MN 12, AN 4.150, Vin Pvr, etc.) **[canon, verified negative]**. So the
Buddha supplies the **antidote principle**; the commentary builds the **diagnostic typology** on top.

## 3. Are the 40 meditation objects the Buddha's enumeration? (And the real count.)

**No — the "40 *kammaṭṭhāna*" are Buddhaghosa's synthesis.** Two independent lines of evidence:

- **Lexical [canon, verified — see caveat].** An exact search for *kammaṭṭhāna* in the original Pāli
  returns hits **only from commentary** and the Visuddhimagga itself; restricted to the canonical layer
  (*mula*) it yields just two hits, both Visuddhimagga, and **zero Tipiṭaka** rows. The technical term
  and the number forty are **absent from the canon**. (Web scholarship confirms: in the canon
  *kammaṭṭhāna* means secular "work/occupation"; its meditation sense is commentarial.)
- **Structural [scholarship].** The closed seven-category list (10 kasiṇa, 10 asubha, 10 *anussati*,
  4 *brahmavihāra*, 4 *āruppa*, 1 food-repulsiveness, 1 four-element-analysis = 40) is codified in the
  Visuddhimagga (5th c. CE), with Upatissa's earlier **Vimuttimagga** as a near-twin precursor that
  lists **thirty-eight**, not forty — so even the commentarial number is unstable.

**What the Buddha actually gave is many discrete objects, several already in closed sub-lists**
**[canon, verified by direct fetch]**:
- the **ten kasiṇa-bases** (AN 10.25; DN 33/34) — *but ending in space + **consciousness***
  (*ākāsa, viññāṇa*), where the Visuddhimagga **drops consciousness and substitutes light**
  (*āloka*) to keep ten. **The canonical kasiṇa list is not identical to Buddhaghosa's.**
- the **six recollections** (AN 6.10: Buddha, Dhamma, Saṅgha, virtue, generosity, deities), plus
  mindfulness of death (AN 6.19) and of the body / 32 parts (MN 119; DN 22);
- the **four divine abidings** and **four formless bases** (DN 33);
- the **satipaṭṭhāna** body-objects — breath, postures, 32 parts, four elements, the **nine** charnel-
  ground stages (DN 22 / MN 10) — which the Visuddhimagga reorganises into **ten** *asubha*;
- the **ten perceptions** of Girimānanda (AN 10.60), and other *saññā* (AN 7.49 etc.).

**The honest count.** There is no closed canonical number. Of the nominal forty, about **31 underlying
practices are individually attested** in the Nikāyas; the remaining "40-ness" — the *light* kasiṇa
substitution, the **9→10** asubha recount, food-repulsiveness and four-element-analysis as *discrete
numbered subjects*, and the *upasamānussati* label — is Buddhaghosa's framing, not the Buddha's. And
the Buddha gave **more** objects than the forty (the insight *saññā*, the non-body satipaṭṭhāna
foundations, postures, clear comprehension), which the samatha-oriented forty simply doesn't list. So:
**he gave the practices; the commentary gave the catalogue, the number, and the diagnostic.**
